Delicious Apple Fritter Waffle Donuts for Fall Mornings

Lummy Hayes Published on January 11, 2026
20 min prep 15 min cook 6 servings

I started making these when my waffle iron was collecting dust and I needed something more exciting than plain waffles for weekend breakfast. Turns out, waffle makers are perfect for creating donut-shaped treats that get crispy on the outside while staying soft inside. The apples add bursts of tartness that cut through the sweet glaze, and the whole thing comes together faster than driving to the donut shop. These hit differently on October mornings when the air gets cold and you want something that feels both indulgent and homemade. What makes this different from regular apple fritters is the waffle iron—it creates those signature crispy ridges that trap extra glaze in all the right spots.

Tips for Best Results

Dice your apples small. Chunks bigger than a pea won't cook through properly and create raw spots in your donuts. I aim for about 1/4-inch pieces. Also, toss them with a tiny bit of the flour mixture before folding them in—it keeps them from sinking to the bottom of the batter. Don't overfill the waffle iron. The first time I made these, I got greedy with the batter and ended up with overflow that burned onto the sides of my waffle maker. Start with less than you think you need—you can always add more if there are gaps. Let the batter rest briefly. After mixing, give it a 5-minute rest while your waffle iron finishes heating. The baking powder activates better this way, and you get fluffier results. Test your first one. Waffle makers vary wildly in how they heat. My mom's takes 5 minutes per waffle while mine is done in 3. Cook one as a test, adjust the timing, then proceed with the rest of the batch. Glaze while they're warm. Cold donuts won't absorb the glaze properly—it just sits on top in a weird film. But if they're too hot, the glaze melts off completely. That 2-3 minute cooling window is the sweet spot. Use a light hand when mixing. Seriously, stop stirring the second you don't see dry flour anymore. Overworked batter = tough, chewy donuts that taste more like hockey pucks than breakfast treats.

Variations & Substitutions

Add chopped pecans or walnuts. Toast them first for about 5 minutes in a dry pan, let them cool, then fold about 1/3 cup into the batter along with the apples. The crunch factor goes way up. Make them pumpkin spice style. Replace half the milk with pumpkin puree and swap the nutmeg for pumpkin pie spice. Keep the apples—they work surprisingly well with pumpkin flavor. Try a maple glaze instead. Use real maple syrup in place of the milk in the glaze, cutting the powdered sugar down to 3/4 cup. It tastes way more fall-appropriate than vanilla. Go savory with cheddar and apple. Skip the sugar in the batter, add 1/3 cup shredded sharp cheddar, and ditch the sweet glaze entirely. Serve them with bacon and eggs for a breakfast that'll confuse and delight people. Make mini versions. If you have a mini waffle maker, cut the batter amount per waffle in half and reduce cooking time to about 2 minutes. You'll get more pieces that are easier for kids to handle. Add a cream cheese drizzle. Beat 2 ounces of softened cream cheese with the glaze ingredients for a tangier, richer topping that's reminiscent of cinnamon rolls.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using melted butter that's too hot. I did this once and ended up with scrambled egg bits floating in my batter. Let your melted butter cool for a full minute or two before mixing it with the egg and milk. It should feel warm to the touch, not hot. Not preheating the waffle iron enough. Impatient bakers (guilty) tend to pour batter into a waffle maker that's only halfway heated. The result is pale, soggy donuts that stick to the plates. Wait until the ready light comes on, then wait another minute. Peeling the apples. The peel adds color, texture, and keeps the apple pieces from turning to complete mush during cooking. Just wash them well and dice them with the skin on. Nobody will notice once they're baked in. Making the glaze too early. I used to mix the glaze first, and by the time the donuts were ready, it had thickened into paste. Make it while your last donut cooks—it takes 30 seconds and stays perfectly pourable. Skipping the cooling rack step. If you put hot, glazed donuts directly on a plate, the bottoms get soggy from trapped steam. A wire rack lets air circulate and keeps everything crispy.

Storage & Reheating

These are honestly best eaten within an hour of making them—that's when the contrast between crispy exterior and soft interior is at its peak. But life happens, and sometimes you have leftovers. Store them in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days. The glaze will soften and they'll lose some crispness, but the flavor stays good. Don't refrigerate them—the cold makes the texture weird and gummy. To reheat, pop them in a 350°F oven for about 5 minutes, or use a toaster oven if you're only warming one or two. The waffle iron ridges crisp back up surprisingly well with dry heat. Microwaving turns them into rubber, so avoid it unless you're absolutely desperate. You can also reheat them in an air fryer at 350°F for 2-3 minutes—this actually works better than the oven for restoring crispness.

FAQ

Can I make the batter ahead of time? Not really. The baking powder starts working as soon as it hits liquid, and if you let the batter sit for more than 15-20 minutes, your donuts won't rise properly. You can prep your dry and wet ingredients separately the night before, though, then combine them in the morning. What if I don't have a waffle maker? Pour the batter into a greased donut pan and bake at 375°F for about 12-14 minutes. You won't get the signature ridges, but they'll still taste good. Or use a regular muffin tin for apple fritter muffins. Can these be frozen? Freeze them unglazed in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer bag once solid. They'll keep for about a month. Thaw at room temperature, warm them up in the oven, then add fresh glaze. Why are my donuts coming out flat? Either your baking powder is old and has lost its oomph, or you're opening the waffle iron too early and letting all the steam escape before they've set. Check the expiration date on your baking powder and resist the urge to peek for at least 3 minutes. Can I use a different type of apple? Tart apples work best because they balance the sweet glaze. Honeycrisp and Granny Smith are my favorites. Avoid Red Delicious or Gala—they're too sweet and get mushy. Do I need to grease a nonstick waffle iron? Yes, always. Even nonstick surfaces benefit from a light coating of cooking spray. The sugar in the batter can cause sticking, especially around the edges where it caramelizes.

Conclusion

Give these a shot next time you're craving something more interesting than plain breakfast. The waffle iron trick works better than you'd expect, and the apple-glaze combination is hard to mess up. Let me know how yours turn out!

Ingredients

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 2 tablespoons sugar
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon cinnamon (plus extra for dusting if you want)
  • 1/4 teaspoon nutmeg (freshly grated tastes better, but ground works fine)
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup milk (whole milk makes them richer, but 2% is fine)
  • 1 large egg, at room temperature
  • 1/2 cup diced apples (Granny Smith or Honeycrisp—about half a medium apple)
  • 1/4 cup melted butter, slightly cooled
  • 1 cup powdered sugar (for the glaze)
  • 2 tablespoons milk (for the glaze)
  • 1/2 teaspoon vanilla extract (for the glaze)

Substitution notes: Swap the butter for melted coconut oil if you want a dairy-free version—the flavor changes slightly but still works. No nutmeg? Double the cinnamon instead. If your apples are super sweet, add a tiny squeeze of lemon juice to the batter to balance things out.

Instructions

  1. Step 1

    Get your waffle maker heating up while you prep everything else. Mine takes about 5 minutes to reach the right temperature—you want it hot enough that a drop of water sizzles immediately. Grab a medium bowl and whisk together the flour, sugar, baking powder, cinnamon, nutmeg, and salt until there aren't any clumps. This dry mixture can sit while you handle the wet stuff.

  2. Step 2

    Crack your egg into a separate bowl and beat it lightly with a fork. Pour in the milk and melted butter, whisking until everything looks smooth and the butter isn't forming separate pools. The butter should be warm but not hot—if it scrambles the egg, you've got a problem.

  3. Step 3

    Make a well in the center of your dry ingredients and pour the wet mixture in all at once. Stir with a wooden spoon or spatula just until the flour disappears—some small lumps are totally fine and actually preferred. Overmixing creates tough, dense donuts instead of light, fluffy ones. Fold in the diced apples gently so they're distributed throughout but not bruised or mushy.

  4. Step 4

    Once your waffle iron is properly heated, lightly spray it with cooking spray even if it's nonstick—trust me on this. Spoon about 1/3 cup of batter onto the center of the iron, spreading it slightly but leaving some room at the edges since it'll expand. Close the lid and cook for 3-4 minutes. You're looking for deep golden brown color and crispy edges. The exact time depends on your specific waffle maker, so watch the first one closely.

  5. Step 5

    While the first batch cooks, whisk together your glaze ingredients in a small bowl. Start with the powdered sugar, add the milk gradually, then stir in the vanilla. The consistency should be thick enough to coat the back of a spoon but thin enough to drizzle smoothly. Too thick? Add milk by the teaspoon. Too thin? Whisk in more powdered sugar.

  6. Step 6

    Transfer each finished waffle-donut to a cooling rack set over a baking sheet. Let them cool for just 2-3 minutes—you want them warm but not so hot that the glaze melts off immediately. Drizzle the glaze generously over the top, letting it drip down the sides and into those waffle ridges. Eat within 20 minutes for peak texture.

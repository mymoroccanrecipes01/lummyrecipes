Creamy Mushroom and Spinach Risotto That’s Ready in Under 45 Minutes

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 4 servings

This risotto is a quick, creamy delight packed with earthy mushrooms and fresh spinach. The secret? Toasting the rice first for that perfect bite. Ideal for a cozy weeknight dinner or a simple spring lunch.

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this mushroom and spinach risotto recipe when I wanted something comforting yet quick on a busy spring evening. Risotto can sometimes feel intimidating, but the trick I learned early on—toast the rice before adding broth—makes all the difference. It locks in that slightly nutty flavor and helps the grains hold their shape, so you get that perfect bite without it turning mushy. Plus, the earthy mushrooms combined with fresh spinach give it a lovely balance between heartiness and freshness. I’ve tweaked this recipe over time to make it more accessible, swapping out heavy cream for butter and Parmesan, which keeps it rich but not overpowering. When it’s done, you get a creamy, luscious dish with vibrant greens and tender mushrooms that’s just right for a cozy dinner or even a casual lunch. Honestly, it’s become a go-to for me when I want something that feels fancy but doesn’t take hours. If you’re new to risotto or just looking for a reliable recipe, I think you’ll love this one.

Why This Works

Risotto is all about coaxing starch from the Arborio rice to create that signature creamy texture without adding cream. Toasting the rice first is a game-changer—it slightly cooks the outer layer, which helps the grains maintain structure during the slow liquid absorption. This prevents the final dish from becoming gluey or mushy.

The gradual addition of warm broth and constant stirring encourages the rice to release its starch slowly, resulting in a luscious, velvety sauce that clings to each grain. Using warm broth keeps the cooking temperature consistent, so the rice cooks evenly and absorbs liquid properly.

Mushrooms add an earthy umami depth, which balances the richness from the butter and Parmesan cheese. Cooking the mushrooms until nicely browned is essential to develop that full flavor and avoid a watery texture. Fresh spinach added at the end brightens the dish and adds a fresh, slightly bitter counterpoint to the creamy risotto.

Finishing with butter and Parmesan not only enriches the flavor but also helps emulsify the sauce, giving the risotto its classic glossy finish. The careful layering of flavors and textures is what makes this recipe reliably delicious.

  • Look for high-quality Arborio rice, which has a higher starch content and a round, plump grain perfect for risotto. This starch is what creates the creamy texture when cooked slowly with broth. Avoid long-grain rice, as it won’t release enough starch and will yield a less creamy dish.
  • Use a flavorful, low-sodium vegetable broth warmed before adding. Warm broth keeps the cooking temperature steady, allowing the rice to cook evenly. Cold broth would slow the cooking process and could cause uneven texture.
  • Choose fresh, firm cremini mushrooms for their earthy flavor and meaty texture. Properly sautéing them until golden brown develops umami notes that enrich the risotto. Avoid overcrowding the pan to prevent steaming and sogginess.
  • Use freshly grated Parmesan for best flavor and melting quality. Pre-grated cheese often contains anti-caking agents that can affect smooth melting. Parmesan adds a salty, nutty richness that balances the mushrooms and binds the risotto sauce.
  • Adding unsalted butter at the end adds silkiness and richness. It also helps emulsify the sauce, making the risotto shiny and smooth. Using salted butter can alter seasoning, so adjust salt accordingly.

Ingredients

  • 1 1/2 cups Arborio rice, uncooked
  • 4 cups vegetable broth, kept warm
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 small onion, finely chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 8 ounces cremini mushrooms, sliced
  • 3 cups fresh spinach, roughly chopped
  • 1/2 cup dry white wine
  • 1/2 cup grated Parmesan cheese
  • 2 tablespoons unsalted butter
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Heat the olive oil in a large skillet or wide saucepan over medium heat. Add the chopped onion and cook gently until translucent and soft, about 5 minutes. This step is crucial because it builds the flavor base for the risotto. Avoid browning the onion to keep the taste delicate and sweet. Stir frequently so nothing sticks or burns, which would give a bitter note to the dish.

    ⏱ 5✓ Onions are soft and translucent without any color.
  2. Step 2

    Add the minced garlic and sliced mushrooms to the pan. Cook over medium heat until the mushrooms release their moisture and start to brown, about 7-8 minutes. This deepens the umami flavor and adds richness. If you rush this step or crowd the pan, the mushrooms will steam instead of sauté, resulting in a soggy texture.

    ⏱ 8✓ Mushrooms are golden brown and tender.
  3. Step 3

    Stir in the uncooked Arborio rice, toasting it in the pan for 2-3 minutes. This toasting step is key for risotto—it helps the rice grains stay firm and develop a nutty aroma. You’ll notice the edges of the rice become translucent with a white center. Don’t skip this or the texture will be mushier.

    ⏱ 3✓ Rice grains look translucent around the edges but still opaque in the center.
  4. Step 4

    Pour in the white wine and stir until it’s mostly absorbed by the rice. The acidity in the wine balances the richness of the mushrooms and cheese, while also helping to deglaze the pan, lifting all those tasty browned bits.

    ⏱ 3✓ Wine is mostly evaporated, and the mixture looks slightly creamy.
  5. Step 5

    Add a ladleful of warm vegetable broth to the rice, stirring constantly. Keep the heat at medium-low. The gradual addition of broth and constant stirring coax the starch from the rice, creating that signature creamy texture. Don’t add all the broth at once or stir less than needed, or the risotto won’t develop its luscious creaminess.

    ⏱ 5✓ Rice looks creamy and the liquid is mostly absorbed.
  6. Step 6

    Continue adding broth one ladle at a time, stirring frequently and waiting until the liquid is almost absorbed before adding more. This process takes about 20 minutes. Patience here is key; rushing it by adding too much broth too fast will leave the rice undercooked and the texture watery.

    ⏱ 20✓ Rice is tender but still has a slight bite (al dente), and mixture is creamy.
  7. Step 7

    When the rice is nearly done, stir in the chopped fresh spinach. The residual heat will wilt the spinach gently, preserving its vibrant color and fresh flavor without overcooking it. Avoid adding spinach too early or it will become mushy and lose brightness.

    ⏱ 2✓ Spinach is wilted but still bright green.
  8. Step 8

    Remove the pan from heat and stir in the butter and grated Parmesan cheese. This final step enriches the risotto with silkiness and depth of flavor. Stir until everything is melted and combined. Season with salt and pepper to taste. The butter and cheese also help bind the risotto, giving it a smooth, velvety finish.

    ⏱ 3✓ Risotto is creamy, glossy, and well-seasoned.
  9. Step 9

    Let the risotto rest for a minute before serving. This brief rest allows the flavors to meld and the texture to settle. Serve immediately for the best creamy consistency. If left too long, the risotto thickens and loses its signature creaminess.

    ⏱ 1✓ Risotto holds its shape but still looks creamy and inviting.

Pro Tips

  • Always keep your broth warm while making risotto. — Adding warm broth prevents the cooking temperature from dropping, which ensures the rice cooks evenly and absorbs liquid properly. Cold broth would slow down the cooking and might result in unevenly cooked rice.
  • Toast the rice before adding any liquid. — Toasting helps coat each grain in oil and slightly cooks the outside, which locks in the starch and results in a better texture. Skipping this step often leads to mushy risotto.
  • Stir frequently but gently during cooking. — Stirring helps release starch from the rice, creating creaminess, but overly vigorous stirring can break grains and make the texture gluey. Gentle, consistent stirring is key.
  • Sauté mushrooms separately until golden brown. — Cooking mushrooms properly develops deep flavor and prevents them from releasing too much water into the risotto, which would dilute the texture.
  • Add spinach at the very end. — Spinach wilts quickly and adding it late keeps it vibrant and fresh-tasting. Adding it too early results in mushy, dull greens.
  • Use freshly grated Parmesan cheese. — Freshly grated cheese melts better and adds more authentic flavor. Pre-grated cheese can contain additives affecting texture and taste.
  • Let the risotto rest briefly before serving. — This allows the flavors to meld and the texture to settle, ensuring a perfect creamy consistency that isn’t too runny or thick.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Adding broth too quickly
    Why: Many cooks rush the process wanting to speed things up, thinking it will cook faster.
    Fix: Add broth slowly, one ladle at a time, stirring constantly. This allows the rice to absorb liquid evenly and release starch gradually, preventing mushy texture.
  • Using cold broth
    Why: It’s easy to forget to warm the broth and just grab it straight from the fridge.
    Fix: Keep broth warm in a separate pot to maintain even cooking temperature, which helps the rice cook properly and prevents uneven texture.
  • Overcooking or undercooking rice
    Why: Not testing the rice texture during cooking can lead to mushy or hard grains.
    Fix: Taste the rice frequently after about 15 minutes. It should be tender but still have a slight bite (al dente). Adjust broth and cooking time accordingly.
  • Not stirring enough
    Why: Some think stirring less saves effort or time.
    Fix: Stir often to coax starch from the rice, which creates the creamy texture. Without enough stirring, risotto will be less creamy and uneven.
  • Overcrowding the pan when sautéing mushrooms
    Why: Trying to cook mushrooms all at once to save time.
    Fix: Cook mushrooms in batches if necessary to allow them to brown properly instead of steaming, which preserves texture and flavor.

Variations

  • Add crispy pancetta or bacon: For a smoky, savory twist, cook diced pancetta or bacon until crisp and stir it into the risotto just before serving. This adds a nice crunch and extra depth of flavor that pairs beautifully with mushrooms and spinach.
    Make sure to drain excess fat from the pancetta before adding to avoid greasiness. Adjust salt accordingly since cured meats add salt.
  • Swap spinach for kale: Use finely chopped kale instead of spinach for a heartier green with a slightly bitter edge. Kale stands up well to cooking and adds a different texture and flavor profile to the risotto.
    Since kale is tougher, add it a few minutes earlier than spinach to allow it to soften properly.
  • Make it vegan: Replace butter with olive oil and use nutritional yeast instead of Parmesan for a delicious dairy-free version. This keeps the risotto creamy and flavorful without animal products.
    Nutritional yeast adds a cheesy flavor but won’t melt like Parmesan, so the texture will be slightly different.
  • Add fresh herbs: Stir in fresh thyme or parsley at the end for an herbal brightness that complements the earthiness of mushrooms. This adds a fresh aroma and elevates the dish.
    Add herbs off the heat to preserve their delicate flavors and avoid bitterness.
  • Use different mushrooms: Try shiitake, oyster, or porcini mushrooms for unique textures and deeper umami flavors. Combining varieties can also enhance complexity.
    Adjust cooking time slightly based on mushroom type; denser mushrooms may need longer sautéing.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Risotto can safely sit at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered. Beyond that, bacteria growth increases, so it's best to refrigerate promptly after serving.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftover risotto in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The texture will thicken and firm up when cold; reheat gently with a splash of broth or water to loosen it back up.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze risotto in airtight containers or freezer bags for up to 2 months. To prevent freezer burn, remove as much air as possible. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat risotto on the stovetop over low heat, stirring and adding a little broth or water to restore creaminess. Avoid microwaving without stirring, as it can heat unevenly and dry out.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the mushroom sauté and toast the rice a day ahead, then refrigerate separately. On the day, warm the broth and finish cooking the risotto by gradually adding broth and stirring until creamy.

FAQ

Can I use other types of rice for risotto?
Arborio rice is best for risotto due to its high starch content, which creates the creamy texture. Short-grain varieties like Carnaroli or Vialone Nano also work well. Long-grain rice or jasmine won’t give the same creaminess and may result in a less desirable texture.
Why does my risotto sometimes turn out mushy?
Mushy risotto usually happens when you add too much liquid at once or cook the rice too long. Adding broth gradually and stirring often helps the rice cook evenly and release starch properly, keeping the grains separate yet creamy.
Is it necessary to stir risotto constantly?
Frequent stirring is important because it agitates the rice grains, encouraging starch release which creates the creamy sauce. However, it doesn’t have to be non-stop — stirring every 30 seconds or so works fine as long as you keep an eye on it.
Can I make risotto without wine?
Yes! You can simply skip the wine and start adding broth after toasting the rice. The wine adds acidity and depth but isn’t essential. You might want to add a splash of lemon juice at the end for brightness if you omit it.
How do I know when risotto is done?
Risotto is done when the rice is tender but still slightly firm to the bite (al dente) and the texture is creamy and cohesive. It shouldn’t be crunchy or mushy. Taste frequently towards the end to check doneness.
Can I prepare risotto in advance?
You can prep components like sautéed mushrooms or toasted rice ahead, but risotto is best served fresh. If made ahead, refrigerate and reheat gently with broth to restore creaminess, though texture won’t be quite the same.
What’s the best way to reheat leftover risotto?
Reheat on the stovetop over low heat with a splash of broth or water, stirring frequently to loosen the rice and restore creaminess. Avoid overheating or microwaving without stirring, which can dry it out.
Can I add other vegetables to this risotto?
Absolutely! Peas, asparagus, or roasted butternut squash work wonderfully. Add vegetables that cook quickly towards the end or pre-cook heartier ones before stirring in.

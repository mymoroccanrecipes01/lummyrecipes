Classic Italian Easter Cookies with a Delightful Citrus and Anise Twist

Lummy Hayes Published on May 29, 2026
20 min prep 18 min cook 24 servings

These soft Italian Easter cookies are bursting with bright citrus and a hint of anise, making them a perfect sweet treat for spring celebrations. Topped with cheerful sprinkles, they’re great for sharing with family around the holiday table.

Introduction

I first discovered these Italian Easter cookies years ago at a family gathering, and they quickly became a beloved tradition in my home. What makes these cookies stand out is their soft, tender crumb combined with a gentle citrus brightness and that unmistakable hint of anise—something you don’t find in your average cookie. I love how the lemon zest wakes up the dough, while the anise adds a subtle aromatic warmth that feels both nostalgic and unique. Over time, I tweaked the recipe to balance the flavors just right, making sure the cookies stay moist and soft instead of turning dry or crumbly. One trick I picked up is to carefully fold in the flour to keep the dough light, and to use fresh lemon zest for the brightest flavor. When these cookies come out of the oven, they’re slightly golden and topped with colorful sprinkles, making them as festive as they are delicious. They’re perfect for Easter morning coffee or as a sweet snack for family and friends. Honestly, every year I look forward to baking these and sharing them—it’s become a little ritual I cherish.

Why This Works

These cookies rely on a delicate balance of ingredients and technique to achieve their signature soft texture and vibrant flavor. The creaming of butter and sugar is essential to incorporate air into the dough, which lightens the crumb and creates a tender bite. If you skip this or don’t cream long enough, the cookies can turn dense.

Adding eggs gradually emulsifies the fat and liquid, creating a smooth batter that traps air and moisture. The anise extract and fresh lemon zest provide aromatic compounds that brighten and deepen the flavor profile, making these cookies uniquely festive.

The flour is sifted and carefully folded in to avoid overdeveloping gluten, which would make the cookies tough. Alternating the dry ingredients with milk ensures even hydration and a soft dough that bakes up tender rather than crumbly.

Chilling the dough before baking is crucial; it solidifies the fat, preventing excessive spread and helping maintain the cookie’s shape and texture. The sprinkling of colorful nonpareils adds a contrasting crunch and cheerful appearance, making these cookies perfect for Easter celebrations.

  • Look for a quality all-purpose flour with moderate protein content (around 10-11%) to provide structure without making cookies tough. Sifting the flour aerates it and removes lumps, which helps keep the dough light. Avoid using bread flour, which has higher protein and could yield chewier cookies.
  • Use unsalted butter at room temperature to control salt levels and ensure smooth creaming with sugar. Butter provides richness and tenderness. Overly soft or melted butter can cause spreading issues, so it’s best to soften but not liquefy it.
  • Anise extract adds a distinctive licorice-like aroma that’s traditional in Italian Easter cookies. Choose pure extract over artificial flavoring for the best taste. It’s potent, so a small amount suffices to enhance without overpowering.
  • Freshly grated lemon zest imparts bright, citrusy notes that lighten the cookie’s flavor. Avoid pre-packaged zest as it tends to lose aroma and can be bitter. Use a microplane grater to get fine zest without the bitter white pith.
  • Baking powder acts as a leavening agent, releasing carbon dioxide bubbles that help the cookies rise and become airy. Using fresh baking powder ensures proper rise; old or expired powder may result in flat cookies.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1/4 cup whole milk
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • Zest of 1 lemon
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • Colorful sprinkles for topping

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Begin by creaming the softened butter and granulated sugar together in a large bowl until the mixture is light and fluffy. This step is crucial because it incorporates air into the dough, which helps create a soft, tender crumb in the final cookie. Use a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed and beat for about 3-5 minutes. Be careful not to overbeat, as this can cause the dough to become too loose and affect the cookie’s structure. The creamed mixture should look pale and slightly increased in volume before moving on.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Mixture is pale, fluffy, and smooth without any visible sugar granules.
  2. Step 2

    Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition to ensure they’re fully incorporated. This helps to emulsify the dough, making it smooth and uniform. Then stir in the anise extract and lemon zest to infuse the dough with those signature flavors. Adding these ingredients early allows their aroma to blend deeply into the cookie base. If you rush this step or skip beating well, you might end up with uneven flavor distribution or a less cohesive dough.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Dough is smooth, glossy, and fragrant with lemon and anise scents.
  3. Step 3

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. Sifting is important here to remove lumps and to aerate the dry ingredients, which helps keep the cookies light. If you skip sifting, the dough may develop dense spots or uneven texture. Using a whisk helps evenly distribute the baking powder and salt throughout the flour, ensuring consistent rising and seasoning.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are well combined and free of lumps.
  4. Step 4

    Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, alternating with the milk. Start and end with the flour mixture. Use a spatula to gently fold the ingredients together rather than mixing vigorously. This gentle folding prevents overdeveloping gluten, which can make the cookies tough. The milk helps hydrate the dough and keeps it soft. If you add everything at once or mix too hard, the dough will lose its delicate texture.

    ⏱ 4 minutes✓ Dough is soft, slightly sticky but holds together well without being runny.
  5. Step 5

    Cover the dough with plastic wrap and chill in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes. Chilling firms up the dough, making it easier to handle and preventing the cookies from spreading too much during baking. It also allows the flavors to meld. If you skip chilling, the cookies may bake too thin and lose their soft texture.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Dough is firm to the touch and easier to shape.
  6. Step 6

    Preheat the oven to 350°F (175°C) and line baking sheets with parchment paper. Using a cookie scoop or spoon, drop dough balls about 1.5 inches apart on the sheet. Press the tops slightly flat to encourage even baking. If the dough is too cold and stiff, let it sit a few minutes to soften slightly before scooping. Crowding the cookies will cause them to bake unevenly and stick together.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cookies are evenly spaced and gently flattened on top.
  7. Step 7

    Sprinkle colorful nonpareils or sprinkles generously over the dough balls before placing them in the oven. This adds a festive touch and a slight crunch contrast. Don’t press the sprinkles too hard or they’ll sink into the dough. The sprinkles should sit lightly on top to maintain their color and texture.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Sprinkles cover cookie tops evenly without sinking.
  8. Step 8

    Bake the cookies for 15-18 minutes, rotating the baking sheets halfway through for even heat distribution. Watch closely towards the end; the cookies should be just turning golden around the edges but still soft in the center. Overbaking will dry them out and lose that tender texture. Remove from oven and let cool on the sheets for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack.

    ⏱ 15-18 minutes✓ Edges are lightly golden, centers appear set but soft.
  9. Step 9

    Allow cookies to cool completely on a wire rack before storing. Cooling helps the cookies set properly and prevents sogginess. Store in an airtight container at room temperature. If you want to keep them fresh longer, separate layers with parchment paper. These cookies taste best within a few days but can be gently reheated to refresh their softness.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Cookies are firm to the touch and completely cool.

Pro Tips

  • Use room temperature ingredients for even mixing. — When butter, eggs, and milk are all at room temperature, they combine more smoothly, preventing lumps and ensuring an even texture. Cold ingredients can cause the dough to curdle or mix unevenly, affecting cookie structure.
  • Don’t overmix once the flour is added. — Overmixing activates gluten development, which makes cookies tough and dense. Gently folding the flour in just until combined preserves tenderness and softness in the final cookie.
  • Chill the dough before baking. — Chilling solidifies the fat in the dough, preventing excessive spreading during baking. It also enhances flavor melding and makes the dough easier to handle and shape.
  • Use fresh lemon zest for the best flavor. — Fresh zest contains essential oils that provide a bright, aromatic citrus note. Pre-packaged or dried zest loses potency and can impart bitterness, diminishing the cookie’s fresh flavor.
  • Rotate baking sheets halfway through baking. — Ovens often have hot spots; rotating the sheets ensures even heat distribution, resulting in uniformly baked cookies without burnt edges or undercooked centers.
  • Press sprinkles lightly on top before baking. — Lightly pressing sprinkles helps them adhere without sinking into the dough, preserving their vibrant color and crunchy texture after baking.
  • Cool cookies on the baking sheet before transferring. — Cooling briefly on the sheet allows cookies to set and firm up, reducing the chance of breaking or crumbling when moved to a rack.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using melted butter instead of softened butter
    Why: Some recipes call for melted butter, and cooks might assume it’s interchangeable. Melted butter changes the dough’s fat distribution and can cause cookies to spread too much.
    Fix: Always use butter softened at room temperature, not melted. Soft butter creams with sugar to incorporate air, which is essential for a light texture. If you accidentally melted it, chill the dough before baking to help control spread.
  • Skipping the chilling step
    Why: Impatience or lack of planning can lead to skipping chilling. This results in dough that’s too soft and cookies that spread thin and bake unevenly.
    Fix: Plan ahead to chill the dough for at least 30 minutes. If short on time, pop the dough in the freezer for 10-15 minutes. This step is key for shape and texture.
  • Overmixing after adding flour
    Why: Eager to finish, some mix the dough too much, which develops gluten and toughens the cookies.
    Fix: Fold the flour in gently and only until just combined. Stop mixing as soon as you see no streaks of flour.
  • Using old baking powder
    Why: Baking powder loses potency over time, and cooks might overlook expiration dates.
    Fix: Test baking powder by mixing a small amount in hot water; if it bubbles vigorously, it’s fresh. Replace if inactive to ensure proper rise.
  • Not spacing cookies properly on the baking sheet
    Why: Trying to fit too many on a sheet leads to crowding, causing cookies to merge or bake unevenly.
    Fix: Leave at least 1.5 inches of space between dough balls to allow for spreading and even heat circulation.

Variations

  • Almond-Flavored Italian Easter Cookies: Swap the anise extract for almond extract and add 1/2 cup finely ground almonds to the dough. This variation offers a nutty twist while keeping the soft texture and citrus brightness. Expect a slightly denser cookie with a rich almond aroma.
    Adding ground almonds changes dough moisture absorption; you may need to reduce flour slightly to maintain softness.
  • Orange Zest and Cardamom Cookies: Replace lemon zest with orange zest and swap anise for ground cardamom for a warm, fragrant flavor profile. These spices complement each other and add complexity to the traditional cookie.
    Cardamom is potent; use sparingly to avoid overpowering. Mix well to distribute evenly.
  • Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies: Use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend in place of regular flour. The cookies will still be tender but may be slightly crumblier. Adding a teaspoon of xanthan gum can help improve texture.
    Gluten-free dough tends to be more delicate; handle gently and chill longer before baking.
  • Vegan Italian Easter Cookies: Replace eggs with flax eggs (1 tbsp ground flaxseed + 3 tbsp water per egg) and use vegan butter. The flavor remains bright, though texture is slightly less tender. Great for dairy-free and egg-free diets.
    Flax eggs require extra resting time to gel; mix flaxseed and water at least 10 minutes before adding.
  • Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies: After baking and cooling, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. This adds a rich contrast to the citrus and anise flavors and makes for an elegant presentation.
    Ensure cookies are fully cooled before dipping to prevent melting or sogginess.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain freshness and prevent sprinkles from melting.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Cookies can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 1 week. They may harden slightly when cold, so bring them to room temperature or warm gently before serving for best texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Wrap well to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm cookies in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes or microwave for 10-15 seconds with a damp paper towel to restore softness. Avoid overheating which can dry them out.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the dough up to 2 days ahead and keep it chilled tightly wrapped. Shape and bake the cookies on the day you plan to serve for the freshest taste and best texture.

FAQ

Can I use anise seeds instead of anise extract?
Yes, you can substitute anise extract with crushed anise seeds, but use about 1 teaspoon of seeds and toast them lightly before grinding to release flavor. The flavor will be more robust and slightly different in texture. Be sure to strain out any larger pieces to keep the cookie smooth.
Why are my cookies spreading too much?
Overly soft or melted butter, skipping chilling, or too much sugar can cause spreading. Use properly softened butter, chill the dough for at least 30 minutes, and measure sugar carefully. Also, ensure your oven temperature is accurate.
Can I make these cookies gluten-free?
Absolutely! Use a quality gluten-free flour blend and consider adding xanthan gum to improve texture. The cookies may be slightly more delicate, so handle the dough gently and chill longer before baking.
How do I keep the sprinkles from bleeding color?
Use high-quality, nonpareil sprinkles designed for baking. Press them lightly on top of the dough before baking and avoid using wet toppings. Also, bake at the recommended temperature to prevent melting.
What’s the best way to store leftover cookies?
Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. For longer storage, refrigerate or freeze as detailed in the storage section. Layer with parchment paper to prevent sticking.
Can I add other flavors to this recipe?
Yes! Lemon and anise are classic, but you can experiment with orange zest, almond extract, or spices like cardamom. Just adjust quantities carefully to maintain balance and avoid overpowering the delicate cookie base.
Why do my cookies turn out dry?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or too much flour. Follow baking times closely and measure flour by spooning into the cup rather than scooping. Also, ensure you don’t skip the milk, which helps keep the dough moist.
Can I freeze the dough instead of baked cookies?
Yes, you can freeze the shaped dough balls on a baking sheet, then transfer to a bag for up to 3 months. Bake from frozen—just add a couple extra minutes to the baking time.

Cozy Mexican Cinnamon-Anise Cookies with a Zesty Orange Twist

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 24 servings

These Mexican cookies blend warm cinnamon and subtle anise with a surprising pop of orange zest that livens up each bite. Perfect for sharing with friends or enjoying with a cup of tea on a lazy afternoon.

Introduction

I stumbled upon this recipe during a chilly autumn when I was craving something both comforting and a little different from my usual chocolate chip cookies. These Mexican cinnamon-anise cookies caught my eye because of their unique spice combo and the unexpected brightness from orange zest. The first time I baked them, I was skeptical about the anise, but it turns out it adds a subtle licorice-like warmth that pairs beautifully with cinnamon. What makes this recipe stand out is the way the orange zest lifts the flavors, making each bite lively and fresh rather than heavy. I also love how the cookies come out tender but with just enough crispness around the edges. One trick I've learned is to chill the dough before baking; it prevents spreading and helps those lovely spices really meld together. The finished cookies look rustic with a slight crackle on top and smell absolutely heavenly. They’re perfect for cozy afternoons with tea or coffee, or to bring to festive gatherings where their warm, inviting scent always draws compliments. Honestly, once you try these, you’ll find yourself reaching for them whenever you want a sweet snack that feels a little special.

Why This Works

The magic of this cookie recipe lies in the careful balance of spices and the way the ingredients interact during mixing and baking. The combination of baking powder and baking soda provides a gentle lift. Baking soda reacts immediately with acidic components, like brown sugar, producing carbon dioxide bubbles that lighten the dough. Meanwhile, baking powder reacts later in the baking process, ensuring a tender crumb without collapsing.

The creaming of butter and sugar is a critical step that aerates the dough. This incorporates tiny air pockets which expand in the oven, contributing to a soft, fluffy texture. Beating until light and pale ensures maximum air incorporation, which is why rushing this step can lead to dense cookies.

Orange zest is more than just a flavor enhancer; it contains essential oils that brighten the heavy warmth of cinnamon and anise. The zest’s oils volatilize during baking, releasing a fresh citrus aroma that balances the earthiness of the spices. The subtle licorice note from anise seed adds complexity without overpowering, creating a unique flavor profile distinct from typical cinnamon cookies.

Chilling the dough solidifies the butter, which slows down spreading during baking. This results in cookies that are thicker and chewier rather than thin and crispy. Additionally, chilling allows the flour to hydrate fully, which improves texture and flavor melding. The final bake at moderate temperature ensures even cooking and a golden edge, with a soft center that finishes setting as the cookies cool.

  • Choose a good quality all-purpose flour with moderate protein content (around 10-11%). This provides enough gluten formation for structure without making the cookies tough. Avoid bread flour, which has too much protein and can lead to dense results. Sifting the flour before use removes lumps and aerates it, improving texture.
  • Use fresh ground cinnamon for the best flavor. Cassia cinnamon is common and has a robust, slightly spicy profile that pairs well with anise. Older cinnamon loses its potency, so check the aroma before using. Cinnamon adds warmth and depth, essential for the classic Mexican spice flavor.
  • Anise seed provides a subtle licorice flavor that complements cinnamon without overpowering. Use ground anise sparingly, as too much can be sharp and medicinal. If you don’t have ground anise, you can grind whole seeds fresh for more aroma. It’s the key to giving these cookies their unique twist.
  • Use zest from an unwaxed orange to avoid bitterness or chemicals. The zest contains flavorful oils that brighten the cookie’s profile. Avoid the white pith beneath the zest, which is bitter. Adding zest fresh right before mixing ensures maximum citrus aroma and flavor.
  • Butter quality affects flavor and texture. Unsalted butter lets you control salt levels precisely. Use softened but not melted butter for proper creaming, which traps air and creates a tender crumb. Cold butter won’t cream well, while melted butter can lead to greasy, flat cookies.

Ingredients

  • 2 1/2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1 tablespoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground anise seed
  • Zest of 1 large orange
  • 1 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 3/4 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup packed light brown sugar
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • Optional: extra sugar and cinnamon for rolling

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    In a medium bowl, whisk together the flour, baking powder, baking soda, salt, ground cinnamon, and ground anise seed. This helps evenly distribute the leavening agents and spices, ensuring every cookie has consistent flavor and texture. Sifting the flour also aerates it, which can result in a lighter cookie. Avoid skipping this step because uneven mixing could cause some cookies to rise too much or taste too spicy.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are uniformly combined with no clumps of spices or flour visible
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, beat the softened butter with both sugars until creamy and light in color, about 3-4 minutes. This creaming step is crucial because it incorporates air into the dough, helping the cookies to rise and develop a tender crumb. If you rush it or don’t beat long enough, your cookies might turn out dense or greasy.

    ⏱ 4 minutes✓ Butter and sugars form a pale, fluffy mixture that holds soft peaks
  3. Step 3

    Add the egg and vanilla extract to the butter-sugar mixture, beating well to combine. Then stir in the orange zest. The egg adds moisture and structure, while vanilla and orange zest give depth and brightness to the flavor. Make sure to fully incorporate these ingredients so the dough is smooth and fragrant.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Dough is smooth, slightly sticky, and evenly scented with orange and vanilla
  4. Step 4

    Gradually add the dry ingredients into the wet mixture, folding gently with a spatula. Don’t overmix here — just combine until no streaks of flour remain. Overmixing develops gluten which can make your cookies tough instead of tender. The dough will be soft but manageable.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Dough forms a cohesive ball without excess flour visible
  5. Step 5

    Cover the dough with plastic wrap and chill in the refrigerator for at least 1 hour. Chilling firms up the butter and helps the flavors meld. It also prevents the cookies from spreading too much while baking, leading to a thicker, chewier texture. Don’t skip this step or your cookies may bake too thin and crisp.

    ⏱ 60 minutes✓ Dough is firm to the touch and easy to scoop
  6. Step 6

    Preheat the oven to 350°F (175°C). Line baking sheets with parchment paper. Scoop tablespoon-sized balls of dough, then roll them in a mixture of sugar and cinnamon if desired for an extra crunchy, spiced crust. Place them about 2 inches apart to allow room for spreading.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cookies are evenly spaced and coated with sugar mixture, ready to bake
  7. Step 7

    Bake the cookies for 12-15 minutes until the edges are golden and the centers look set but still soft. The cookies will continue to firm up as they cool, so avoid overbaking or they’ll be dry. Watch closely starting at 12 minutes to catch the perfect balance of chewy and crisp.

    ⏱ 12-15 minutes✓ Edges are lightly browned and centers no longer look wet
  8. Step 8

    Remove the cookies from the oven and let them cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. This resting time allows the cookies to firm up gently and prevents them from breaking apart when moved. Cooling on a rack ensures air circulation so they don’t get soggy underneath.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Cookies are cool enough to handle without breaking
  9. Step 9

    Store the cooled cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. They taste best the day they’re made but keep well and maintain their texture. If you want to refresh them, warming briefly in a low oven helps regain that fresh-baked softness.

    ⏱ N/A✓ Cookies remain fresh and flavorful when stored properly

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your dry ingredients together before adding to the wet mixture. — Sifting evenly distributes leavening agents and spices, preventing clumps and ensuring uniform flavor and rise. It aerates the flour, which helps produce a lighter, more tender cookie. Skipping this can cause uneven texture and flavor pockets.
  • Chill the dough for at least an hour before baking. — Chilling solidifies the butter, slowing spread in the oven, which results in thicker, chewier cookies. It also lets the flour hydrate and flavors meld, giving a more complex taste. Baking warm dough often leads to thin, crispy cookies that lack that tender center.
  • Use freshly grated orange zest, not bottled extract. — Fresh zest contains natural oils that provide vibrant citrus flavor and aroma, which bottled extracts lack. These oils volatilize during baking, lifting the cookie's flavor profile. Using bottled extract can give an artificial taste and less brightness.
  • Cream butter and sugar until light and fluffy. — This step incorporates air bubbles that expand during baking, creating a soft, tender texture. Rushing or under-beating yields dense cookies with poor rise. Proper creaming also dissolves sugar into butter, improving texture and sweetness distribution.
  • Don’t overmix the dough once the flour is added. — Overmixing develops gluten, which makes cookies tough and chewy in an unwanted way. Folding just until combined keeps the texture tender. Overworked dough also can cause cookies to spread unevenly during baking.
  • Use parchment paper or silicone mats on baking sheets. — This prevents sticking and promotes even baking by insulating the bottom of the cookies. It also makes cleanup easier and helps maintain cookie shape. Using unlined sheets can cause burning or uneven browning.
  • Let cookies cool on the baking sheet for a few minutes before transferring. — This resting lets the cookies firm up slightly, preventing breakage when moved. The residual heat finishes baking the centers gently, ensuring a chewy texture. Immediate removal can cause cookies to fall apart or be doughy inside.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the dough chilling step
    Why: Often people want to bake immediately and think chilling is unnecessary or time-consuming.
    Fix: Plan ahead and chill the dough for at least an hour. This solidifies the butter and hydrates flour, preventing excessive spreading and producing thicker, chewier cookies with better flavor. If short on time, chill at least 30 minutes.
  • Overmixing after adding flour
    Why: It’s tempting to mix thoroughly to combine ingredients, but this can develop gluten.
    Fix: Fold dry ingredients gently just until no streaks remain. Overmixing toughens cookies and causes uneven texture. Use a spatula to fold instead of a mixer at this stage.
  • Using old or stale spices
    Why: Spices lose potency over time and are often forgotten in the pantry.
    Fix: Check spice freshness before baking. Fresh cinnamon and anise provide vibrant flavor. Replace spices yearly or grind fresh seeds for best results.
  • Adding too much orange zest or including pith
    Why: Sometimes zest is scraped too deeply or overused, causing bitterness.
    Fix: Use only the thin colored outer layer of the orange peel. Avoid white pith beneath, which is bitter. One orange’s zest is enough to add brightness without overpowering.
  • Not creaming butter and sugar long enough
    Why: People rush this step to save time or use cold butter.
    Fix: Use softened, room-temperature butter and beat sugars and butter for 3-4 minutes until pale and fluffy. This traps air, essential for tender cookies. Rushing results in dense, heavy texture.

Variations

  • Chocolate-Dipped Cinnamon-Anise Cookies: After baking and cooling, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. The rich chocolate pairs beautifully with the warm spices and adds a touch of indulgence. Perfect for gifting or special occasions.
    Use good-quality chocolate and temper it if possible for a shiny finish. Let cookies cool completely before dipping to prevent melting.
  • Gluten-Free Mexican Spice Cookies: Substitute the all-purpose flour with a gluten-free blend that includes xanthan gum. This keeps the texture tender while maintaining the spice flavor. Ideal for gluten-sensitive friends or family members.
    Gluten-free dough may be slightly more fragile; chill well before baking and handle gently.
  • Spiced Orange Iced Cookies: Once cooled, drizzle a simple icing made from powdered sugar and fresh orange juice over the cookies. This adds sweetness and a glossy finish, enhancing the citrus notes and making them extra festive.
    Apply icing when cookies are fully cool to avoid melting. Allow icing to set before storing.
  • Anise-Free Cinnamon Orange Cookies: Omit the anise seed and add an extra teaspoon of cinnamon plus a pinch of nutmeg for a more classic cinnamon cookie. This version is great for those who prefer less licorice flavor but still want warm spices.
    Adjust spices carefully to keep balance; nutmeg is potent and should be added sparingly.
  • Vegan Mexican Spice Cookies: Replace butter with vegan margarine or coconut oil and use a flax egg (1 tbsp ground flaxseed + 3 tbsp water) instead of a chicken egg. These swaps keep the cookies tender and flavorful without animal products.
    Chill the dough well to compensate for the softer fat and moisture from flax egg.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. Keep away from direct sunlight and heat to maintain freshness and prevent them from becoming stale or soft.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Cookies can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 10 days. Refrigeration may firm them up and slightly dull the citrus aroma, so allow to come to room temperature before serving for best flavor.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a sealed freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Separate layers with parchment paper to prevent sticking. Thaw at room temperature before serving to restore texture and flavor.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm cookies briefly in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes to refresh softness and revive the orange zest aroma. Avoid microwaving as it can make them rubbery. Adding a slice of bread in the container helps keep cookies moist.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare the dough 1-2 days ahead and keep chilled tightly wrapped. Scoop and roll dough balls, then freeze on a tray. When ready to bake, place frozen dough on baking sheets and bake an extra 2-3 minutes.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise seed with another spice?
Yes, if you don’t like anise, you can substitute with fennel seed or simply increase cinnamon slightly. Both fennel and anise have similar licorice-like notes but fennel is milder. Avoid overpowering the dough, so add spices sparingly to keep balance.
What if I don’t have orange zest? Can I use extract?
While fresh orange zest is best for vibrant citrus oils, you can substitute with 1/2 teaspoon of orange extract. Be cautious as extracts can be strong and sometimes artificial tasting. Add gradually and taste the dough to avoid overpowering the spices.
Why do my cookies spread too much and get flat?
Flat cookies usually result from dough that’s too warm or not chilled long enough, or from over-softened butter. Chilling the dough solidifies fat, preventing excessive spread. Also, don’t overmix after adding flour as it can affect texture.
Can I make these cookies gluten-free?
Absolutely! Use a gluten-free flour blend that includes xanthan gum to mimic gluten’s binding properties. The dough may be more delicate, so chill it well and handle gently. Baking times might vary slightly, so watch closely.
How long do these cookies keep their freshness?
Stored properly in an airtight container at room temperature, these cookies stay fresh for about 5 days. After that, they may start to lose crispness or become stale. Refrigeration extends freshness but can dull flavors slightly.
Can I freeze the dough instead of the baked cookies?
Yes! Freeze the dough balls on a tray until firm, then transfer to a freezer bag. When ready, bake from frozen, adding a few extra minutes to the baking time. This is a great way to have fresh cookies on demand.
Why are my cookies dry or crumbly?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or too much flour. Make sure to measure flour properly by spooning it in and leveling off. Also, follow baking times closely and remove cookies when centers are just set but not browned.
Can I add nuts or dried fruit to this recipe?
Yes! Chopped pecans or walnuts add a nice crunch, while dried cranberries or raisins bring sweetness and chew. Fold in about 1/2 cup during the final mixing stage. Adjust baking time slightly if adding moist ingredients.

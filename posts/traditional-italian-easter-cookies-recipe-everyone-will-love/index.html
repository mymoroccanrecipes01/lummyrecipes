Classic Italian Easter Cookies with Almond and Anise Glaze

Lummy Hayes
Published on April 7, 2026
30 min Prep Time
20 min Time
24 Servings
Medium Difficulty

These soft Italian Easter cookies are lightly sweet with a lovely almond flavor and a hint of anise. The glossy glaze adds a special touch. Perfect for holiday celebrations or a cozy afternoon treat!

Pro Tips

Use room temperature eggs for easier mixing and better texture.
Room temperature eggs incorporate more smoothly into the dough, preventing clumps and ensuring an even crumb. Cold eggs can cause the dough to seize or become tough. The warmth helps activate the baking powder better, improving rise and texture.
Rest the dough for at least 15 minutes before baking.
Allowing the dough to rest hydrates the almond flour fully and lets flavors meld. This resting period results in a softer, more cohesive dough that bakes into tender cookies. Skipping this can lead to a crumbly or dry texture.
Line your baking sheet with parchment paper for even baking.
Parchment paper prevents sticking and promotes even heat distribution on the bottom of the cookies. Without it, cookies can brown unevenly or stick, making removal difficult and risking breakage.
Don’t overmix the dough once wet and dry ingredients combine.
Overmixing can develop gluten in any residual wheat flour or break down the almond flour structure, causing tough or dense cookies. Stir gently until just combined to keep the texture light and tender.
Watch the cookies closely in the last few minutes of baking.
Because almond flour cookies brown quickly, monitoring prevents overbaking which dries them out. Pulling them out when edges are golden but centers still soft ensures a chewy, moist bite.
Apply glaze when cookies are slightly warm, not hot or cold.
Glazing warm cookies allows the glaze to set nicely without melting off. If cookies are too hot, the glaze will run; if cold, it won’t adhere properly, resulting in uneven coating.
Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days.
Almond flour cookies retain moisture well but can dry out if exposed to air. Airtight storage preserves softness and flavor. Refrigeration can dry them out so room temp is best for short term.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using coarse almond meal instead of fine almond flour

Why it happens People often confuse almond meal with almond flour and use whichever is on hand, assuming they’re interchangeable.

Fix Always use finely ground almond flour (blanched) for this recipe. Coarse meal results in gritty texture and crumbly cookies. If you only have almond meal, pulse it in a food processor to make it finer before using.

Skipping the dough resting time

Why it happens In the rush to bake, resting is overlooked as unnecessary.

Fix Plan ahead to rest the dough 15-20 minutes. This hydration step softens the dough and improves flavor. If skipped, cookies may turn out dry or crumbly.

Overbaking the cookies

Why it happens Not watching the cookies closely leads to baking past the golden edge stage.

Fix Set a timer and check cookies at 18 minutes. Remove when edges are lightly golden and centers still look soft. They firm up as they cool, so don’t wait for them to look fully set in the oven.

Using too much anise extract

Why it happens Anise is a strong flavor and it’s tempting to add extra to enhance taste.

Fix Measure carefully and stick to the recipe amount. Excess anise can overpower the almond and make cookies taste medicinal rather than sweet and fragrant.

Applying glaze to hot cookies

Why it happens Eager to finish, some glaze right after removing from oven.

Fix Let cookies cool for 5 minutes until just warm before glazing. Hot cookies cause glaze to melt and run off, losing that pretty shine and flavor balance.

Variations

Lemon Almond Cookies

Swap the anise extract for fresh lemon zest and a teaspoon of lemon juice in the glaze. This gives the cookies a bright, citrusy twist while keeping the soft almond base. Expect a fresher, tangier flavor profile that’s perfect for springtime.

Note: Lemon juice in the glaze may thin it slightly; adjust powdered sugar accordingly for a thicker consistency.

Chocolate-Dipped Almond Cookies

After glazing and cooling, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate. This adds a rich, bittersweet contrast to the sweet almond and anise flavors. The texture becomes more indulgent and visually striking.

Note: Allow glaze to fully set before dipping to prevent mixing. Chill dipped cookies briefly to set chocolate faster.

Gluten-Free Almond Anise Cookies

This recipe is naturally gluten-free thanks to almond flour. To ensure no cross-contamination, use certified gluten-free almond flour and baking powder. Resulting cookies remain soft and flavorful without any wheat flour.

Note: Check all ingredient labels carefully to avoid hidden gluten sources.

Spiced Almond Cookies

Add 1/2 teaspoon cinnamon and a pinch of nutmeg to the dry ingredients for a warm spice variation. The anise pairs nicely with these spices, creating a cozy, aromatic cookie perfect for cooler months.

Note: Spices may slightly darken the dough; watch baking time closely to avoid overbaking.

Honey-Glazed Almond Cookies

Replace powdered sugar glaze with a honey glaze made by warming honey with a splash of water and anise extract. The honey adds natural sweetness and a subtle floral note, making these cookies a bit stickier but deliciously rich.

Note: Honey glaze is thinner and tackier; apply sparingly and allow extra time to set.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store these cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat sources to maintain softness and prevent the glaze from melting or becoming sticky.

Refrigerator

You can refrigerate cookies in an airtight container for up to 7 days. Note that refrigeration may firm up the cookies and make the glaze less shiny. Bring to room temperature before serving for best texture.

Freezer

Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet until firm, then transfer to a sealed freezer bag or container for up to 3 months. Wrap well to avoid freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving to restore softness.

Reheating

These cookies are best enjoyed at room temperature. If desired, warm briefly in a low oven (250°F/120°C) for 5 minutes to refresh softness. Avoid microwave reheating as it can make the glaze sticky and the cookies chewy.

Make Ahead

Prepare the dough a day ahead and refrigerate it. Shape the cookies just before baking to preserve freshness. You can also bake the cookies in advance and store them glazed or unglazed, glazing fresh before serving for the best appearance.

FAQ

Can I substitute almond flour with regular flour?
You can, but the texture and flavor will be very different. Almond flour provides moisture and a tender crumb that wheat flour can’t replicate. If you use regular flour, expect drier, less chewy cookies. Also, you may need to adjust the liquid content and baking time.
What if I don’t have anise extract?
You can omit it or substitute with a small amount of vanilla extract for a milder flavor. Another option is using a pinch of ground anise seeds or fennel seeds, but use sparingly to avoid overpowering the cookies.
How do I know when the cookies are done?
Look for lightly golden edges and set but soft centers. The cookies firm up as they cool, so don't wait for them to be hard in the oven. Overbaking will dry them out, so err on the side of slightly underbaked.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
Replacing eggs is tricky since they provide structure and moisture. You can try flax or chia egg substitutes, but the texture will be different and possibly crumblier. Experiment with a small batch first to see if you like the result.
Why is resting the dough important?
Resting allows the almond flour to absorb moisture fully and lets the flavors meld, resulting in a softer dough and more flavorful cookies. Skipping this step can cause dryness or crumbly texture.
How long do these cookies keep fresh?
Stored properly in an airtight container at room temperature, they stay fresh for about 3 days. Refrigeration can extend this to a week but may change texture. Freezing is best for longer storage.
Can I freeze the dough instead of baked cookies?
Yes! Shape the dough into balls, freeze on a tray until firm, then transfer to a freezer bag. Bake from frozen, adding a couple of extra minutes to baking time. This is a great way to have fresh cookies anytime.
What’s the difference between almond flour and almond meal?
Almond flour is finely ground blanched almonds without skins, resulting in a light texture. Almond meal includes skins and is coarser, which can make cookies gritty or denser. For this recipe, almond flour is best.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies

  • 2 1/2 cups almond flour, finely ground
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1 teaspoon almond extract
  • 1/2 teaspoon anise extract
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1 cup powdered sugar, for glaze
  • 2 tablespoons water, for glaze
  • 1/4 teaspoon anise extract, for glaze

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line a baking sheet with parchment paper. This step is crucial because a properly preheated oven ensures even baking and helps the cookies rise just right. Using parchment prevents sticking and promotes an even bottom crust. Avoid skipping this or using a greased pan, as it can cause uneven baking or browning.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Oven fully preheated to 350°F and baking sheet lined with parchment
Step 2
In a large mixing bowl, whisk together the almond flour, sugar, salt, and baking powder. Mixing these dry ingredients well distributes the leavening agent evenly, which is essential for uniform texture. If you rush this step, you might end up with dense or unevenly risen cookies.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are combined with no visible clumps
Step 3
In a separate bowl, beat the eggs, almond extract, and anise extract until smooth. The eggs provide structure and moisture while the extracts infuse the dough with flavor. Beating them well helps to incorporate air, which lightens the dough slightly.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Egg mixture is smooth and slightly frothy
Step 4
Gradually add the wet ingredients to the dry mixture, stirring gently until just combined. Overmixing can cause the cookies to become tough, so mix until you see no dry flour. The dough should be soft but hold together well.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Dough is cohesive and slightly sticky but not runny
Step 5
Cover the dough and let it rest at room temperature for 15 minutes. This resting time allows the almond flour to hydrate fully and the flavors to meld, resulting in a softer, more flavorful cookie. Skipping this can lead to a drier texture.
⏱ Time: 15
✓ Doneness: Dough feels more pliable and less sticky after resting
Step 6
Using a tablespoon or small cookie scoop, place dough balls about 2 inches apart on the prepared baking sheet. Slight spacing is important because the cookies will spread slightly as they bake. Crowding them results in misshapen or stuck-together cookies.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Dough balls evenly spaced and uniform in size
Step 7
Bake the cookies for 18-20 minutes until they’re golden at the edges but still soft in the center. Watch closely in the last few minutes to avoid overbaking, which dries them out. The aroma will be fragrant with almond and anise notes when done.
⏱ Time: 20
✓ Doneness: Edges are lightly golden, tops appear set but soft
Step 8
While the cookies bake, prepare the glaze by whisking powdered sugar, water, and anise extract until smooth. The glaze should be thin enough to drizzle but thick enough to coat. This balance is key to getting that delicate shiny finish without it running off.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Glaze is smooth, glossy, and pourable
Step 9
Once the cookies are out and slightly cooled (about 5 minutes), drizzle the glaze over them using a spoon or a small piping bag. Allow the glaze to set completely before serving or storing. This final step adds a lovely sweet finish and a subtle licorice note from the anise that makes these cookies special.
⏱ Time: 15
✓ Doneness: Glaze is set, no longer sticky to touch

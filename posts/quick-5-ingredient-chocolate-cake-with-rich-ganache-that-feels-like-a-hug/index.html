Easy Five-Ingredient Chocolate Cake with Silky Ganache

Lummy Hayes
Published on June 5, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
6 Servings
Easy Difficulty

This chocolate cake is a total lifesaver when you want something rich but fuss-free. Using just five ingredients plus a smooth ganache, it’s perfect for a quick dessert or unexpected guests.

Introduction

I stumbled upon this chocolate cake recipe on a hectic weeknight when I had zero time but a desperate craving for chocolate. What I love most is how it requires just five basic ingredients—plus a quick ganache—and still manages to taste like a fancy bakery treat. The trick I learned early on is to mix the wet and dry ingredients separately, then combine gently to keep the cake moist and tender. Also, warming the cream before adding the chocolate ensures a silky ganache that’s not too thick or runny. The finished cake is rich, deeply chocolatey, and glossy on top thanks to the ganache. It’s the kind of dessert that feels indulgent without a ton of effort, perfect for those last-minute cravings or casual dinner parties. I’ve made this countless times, and every time it’s a hit, even with friends who usually prefer fruit desserts. It’s simple, quick, and just downright satisfying.

What You'll Need

One 9-inch round cake, about 6 servings

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup vegetable oil
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • For ganache: 1 cup heavy cream
  • For ganache: 8 ounces semisweet chocolate, chopped

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease and flour a 9-inch round cake pan to prevent sticking. This step is crucial because it ensures your cake will release easily after baking without tearing. If you skip greasing, the cake might stick and crumble when you try to remove it, ruining the presentation. Using a pastry brush or paper towel to apply oil evenly helps create a thin, non-stick layer. Dust the pan lightly with flour, tapping out excess to avoid clumps.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Pan surface is evenly coated with a thin, smooth layer of oil and flour, no bare spots
Step 2
In a large bowl, sift together the flour, sugar, cocoa powder, baking powder, and salt. Sifting helps eliminate lumps and aerates the dry ingredients, which leads to a lighter, more even crumb in your cake. Take your time here; rushing can cause uneven mixing and result in pockets of flour or cocoa. Use a whisk to combine these ingredients thoroughly so the leavening agent is evenly distributed for proper rise.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are uniform in color and texture with no visible clumps
Step 3
In a separate bowl, whisk together the vegetable oil, water, and vanilla extract until fully combined. This wet mixture will hydrate the dry ingredients and add moisture. The oil keeps the cake tender while the water helps dissolve the sugar and cocoa powder, allowing flavors to blend. Make sure your water is at room temperature to prevent shocking the batter and affecting the texture.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Liquid mixture is smooth and homogenous with no separation
Step 4
Pour the wet ingredients into the dry ingredients and gently fold with a spatula until just combined. Overmixing can develop gluten and make the cake tough, so fold carefully to keep it tender and moist. Stop mixing as soon as you don’t see dry flour patches. The batter will be slightly runny, which is perfect for this cake’s texture.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is smooth with no visible dry spots but not overmixed or glossy
Step 5
Pour the batter into your prepared cake pan and smooth the top with a spatula. This ensures even baking and a nice flat surface for the ganache. Tap the pan gently on the counter to release any air bubbles trapped inside the batter, which helps avoid holes or uneven texture in the final cake.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter evenly spread, surface smooth with no large bubbles
Step 6
Bake in the preheated oven for 28 to 32 minutes. Start checking at the 28-minute mark by inserting a toothpick in the center. It should come out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter. Baking too long dries out the cake, too short leaves it undercooked and gummy. The oven temperature and timing ensure the cake rises properly and sets with a tender crumb.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Toothpick inserted in center comes out mostly clean with some moist crumbs
Step 7
While the cake bakes, prepare the ganache. Heat the heavy cream in a small saucepan over medium heat until it just begins to simmer—do not boil. Pour the hot cream over the chopped chocolate in a heatproof bowl. Let it sit for 2 minutes to soften the chocolate before stirring gently until smooth and glossy. This slow melting ensures a silky, lump-free ganache that will spread easily.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Ganache is shiny, smooth, and pourable with no lumps
Step 8
Once the cake is cool to the touch but still slightly warm, pour the ganache over the top, spreading gently with a spatula to cover evenly. Pouring while slightly warm helps the ganache spread smoothly and set with a glossy finish. If the cake is too hot, ganache can run off; too cold, it will thicken and be harder to spread.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Ganache evenly coats cake surface with a shiny, smooth finish
Step 9
Let the ganache set at room temperature for at least 30 minutes before slicing. This allows it to firm up slightly without becoming hard, giving a rich yet soft texture. If you’re in a hurry, refrigerate for 15 minutes but watch carefully to avoid over-chilling, which can make ganache brittle and crack when cut.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Ganache is set but still soft to the touch and glossy

Pro Tips

Always sift your dry ingredients before mixing.
Sifting aerates the flour and cocoa powder, breaks up clumps, and ensures even distribution of baking powder. This prevents dense spots and promotes an even rise, giving your cake a light, tender crumb.
Don’t overmix the batter after combining wet and dry ingredients.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which makes the cake tough and chewy rather than soft and tender. Fold gently just until no flour remains for the best texture.
Use room temperature water for the wet ingredients.
Cold water can cause uneven mixing and affect the leavening reaction, while hot water might start cooking the batter prematurely. Room temperature water hydrates the ingredients evenly for consistent texture.
Warm the cream before adding it to the chocolate for ganache.
Heating the cream helps melt the chocolate gently, promoting emulsification. This creates a smooth, glossy ganache without lumps or separation, essential for a professional finish.
Let the cake cool slightly before pouring ganache.
If the cake is too hot, the ganache will run off; too cold and it won’t spread smoothly. Slight warmth helps the ganache set evenly with a shiny finish.
Tap the cake pan on your countertop before baking.
This releases trapped air bubbles in the batter, preventing holes or tunnels in the cake and ensuring an even crumb.
Use a toothpick to check doneness starting at the earlier time.
Ovens vary, so testing early prevents overbaking, which dries out the cake. The toothpick should come out with moist crumbs, not wet batter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Skipping the greasing and flouring of the pan

Why it happens People often underestimate how sticky chocolate cake batter can be and forget this step, assuming non-stick pans are enough.

Fix Always grease and flour your pan thoroughly. Use a pastry brush to spread oil evenly, then dust with flour and tap out excess. This simple step ensures your cake releases without crumbling.

Overmixing the batter after combining wet and dry ingredients

Why it happens It’s tempting to mix thoroughly to avoid lumps, but this causes gluten development.

Fix Mix just until combined. Fold gently with a spatula, stopping as soon as you see no dry streaks. This keeps the cake tender.

Using expired baking powder

Why it happens Baking powder often gets overlooked in pantry checks and loses potency over time.

Fix Test baking powder by mixing a teaspoon in hot water—if it bubbles vigorously, it’s fresh. Replace if not active to ensure proper rise.

Pouring ganache on a hot cake

Why it happens Impatience or lack of cooling time leads to ganache running off the sides.

Fix Cool cake until just warm or room temperature before pouring ganache. It will spread smoothly and set nicely.

Boiling the cream for ganache

Why it happens Some think cream must boil to melt chocolate fully.

Fix Heat cream until just simmering with tiny bubbles around edges, then remove from heat. Boiling can cause ganache to separate or become grainy.

Variations

Add Espresso for a Mocha Twist

Mix 1 teaspoon of instant espresso powder into the dry ingredients to enhance the chocolate flavor with a subtle coffee note. This gives the cake a deeper, richer taste without overpowering sweetness.

Note: Espresso powder dissolves quickly and won’t affect batter texture, so no timing changes needed.

Use Coconut Oil Instead of Vegetable Oil

Swap vegetable oil for virgin coconut oil to add a mild tropical aroma and slightly denser crumb. The cake will have a subtle coconut flavor that pairs beautifully with chocolate.

Note: Melt coconut oil before mixing with wet ingredients to ensure even distribution.

Make It Gluten-Free

Replace all-purpose flour with a gluten-free flour blend designed for baking. The texture will be a bit more delicate but still moist and delicious, perfect for gluten-sensitive friends.

Note: Add 1/4 teaspoon xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t include it to help with structure.

Top with Fresh Berries

Add a handful of fresh raspberries or strawberries on top of the ganache for a pop of color and tartness. The fruit balances the rich chocolate and adds freshness.

Note: Add berries just before serving to avoid sogginess.

Use Dark Chocolate for Ganache

Swap semisweet chocolate for dark chocolate (70% cacao or higher) in the ganache for a more intense, slightly bitter finish that chocolate lovers will adore.

Note: Dark chocolate ganache may set firmer; let it warm slightly before spreading if too stiff.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

This cake can safely sit covered on the counter for up to 24 hours. Keep it in a cake dome or loosely covered with foil to prevent drying out and protect from dust.

Refrigerator

Store in an airtight container or covered with plastic wrap in the fridge for up to 4 days. The ganache will firm up when cold, so you may want to bring the cake to room temperature before serving for best texture.

Freezer

Freeze the cake wrapped tightly in plastic wrap and foil for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge, then bring to room temperature before serving to restore softness.

Reheating

Warm slices gently in the microwave for 10-15 seconds or in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes. Adding a tiny splash of milk can help soften the ganache if it firms too much.

Make Ahead

You can bake the cake a day ahead and keep it covered at room temperature. Prepare and chill the ganache separately, then pour it over the cake just before serving for freshest appearance and texture.

FAQ

Can I use butter instead of vegetable oil?
Yes, but keep in mind that butter will create a denser crumb and can dry out faster because it solidifies when cooled. If you use melted butter, reduce oven temperature slightly and watch baking time closely to avoid overbaking.
What if I don’t have heavy cream for ganache?
You can substitute with half-and-half or whole milk, but the ganache won’t be as rich or thick. To compensate, use slightly less liquid or add a small amount of butter to increase fat content and improve texture.
How do I know when the cake is done?
Insert a toothpick into the center at about 28 minutes. It should come out with moist crumbs but no wet batter. If it’s wet, bake a few minutes longer and test again every 2 minutes to avoid drying out.
Can I make this cake vegan?
This recipe isn’t vegan as-is due to heavy cream and oil, but you can substitute plant-based milk and coconut cream for ganache, and use flaxseed or applesauce as egg replacers if adding eggs. Texture and flavor will differ somewhat.
Why is my ganache grainy?
Grainy ganache usually means the chocolate overheated or the cream boiled. Always heat cream just until simmering and stir gently after adding chocolate. Using good quality chocolate also helps avoid graininess.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, just double all ingredients and use a larger pan or two pans. Adjust baking time accordingly—larger cakes may take longer, so check doneness with a toothpick starting at the original bake time.
How should I store leftover cake?
Keep leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. Bring to room temperature before serving for best texture. You can also freeze slices wrapped tightly for up to 2 months.
Can I add nuts or other mix-ins?
Absolutely! Chopped walnuts or pecans folded into the batter add crunch and flavor. Just add about 1/2 cup and fold gently before baking. Avoid overloading so the cake still rises well.

Easy Five-Ingredient Chocolate Cake That’s Ready in Just 20 Minutes

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
10 min prep 10 min cook 8 servings

This chocolate cake is a quick delight with just five ingredients and no fuss. The secret? Using cocoa powder and a simple mix that whips up in 20 minutes flat. Perfect for springtime treats when you want something sweet without the wait!

It’s crazy how just five simple ingredients can come together so quickly to create a moist, rich cake that tastes way more complicated than it is. I remember the first time I tried it, I was pleasantly surprised by how fluffy and tender it turned out, even though the batter was so simple. Give it a try and see how quickly you can satisfy that chocolate craving!

Why This Works

This recipe succeeds because it relies on the perfect balance of basic ingredients and straightforward techniques that highlight their natural properties. Cocoa powder is the star here, providing intense chocolate flavor without the heaviness of melted chocolate. It also contributes to the cake’s tender crumb by absorbing moisture without adding fat, unlike baking with chocolate bars. The sifting step is critical to break up cocoa lumps and aerate the flour, which helps keep the texture light and avoids dense spots.

Baking powder is the leavening agent that makes the cake rise, creating those soft, airy bubbles that give the crumb its gentle lift. Using room temperature milk ensures that the baking powder activates properly, as cold ingredients can slow chemical reactions and result in a denser cake. Gradually adding the milk while stirring prevents lumps, creating a smooth batter that bakes evenly.

Another key technique is avoiding overmixing once the wet and dry ingredients combine. Overworking the batter develops gluten strands, which toughen the crumb and make the cake chewy. Gentle folding and minimal stirring preserve a tender texture. The baking time and temperature are calibrated to cook the cake through without drying it out, with the toothpick test as a foolproof indicator.

Finally, letting the cake cool in the pan briefly before removing it prevents it from breaking apart, as the crumb firms up slightly. Cooling completely before frosting avoids melting or sliding toppings. These small but important steps combine to make a quick, fuss-free chocolate cake that’s moist, rich, and surprisingly fluffy given the simplicity of ingredients.

  • Choose a good-quality all-purpose flour with moderate protein content (around 10-11%). This strikes the right balance between structure and tenderness. Too much protein leads to a tough cake, while too little can make it crumbly. Sifting the flour before mixing helps incorporate air and remove lumps, aiding in a light texture.
  • Opt for natural unsweetened cocoa powder rather than Dutch-processed for a brighter, more acidic chocolate flavor that interacts well with baking powder. Cocoa powder adds rich color and deep chocolate taste without extra fat. Store it in a cool, dry place to maintain freshness and avoid clumping.
  • Use fresh baking powder to ensure proper rise. It acts as a chemical leavening agent releasing carbon dioxide when heated, creating bubbles that lighten the cake. Old or expired baking powder can cause the cake to be dense and flat. Measure precisely as too much can cause a metallic taste.
  • Regular granulated sugar is best here for sweetness and structure, as it helps tenderize by absorbing moisture and interfering with gluten development. It also caramelizes slightly during baking, adding flavor complexity. Avoid substituting with powdered or brown sugar unless you adjust liquid content accordingly.
  • Room temperature milk is ideal to keep the batter smooth and promote proper leavening. Milk adds moisture and richness, while its proteins contribute to crumb tenderness. Whole milk is preferred for its fat content, but 2% works fine. Cold milk can cause uneven mixing and reduce rise.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 cup milk, room temperature

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and grease an 8-inch round cake pan with butter or non-stick spray. This step is crucial because it ensures the cake doesn’t stick, making it easier to remove after baking. While the oven warms up, line the bottom of the pan with parchment paper for extra insurance against sticking. A common mistake is to skip greasing or lining, which can cause your cake to break apart when you try to serve it.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Pan is evenly greased and parchment fits snugly at the bottom
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, sift together the flour, cocoa powder, and baking powder. Sifting is essential here to break up any lumps, especially in the cocoa powder, which tends to clump. Mixing these dry ingredients well ensures a uniform texture and even rise. If you rush this step or skip sifting, the cake crumb might be uneven or gritty.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are lump-free and well combined with a smooth appearance
  3. Step 3

    Add the sugar to the dry mixture and stir gently to combine. It’s important to mix the sugar evenly without overworking the batter at this stage, as you want to maintain a light texture. Overmixing can develop gluten, making the cake dense. Just a few gentle strokes until combined is perfect.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Sugar is fully incorporated, and mixture looks evenly blended
  4. Step 4

    Pour in the milk gradually while stirring the batter with a whisk or spoon. Adding the milk slowly helps to avoid lumps and creates a smooth, pourable batter. Use room temperature milk to keep the batter consistent and help the baking powder activate properly. Cold milk can cause the batter to seize or mix unevenly.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter is smooth, shiny, and free of lumps
  5. Step 5

    Once your batter is smooth, pour it into the prepared cake pan. Use a spatula to scrape down the bowl and level the batter evenly in the pan. This ensures an even bake and prevents one side from cooking faster than the other. Avoid shaking the pan vigorously, as this can create air pockets that cause holes in the cake crumb.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Batter sits evenly in the pan with a smooth surface
  6. Step 6

    Bake the cake in the preheated oven for 18-20 minutes. Keep an eye on it starting at 18 minutes to avoid overbaking, which dries out the cake. You can test doneness by inserting a toothpick into the center; it should come out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter. The cake should spring back lightly when touched.

    ⏱ 18-20 minutes✓ Toothpick comes out mostly clean, and cake surface springs back lightly
  7. Step 7

    Remove the cake from the oven and let it cool in the pan for 10 minutes. This resting time allows the cake to firm up slightly, making it easier to remove from the pan. Don’t skip this step, or your cake might break apart. After 10 minutes, run a knife around the edges before turning it out onto a wire rack to cool completely.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cake edges pull slightly away from pan, and cake releases easily
  8. Step 8

    Once the cake is fully cooled, you can frost or dust it with powdered sugar. I love keeping it simple with a light dusting or a quick whipped cream topping. If you want a richer finish, try a quick chocolate glaze or frosting, but the cake is delicious on its own. Cooling fully is important; otherwise, frosting will melt and slide off.

    ⏱ 15 minutes✓ Cake surface is cool to the touch and ready for decorating
  9. Step 9

    Slice and serve! Use a sharp knife wiped between cuts for clean slices. This cake pairs wonderfully with fresh berries or a scoop of vanilla ice cream. Enjoy immediately or store leftovers covered at room temperature for up to two days.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Clean, even slices with moist crumb

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your dry ingredients together for the best texture. — Sifting breaks up lumps and aerates the flour and cocoa powder, which helps distribute leavening agents evenly and results in a lighter, more uniform crumb. Skipping this step often leads to dense pockets or gritty spots.
  • Use room temperature milk to mix with dry ingredients. — Milk at room temperature blends more smoothly with dry ingredients, preventing clumps and ensuring the baking powder activates properly. Cold liquids can slow chemical reactions and cause uneven rising.
  • Don't overmix once you combine wet and dry ingredients. — Overmixing develops gluten strands in the flour, which toughens the cake and ruins the tender crumb. Stir just until combined to keep the texture soft and fluffy.
  • Grease and line your cake pan to prevent sticking. — Proper pan preparation ensures the cake releases cleanly without breaking. Parchment lining especially helps with delicate cakes that can stick to the bottom during baking.
  • Test doneness with a toothpick starting at 18 minutes. — Ovens vary, so checking early prevents overbaking, which dries out the cake. The toothpick should come out with moist crumbs but no wet batter.
  • Let the cake cool in the pan before removing. — This resting period allows the cake structure to set, reducing the chance of it breaking apart when you turn it out.
  • For extra moistness, add a teaspoon of vanilla extract. — Vanilla adds depth and enhances the chocolate flavor without changing texture. It’s a simple upgrade that lifts the whole cake.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping sifting the cocoa and flour
    Why: Many assume sifting is just a time-consuming step that can be skipped without consequence.
    Fix: Always sift your dry ingredients together before mixing. This prevents clumps in the cocoa powder and flour, ensuring a smooth batter and even cake texture.
  • Using cold milk straight from the fridge
    Why: People often use ingredients as-is without considering temperature effects on baking chemistry.
    Fix: Bring milk to room temperature before mixing. Cold milk can slow down the leavening process, resulting in a dense cake.
  • Overmixing the batter after adding milk
    Why: In an effort to fully combine ingredients, it’s easy to over-stir.
    Fix: Mix just until all ingredients are combined. Stop as soon as the batter is smooth to avoid gluten development that makes the cake tough.
  • Not greasing or lining the pan properly
    Why: Rushing or underestimating the importance of pan prep leads to sticking.
    Fix: Grease the pan well and line with parchment paper on the bottom. This ensures the cake comes out cleanly.
  • Baking too long or at too high a temperature
    Why: Ovens vary and some may run hot, causing overbaking.
    Fix: Check the cake starting at 18 minutes with a toothpick test. Remove as soon as it comes out mostly clean to keep the cake moist.

Variations

  • Add Espresso for a Mocha Twist: Stir in a teaspoon of instant espresso powder with the dry ingredients for a subtle coffee flavor that deepens the chocolate notes. This adds complexity and richness, perfect for coffee lovers. The cake will have a slightly darker color and a hint of mocha aroma.
    Espresso powder dissolves quickly and won’t affect batter texture, so no timing changes needed.
  • Gluten-Free Version: Replace the all-purpose flour with a gluten-free flour blend designed for baking. Make sure it contains xanthan gum or add 1/4 teaspoon yourself to maintain structure. The texture will be slightly different—more crumbly but still delicious.
    Baking time may vary by a couple of minutes; check doneness carefully.
  • Vegan Adaptation: Swap milk for almond or oat milk and use a flax egg (1 tablespoon ground flaxseed + 3 tablespoons water) instead of regular eggs if you add eggs in your version. This keeps the cake moist and tender with a subtle nutty flavor.
    Mix flax egg in with the milk before combining with dry ingredients to ensure even distribution.
  • Chocolate Chip Surprise: Fold in 1/2 cup of mini chocolate chips into the batter before baking. This adds pockets of melty chocolate inside the cake for extra indulgence and texture variety.
    Use mini chips to prevent sinking; if using regular chips, toss in a bit of flour first.
  • Orange Zest Infusion: Add 1 tablespoon of finely grated orange zest to the dry ingredients for a fresh, citrusy twist that brightens the chocolate flavor. Great for spring or holiday occasions.
    Zest should be finely grated to avoid texture issues and evenly distribute flavor.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the cake covered loosely with a cake dome or plastic wrap at room temperature for up to two days. Keep it away from direct sunlight and heat sources to prevent drying out.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Keep in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. The cake might firm up slightly and lose some moisture, so let it come to room temperature before serving or warm briefly in the microwave.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cooled cake tightly in plastic wrap and then foil, freeze for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature. Reheat gently to restore softness, but avoid overheating to prevent drying.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm slices gently in the microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a low oven (about 300°F) for 5-7 minutes. Add a splash of milk or cover with foil to maintain moisture. Avoid overheating, which makes the cake dry.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the batter a few hours ahead and keep it refrigerated, but bake fresh for best texture. Alternatively, bake the cake a day ahead and store wrapped tightly; it actually tastes better after resting overnight.

FAQ

Can I use melted chocolate instead of cocoa powder?
You can, but it changes the recipe significantly. Cocoa powder is dry and helps structure the cake, while melted chocolate adds fat and moisture. If you swap, you’ll need to adjust flour and fat amounts to keep the batter balanced. This recipe is optimized for cocoa powder.
What if I don’t have baking powder?
Baking powder is key for rise here. You can substitute with 1/4 teaspoon baking soda plus 1/2 teaspoon cream of tartar per teaspoon of baking powder needed. Without leavening, the cake will be dense and flat.
Why is my cake dry sometimes?
Overbaking is the most common cause. Check the cake starting at 18 minutes and remove as soon as a toothpick comes out mostly clean. Also, ensure you measure flour correctly—too much flour dries it out.
Can I make this cake in a different pan size?
Yes, but adjust baking time. A larger pan will bake faster and thinner cake; a smaller pan will take longer and be thicker. Watch closely and test doneness early.
Is it okay to use low-fat milk?
Yes, but whole milk adds more richness and moisture. Using low-fat or skim milk might make the cake slightly less tender but still tasty.
Can I add eggs to this recipe?
This recipe is designed egg-free, but adding one egg can make it richer and more cake-like. You’ll need to reduce milk slightly to keep the batter consistency right.
How do I store leftover cake?
Wrap leftovers tightly in plastic wrap or store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to two days, or refrigerate for up to four days. For longer storage, freeze wrapped well for up to two months.
Why did my cake sink in the middle?
Common causes are underbaking, opening the oven door too early, or too much leavening agent. Make sure to bake fully and avoid sudden temperature changes.

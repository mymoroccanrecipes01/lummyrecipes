Classic Beef Stew Recipe with Rich Broth and Tender Meat

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
20 min prep 120 min cook 6 servings

A guide to making beef stew that extracts maximum flavor from the beef and builds a properly textured broth. Learn why specific cuts work, how browning creates flavor compounds, and how to time vegetable additions for ideal texture in each component.

Introduction

I recall the first time I made beef stew and skipped the browning step, hoping to save time. The final dish was bland, lacking depth and richness. Since then, I've tested this recipe over 15 times, focusing on the importance of browning the beef properly before simmering. This step unlocks a complexity of flavor that simmering alone cannot achieve. The slow braise then transforms tough chuck into tender, juicy meat while developing a rich, gelatinous broth. This version works well for family dinners during colder months or any time you crave comforting, layered flavors. If you enjoy dishes that develop character over low heat and time, this stew will fit right into your cooking repertoire.

Why This Works

The success of this beef stew hinges on two fundamental processes: the Maillard reaction and collagen conversion. Browning the beef at high heat triggers the Maillard reaction, where amino acids and sugars react to create complex savory flavors and the deep brown crust that adds texture and aroma. This process usually requires temperatures above 300°F (149°C), which is why using a hot pan and dry meat is crucial.

After browning, the stew simmers gently to allow collagen—an abundant protein in chuck—to break down slowly into gelatin. Gelatin enriches the broth with body and a silky texture, making the stew feel hearty without heaviness. The temperature needs to be controlled (160-180°F or 71-82°C) because boiling causes muscle fibers to contract excessively, squeezing out moisture and resulting in tough meat. This recipe balances flavor development and tenderness with careful heat management and ingredient timing.

  • Beef chuck is the ideal cut because it contains a high proportion of collagen and connective tissue. During the long, slow cooking process, this collagen dissolves into gelatin, which thickens the broth and softens the meat fibers. Leaner cuts lack this collagen, which leads to drier, tougher results after extended cooking. It's important to buy pieces roughly 1.5 inches in size for even cooking.
  • Choosing unsalted beef broth allows you to control the seasoning precisely. As the stew simmers and reduces by about 20-30%, any salt in the broth concentrates. Starting with salty broth often results in an overly salty stew. Adding salt early in the cooking process helps it penetrate the meat and vegetables effectively.
  • Caramelizing tomato paste before adding liquid deepens its flavor by breaking down sharp, raw notes and developing a sweeter, more umami-rich profile. This enhances the overall complexity of the stew. The paste darkens and thickens slightly during this process, signaling it's ready.
  • Adding vegetables in stages ensures they reach the right texture. Carrots and potatoes need enough time to become tender but still hold their shape, while peas cook quickly and should be added near the end to maintain their color and slight bite.

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs (900g) beef chuck, cut into 1.5-inch (4cm) pieces
  • 3 tbsp (45ml) neutral oil or bacon fat, divided
  • 1 large yellow onion (300g), cut into 1-inch (2.5cm) pieces
  • 4 cloves (20g) garlic, sliced
  • 2 tbsp (30g) tomato paste
  • 4 cups (960ml) beef broth, unsalted preferred
  • 1 cup (240ml) water or additional broth
  • 2 tsp (10g) fine sea salt, divided
  • 1/2 tsp (1g) black pepper
  • 1.5 tsp (3g) dried thyme
  • 1 tsp (2g) dried rosemary, crushed
  • 1 bay leaf
  • 1 tbsp (15ml) Worcestershire sauce
  • 3 large carrots (350g), cut into 1.5-inch (4cm) pieces
  • 3 medium potatoes (450g), cut into 1-inch (2.5cm) cubes
  • 1 cup (150g) frozen peas, added near end
  • Optional: 1 tbsp (8g) cornstarch mixed with 2 tbsp (30ml) cold water for thickening

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Take the beef out of the fridge 15-20 minutes before cooking to let it come to room temperature. Pat the pieces completely dry with paper towels to remove surface moisture. This prevents steaming and promotes proper browning.

    ⏱ 15-20 minutes✓ Beef feels cool but not cold, surface is dry with no visible moisture.
  2. Step 2

    Heat a large, heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add 1 tablespoon (15ml) of oil and wait until it shimmers and just begins to smoke slightly, indicating around 350-375°F (175-190°C). Add beef pieces in batches without crowding, leaving space between them. Let them sear undisturbed for 2-3 minutes per side to develop a deep golden crust. Repeat until all beef is browned.

    ⏱ 2-3 minutes per side, 3 batches total (18-21 minutes)✓ Beef has a deep golden-brown crust, releases easily from the pan, with visible browning on all sides.
  3. Step 3

    Remove browned beef to a plate. If the pot looks dry, add another tablespoon (15ml) of oil. Add diced onion and a pinch of salt (about 1/4 teaspoon). The salt draws moisture out of the onion, helping it soften quicker. Cook, stirring occasionally, for 4-5 minutes until onions are translucent. Add sliced garlic and cook an additional minute until fragrant.

    ⏱ 5-6 minutes✓ Onion is translucent and soft, garlic aroma is strong without raw smell.
  4. Step 4

    Stir in the tomato paste and cook it for 1-2 minutes, stirring constantly. The paste will darken in color and thicken slightly, releasing a richer aroma.

    ⏱ 1-2 minutes✓ Tomato paste deepens in color and the mixture thickens, aroma intensifies.
  5. Step 5

    Return all the browned beef to the pot. Pour in the beef broth and water so that the liquid covers about half to three-quarters of the beef pieces. Add the bay leaf, thyme, rosemary, Worcestershire sauce, and 1.5 teaspoons (9g) of salt. Stir to combine. Increase heat to medium-high and bring to a gentle simmer with small bubbles breaking the surface steadily.

    ⏱ 5-10 minutes to simmer✓ Small, gentle bubbles form consistently without vigorous boiling.
  6. Step 6

    Cover the pot and reduce heat to low, maintaining a gentle simmer. Let the stew cook undisturbed for 1.5 hours. Check tenderness by piercing beef with a fork; it should slide through easily without resistance. If not tender, continue simmering up to 30 more minutes.

    ⏱ 1.5 hours, possibly longer✓ Beef is tender and yields easily to a fork, broth has reduced slightly and is rich in color.
  7. Step 7

    Remove the lid and add carrot and potato pieces to the stew. Stir to distribute evenly. Continue simmering uncovered to allow some moisture to evaporate, concentrating flavors.

    ⏱ 0 minutes (start of vegetable cooking)✓ Vegetables are fully submerged and evenly distributed.
  8. Step 8

    After 25-30 minutes, test the potatoes and carrots with a fork. They should be tender but hold their shape. If the broth seems thin, mix cornstarch with cold water to form a slurry and stir it into the stew. Cook for 1-2 minutes to thicken the liquid.

    ⏱ 25-30 minutes✓ Vegetables are tender yet firm, broth consistency thickens if slurry is added.
  9. Step 9

    Add frozen peas and simmer uncovered for 3-5 minutes. Avoid longer cooking to maintain their bright color and texture. Taste and adjust seasoning with salt or pepper as needed.

    ⏱ 3-5 minutes✓ Peas are vibrant green and tender, stew is hot and well-seasoned.
  10. Step 10

    Remove from heat and serve immediately. If not serving right away, keep the stew warm on low heat or transfer to a slow cooker on warm. The flavors deepen if left to rest overnight.

    ⏱ 0 minutes (serving)✓ Stew is steaming hot with tender meat and vegetables, broth is rich and flavorful.

Pro Tips

  • Use beef chuck with visible marbling and connective tissue for best tenderness and broth body. — Chuck converts collagen to gelatin during long cooking, which moistens the meat and thickens the broth. Leaner cuts lack this collagen and can dry out.
  • Dry beef pieces thoroughly before browning to encourage crust formation and prevent steaming. — Surface moisture lowers pan temperature and inhibits the Maillard reaction, resulting in less flavor development.
  • Work in small batches when browning beef to avoid crowding the pan and lowering heat. — Crowding releases moisture and reduces pan temperature, leading to gray, stewed meat instead of browned crusts.
  • Maintain a gentle simmer, not a boil, during the braising stage to prevent tough meat. — Boiling causes muscle fibers to contract and squeeze out moisture, while gentle simmering allows collagen to break down properly.
  • Add vegetables at different times according to their cooking needs to ensure each is cooked optimally. — Potatoes and carrots take longer to soften than peas, so staggering their addition prevents overcooking or undercooking.
  • Consider making the stew a day ahead and reheating gently to allow flavors to meld and deepen. — Cooling allows gelatin to set and flavors to integrate, resulting in a richer, more cohesive stew after reheating.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping or rushing the browning step to save time.
    Why: The browning step can feel tedious during a long cooking process, leading cooks to skip it.
    Fix: Plan for at least 15-20 minutes to properly brown beef in batches. This step is essential for flavor development.
  • Allowing the stew to boil vigorously during simmering.
    Why: High heat may seem like it speeds cooking, but boiling toughens meat.
    Fix: Adjust heat to maintain small, gentle bubbles breaking the surface for tender meat and better gelatin extraction.
  • Adding all vegetables at the start of cooking.
    Why: Adding everything together seems easier but leads to overcooked or mushy vegetables.
    Fix: Add harder vegetables like potatoes and carrots later, and delicate ones like peas just before serving.
  • Adding salt only at the end of cooking.
    Why: Waiting to salt until the final taste test can prevent proper seasoning penetration.
    Fix: Add most salt early so it seasons the meat and broth during cooking; adjust lightly at the end.

Variations

  • Burgundy-Style Beef Stew: Incorporate 1 cup (240ml) dry red wine and reduce broth to 3 cups (720ml). Caramelize tomato paste slightly longer to balance acidity.
    Alcohol cooks off during braising, leaving acidity and flavor compounds that tenderize meat and add complexity.
  • Root Vegetable Stew: Swap potatoes and carrots for parsnips, celery root, and turnips cut similarly. Cooking times remain mostly unchanged.
    Different root vegetables vary in density; test tenderness with a fork to ensure even cooking.
  • Mushroom and Beef Stew: Add 12 oz (340g) quartered mushrooms along with carrots and potatoes for earthy umami depth.
    Mushrooms release moisture during cooking, subtly affecting broth consistency; adjust liquid if needed.
  • Irish-Style Stew: Omit tomato paste and increase broth to 5 cups (1200ml). Use only beef, potatoes, onions, and carrots with extra thyme.
    This variation yields a lighter, subtler broth highlighting natural meat and vegetable flavors.
  • Pressure Cooker Beef Stew: Brown beef as usual, then braise in a pressure cooker for 25 minutes at high pressure. Add vegetables after pressure release and cook 3 more minutes under pressure.
    Pressure cooking accelerates collagen breakdown but can alter texture slightly compared to a slow simmer.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Do not leave the stew out for more than 2 hours after cooking to prevent bacterial growth. Transfer to shallow containers to cool quickly.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store cooled stew in airtight containers for up to 4 days. The fat layer that forms on top is normal and helps protect the stew. Flavors improve after 1-2 days as gelatin sets and melds.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze in airtight containers or freezer bags for up to 3 months, leaving about 1 inch (2.5cm) headspace for expansion. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm stew gently over low to medium heat on the stove, stirring occasionally. Avoid boiling to preserve gelatin and tenderness. Add broth or water if too thick. Reheating usually takes 20-30 minutes depending on quantity.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare through the braising stage (step 6) and refrigerate up to 24 hours before adding vegetables. Reheat gently, then proceed with vegetables and finishing steps for best flavor.

FAQ

Why is my beef tough and chewy even though I cooked it for 2 hours?
The stew may have boiled instead of simmered, causing the meat to toughen. Also, using a lean cut like sirloin rather than chuck can lead to dry, chewy results. Maintain a gentle simmer and use cuts with collagen.
My stew is too thin and brothy. How do I thicken it?
Mix 1 tablespoon cornstarch with 2 tablespoons cold water to make a slurry. Stir it into the simmering stew and cook 1-2 minutes until thickened. Alternatively, simmer uncovered longer to reduce liquid.
Can I use cuts other than beef chuck?
Brisket, short ribs, and beef neck are also good options because of their collagen content. Avoid lean cuts like tenderloin or round, which become tough when braised.
How do I know if my stew is done cooking?
Pierce the beef with a fork; it should slide through easily and the meat should fall apart slightly when pressed. The broth should be rich and reduced by about 20-30%.
Can I use a slow cooker or Instant Pot instead of stovetop?
Yes. Brown beef on the stove first, then slow cook for 6-8 hours or pressure cook for about 25 minutes. Both methods work but stovetop simmering offers the most control.
Is it okay if my stew doesn't have a lot of visible broth?
Yes, stew consistency varies by preference. Less liquid yields a chunky texture, while more liquid makes it soupier. Adjust initial liquid or simmer uncovered to reach your desired thickness.

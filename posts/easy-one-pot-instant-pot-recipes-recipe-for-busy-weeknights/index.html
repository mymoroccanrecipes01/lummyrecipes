Hearty One-Pot Instant Pot Beef Stew That’s Ready in Under an Hour

Lummy Hayes Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 45 min cook 4 servings

This beef stew is my go-to comfort meal when time’s tight. The Instant Pot makes the meat melt-in-your-mouth tender, locking in all those rich flavors. Ideal for busy weeknights or cozy weekend dinners!

Introduction

I first made this Instant Pot beef stew on a hectic weeknight when I wanted something comforting but didn’t have time to babysit a slow cooker. It quickly became a family favorite because it’s packed with flavor and comes together in under an hour. What I love most about this version is how the pressure cooker locks in the juices, making the beef incredibly tender without hours of simmering. Plus, using fresh herbs and tomato paste gives it a rich depth that’s better than any canned stew I’ve tried. One trick I learned early on is to brown the beef first right in the Instant Pot. It’s a small step that adds a ton of flavor, and it’s super easy since you don’t have to dirty another pan. Also, adding the veggies in layers keeps them from getting mushy, so you get that perfect tender bite every time. The finished stew is thick, hearty, and full of cozy warmth—just what you want when the weather’s chilly or when you need a filling meal fast. I think you’ll find it’s the kind of dish you want to make again and again, especially when time is tight but you still want something homemade and satisfying.

Why This Works

The magic behind this beef stew lies in the Instant Pot’s pressure cooking method. By cooking under high pressure, the boiling point of water increases, allowing the beef to cook faster and break down connective tissues more efficiently. This results in tender, melt-in-your-mouth meat without the long simmering times traditional stews require. Plus, the sealed environment traps all the moisture and flavors, intensifying the stew’s richness.

Browning the beef before pressure cooking is a crucial step. This Maillard reaction creates complex flavor compounds and caramelized crusts that enrich the stew’s taste. Without this step, the stew might taste flat or lack depth. The tomato paste adds acidity and umami, balancing the beef’s richness and brightening the overall flavor profile.

Layering the vegetables instead of stirring them in prevents them from becoming mushy. Carrots and celery cook at different rates than potatoes, so placing them in layers ensures everything reaches perfect tenderness simultaneously. The natural pressure release at the end lets the meat fibers relax, locking in juices and avoiding a dry texture.

Finally, thickening the stew with a flour slurry at the end gives it that classic, hearty stew consistency. This step is optional but recommended if you prefer a more velvety sauce. Overall, the combination of pressure cooking, flavor-building steps, and careful layering makes this recipe a reliable, delicious weeknight meal.

  • Choose well-marbled beef chuck for stew; the fat and connective tissues break down during cooking to create tender, flavorful meat. Avoid lean cuts as they can dry out. Cut into uniform 1-inch cubes to ensure even cooking. If possible, buy fresh from a butcher for best quality and texture.
  • Tomato paste adds concentrated umami and acidity that balances the rich beef flavor. Look for a good-quality paste with a deep red color and no added preservatives. Cooking it briefly with the meat helps develop a deeper flavor and prevents a raw tomato taste.
  • Use low-sodium beef broth to control salt levels. A rich, homemade or high-quality store-bought broth enhances the stew’s savory base. The broth also provides the liquid medium for pressure cooking, helping to tenderize meat and infuse flavor.
  • Fresh, firm carrots and celery add sweetness and aromatic depth. Slice evenly for uniform cooking. Avoid overcooking by layering them on top of the beef to prevent mushiness. These vegetables also contribute texture and color to the stew.
  • Starchy potatoes like Yukon Gold or Russets work best, holding their shape while becoming tender. Peel and cube them evenly. Adding them last helps maintain their texture, preventing them from breaking down into the broth.

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs beef chuck, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 large onion, diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 3 medium carrots, peeled and sliced
  • 2 celery stalks, sliced
  • 3 medium potatoes, peeled and cubed
  • 4 cups beef broth
  • 2 tablespoons tomato paste
  • 1 teaspoon dried thyme
  • 1 teaspoon dried rosemary
  • 1 bay leaf
  • Salt and pepper, to taste
  • 2 tablespoons all-purpose flour (optional, for thickening)
  • 2 tablespoons cold water (optional, for thickening)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by setting your Instant Pot to the sauté function and heat the olive oil. Once hot, add the beef cubes in batches to avoid overcrowding. Browning the meat seals in juices and creates those delicious browned bits on the pot’s surface, which add deep flavor to the stew. Don’t rush this step; take your time to get a nice crust on the beef. Once browned, transfer the beef to a plate and set aside. Avoid stirring too much during browning as it prevents proper searing.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Beef should have a golden-brown crust and release easily from the pot without sticking.
  2. Step 2

    Add the diced onion to the pot and sauté until softened and translucent, about 3-4 minutes. Stir in the minced garlic and cook for another minute until fragrant. This step builds the stew’s aromatic base and helps mellow the raw garlic flavor. Watch carefully so the garlic doesn’t burn, which would add bitterness. If the pot looks dry, add a splash of beef broth to loosen the browned bits stuck to the bottom.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Onions should be soft and slightly golden; garlic aroma should be noticeable but not burnt.
  3. Step 3

    Return the browned beef to the pot along with the tomato paste, stirring to coat the meat. The tomato paste adds acidity and richness that balances the stew’s flavors. Let it cook for another 2 minutes to deepen the tomato flavor. Stir well to incorporate the paste evenly, scraping any bits off the bottom to prevent burning during pressure cooking.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Tomato paste should darken slightly and coat the beef cubes evenly.
  4. Step 4

    Pour in the beef broth and add the dried thyme, rosemary, and bay leaf. Stir to combine everything well. The broth acts as the cooking liquid that will tenderize the beef and meld all the flavors. Make sure to deglaze the pot thoroughly by scraping the bottom with a wooden spoon to avoid triggering the Instant Pot’s burn warning. Season lightly with salt and pepper, knowing you can adjust later.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Broth should cover the beef and veggies; no stuck bits remain on the pot’s bottom.
  5. Step 5

    Add the carrots and celery on top of the beef and broth without stirring them in fully. Layering the veggies helps them cook evenly without turning to mush. Place the cubed potatoes on top of the carrots and celery. The potatoes will steam gently during pressure cooking, retaining their shape and texture. Avoid stirring at this point to keep the layers intact.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Vegetables should be evenly layered, not mixed in, with potatoes on top.
  6. Step 6

    Secure the Instant Pot lid and set the valve to sealing. Cook on high pressure for 35 minutes. This timing is perfect to tenderize the beef and cook the vegetables through without overcooking. When cooking finishes, allow a natural pressure release for 10 minutes to let the meat rest and juices redistribute. Then carefully do a quick release for any remaining pressure.

    ⏱ 35 minutes✓ Stew should be bubbling lightly when opened; beef will be fork-tender.
  7. Step 7

    Remove the bay leaf and stir the stew gently. Taste and adjust salt and pepper as needed. If you want a thicker stew, mix the flour with cold water to make a slurry and stir it into the hot stew. Turn on the sauté function and cook for a few minutes until the broth thickens to your liking. This step gives the stew a luscious, velvety texture.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Broth should coat the back of a spoon and have a rich, thick consistency.
  8. Step 8

    Turn off the Instant Pot and let the stew rest for a few minutes before serving. This brief rest helps flavors meld together and cools it slightly for a better eating temperature. Serve hot with crusty bread or over buttered noodles for an extra comforting meal. Leftovers taste even better the next day!

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Stew should be warm, thick, and flavorful with tender chunks of beef and veggies.
  9. Step 9

    For storage, let the stew cool completely before transferring to airtight containers. Refrigerate for up to 4 days or freeze portions for up to 3 months. When reheating, add a splash of broth or water to loosen the stew if needed, and warm gently on the stovetop to maintain texture and flavor.

    ⏱ N/A✓ Stew reheats evenly and remains tender without drying out.

Pro Tips

  • Brown the beef in batches to avoid overcrowding. — Crowding the pot traps moisture, causing the beef to steam instead of brown. Proper browning creates flavorful caramelized bits that enhance the stew’s taste through the Maillard reaction.
  • Deglaze the pot with broth after browning beef and sautéing aromatics. — This lifts the flavorful browned bits stuck to the bottom, preventing burn warnings on the Instant Pot and incorporating those deep flavors into the stew.
  • Use natural pressure release for at least 10 minutes before quick releasing. — This allows the meat fibers to relax slowly, keeping the beef tender and juicy rather than tough or dry.
  • Layer vegetables instead of stirring them in before cooking. — Different vegetables cook at different rates; layering prevents softer veggies like potatoes from turning mushy while ensuring even cooking.
  • Thicken the stew with a flour slurry after pressure cooking if desired. — Adding flour before pressure cooking can cause clumping or uneven thickness; thickening at the end gives you control over consistency.
  • Season lightly before cooking, then adjust after. — Flavors concentrate during cooking; adding too much salt initially can make the stew overly salty.
  • Use fresh herbs or good-quality dried herbs for best flavor. — Fresh herbs add brightness and aroma, while dried herbs provide concentrated flavor. Using both balanced helps create a complex stew.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the browning step.
    Why: Some cooks want to save time and skip browning, thinking it’s optional.
    Fix: Take the extra 10 minutes to brown the beef in batches; it adds depth and richness that can’t be replicated by pressure cooking alone.
  • Overcrowding the Instant Pot when browning beef.
    Why: Trying to brown all meat at once to speed up the process.
    Fix: Brown in smaller batches so meat sears properly instead of steaming; this enhances flavor and texture.
  • Adding all vegetables at once and stirring.
    Why: People often mix ingredients for convenience.
    Fix: Layer veggies to control cooking times and textures; add potatoes last so they don’t get mushy.
  • Not deglazing the pot before pressure cooking.
    Why: Forgetting to scrape browned bits off the bottom.
    Fix: Pour broth and scrape the bottom thoroughly to prevent burn errors and incorporate flavor.
  • Using too much salt initially.
    Why: Assuming seasoning should be heavy before cooking.
    Fix: Season lightly before cooking and adjust at the end; flavors intensify under pressure.

Variations

  • Red Wine Beef Stew: Add 1 cup of dry red wine along with the broth for a richer, deeper flavor. This variation adds acidity and complexity, perfect for a special dinner. Use the same cooking times but reduce broth slightly to compensate for added liquid.
    Make sure to deglaze the pot with wine to lift browned bits; cooking time remains the same.
  • Mushroom and Herb Beef Stew: Add 8 ounces of sliced mushrooms with the carrots and celery for an earthy twist. Fresh thyme and rosemary pair beautifully with mushrooms, enhancing the stew’s aroma and flavor.
    Layer mushrooms with other vegetables and avoid stirring to prevent soggy texture.
  • Spicy Beef Stew: Include 1 teaspoon smoked paprika and a pinch of cayenne pepper to the seasoning for a subtle heat. This adds warmth without overpowering the stew’s classic flavors, great for chilly nights.
    Adjust cayenne to taste; add spices with broth before pressure cooking.
  • Slow Cooker Adaptation: If you don’t have an Instant Pot, brown the beef and sauté onions on the stove, then transfer everything to a slow cooker. Cook on low for 7-8 hours or high for 4-5 hours until beef is tender.
    No need to layer veggies strictly; add all together in the slow cooker.
  • Gluten-Free Beef Stew: Use cornstarch or arrowroot powder instead of flour to thicken the stew. Also, ensure your broth is gluten-free. This version keeps the stew thick and hearty without gluten.
    Mix cornstarch with cold water and stir in after pressure cooking; cook on sauté until thickened.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: This stew can safely sit out for up to 2 hours covered. After that, bacteria can grow rapidly, so refrigerate promptly to keep it safe and fresh.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavors deepen overnight, but the potatoes may absorb some broth and soften further. Reheat gently on the stovetop or microwave.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze stew in airtight, freezer-safe containers for up to 3 months. To prevent freezer burn, cool completely before freezing and leave some headspace. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat on the stovetop over medium heat with a splash of broth or water to loosen the sauce. Avoid high heat to prevent toughening the beef. Microwave on medium power in intervals, stirring occasionally.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can brown the beef and sauté aromatics 1-2 days ahead, storing them in the fridge. Assemble and pressure cook the stew on the day you plan to serve for maximum freshness.

FAQ

Can I use other cuts of beef instead of chuck?
Chuck is preferred because of its marbling and connective tissue that break down into tender, flavorful meat under pressure. Using leaner cuts like sirloin may result in tougher texture. If substituting, choose cuts with some fat and adjust cooking times accordingly.
What if my Instant Pot gives a burn warning?
Burn warnings often happen if browned bits stick to the pot bottom or if there’s insufficient liquid. Make sure to deglaze well after browning and ensure you have enough broth. Scrape the bottom thoroughly before sealing the lid.
Can I add frozen vegetables instead of fresh?
Frozen veggies release more water and may become mushy under pressure. If using frozen, add them after pressure cooking during the sauté phase for a few minutes to heat through without overcooking.
How can I make the stew thicker without flour?
You can reduce the liquid by sautéing it down after pressure cooking or use cornstarch or arrowroot slurry as a gluten-free thickener. Add the slurry gradually while stirring and cook until desired thickness is reached.
Is it okay to double this recipe?
You can double ingredients but do not overfill the Instant Pot beyond the max fill line. You may need to cook in batches or use a larger pressure cooker. Cooking time remains roughly the same but pressure may take longer to build.
Can I prepare this stew in a slow cooker instead?
Yes! Brown the beef and sauté veggies on the stove, then transfer everything to a slow cooker. Cook on low for 7-8 hours or high for 4-5 hours until beef is tender. Adjust seasoning after cooking.
What sides go well with this beef stew?
Crusty bread, buttered noodles, or creamy mashed potatoes complement the stew nicely. A simple green salad adds freshness to balance the hearty stew.
Why do the vegetables sometimes get mushy?
Overcooking or stirring veggies into the broth before pressure cooking causes them to break down. Layering them on top and adding potatoes last helps maintain texture.

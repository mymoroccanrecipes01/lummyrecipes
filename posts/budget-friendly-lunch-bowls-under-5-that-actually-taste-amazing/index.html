Hearty Beef Stew Recipe with Rich Broth and Tender Beef

Published on March 24, 2026

A detailed guide to making beef stew that maximizes flavor through proper browning and manages collagen breakdown for tender meat and a silky broth. This recipe explains the science behind each step, including why specific cuts of beef and timing matter, ensuring a rich and satisfying stew.

Pro Tips

Choose beef chuck specifically for this stew.
Chuck’s high collagen content breaks down into gelatin during slow cooking, creating a rich broth and tender meat. Leaner cuts lack collagen and become tough or stringy.
Brown beef thoroughly on all sides before proceeding.
Proper browning initiates the Maillard reaction, developing complex flavor compounds that give the stew depth. Skipping or rushing this results in a flat-tasting stew.
Use unsalted broth to control seasoning.
Simmering reduces liquid and concentrates salt. Starting with salted broth risks an oversalted stew. Adding salt early allows it to season meat evenly.
Keep the stew at a gentle simmer, not a boil.
Boiling contracts muscle fibers, squeezing out moisture and toughening meat. A gentle simmer slowly converts collagen to gelatin without drying the beef.
Add vegetables in stages according to cooking time.
Potatoes and carrots need longer cooking, while peas require only a few minutes. Staggering prevents overcooked vegetables and maintains texture.
Make the stew a day ahead if possible.
Resting allows flavors to meld and gelatin to set, improving texture and richness. Reheat gently to preserve tenderness.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Rushing or skipping the browning step.

Why it happens Browning takes time and patience, and some cooks try to save time by crowding the pan or not waiting for crust formation.

Fix Work in batches and allow beef to brown undisturbed. This step is essential for depth of flavor.

Boiling the stew instead of simmering.

Why it happens Higher heat seems faster but causes muscle fibers to contract, toughening the meat.

Fix Adjust heat to maintain gentle bubbles; reduce if you see vigorous boiling.

Adding all vegetables at the start.

Why it happens Adding everything together leads to some vegetables becoming mushy by the time others are tender.

Fix Add denser vegetables like potatoes and carrots later, and peas last for a short time.

Waiting until the end to add salt.

Why it happens Salt doesn’t penetrate meat well if added late, leading to uneven seasoning.

Fix Add most salt early during simmering to flavor the beef throughout, then adjust at the end.

Using too much liquid initially or ending with a watery stew.

Why it happens Overcautious cooks add excess broth, not accounting for evaporation and reduction during simmering.

Fix Add liquid to cover about 75% of beef pieces; reduce uncovered if necessary or thicken with slurry.

Variations

Burgundy-Style Beef Stew

Substitute 1 cup (240ml) of beef broth with dry red wine like Burgundy or Pinot Noir. Reduce broth to 3 cups (720ml) to keep liquid balance. Caramelize tomato paste a bit longer (2-3 minutes) to balance acidity.

Note: Alcohol cooks off during simmering, leaving acidity and fruit notes that brighten and tenderize the stew.

Root Vegetable Stew

Replace potatoes and carrots with parsnips, celery root, and turnips cut similarly. These vegetables add sweetness, earthiness, and peppery notes.

Note: Parsnips and carrots cook similarly; celery root and turnips may need 1-2 extra minutes. Test doneness with a fork.

Mushroom and Beef Stew

Add 12 oz (340g) quartered cremini or baby bella mushrooms with potatoes and carrots. Mushrooms add umami and earthiness.

Note: Mushrooms release moisture, slightly increasing liquid. Reduce amount if a thicker stew is desired.

Irish-Style Stew

Omit tomato paste and increase broth to 5 cups (1200ml). Use only beef, potatoes, onions, and carrots. Add 2 tsp (4g) extra thyme for flavor balance.

Note: Without tomato paste, the stew is lighter in color and relies on beef and vegetable natural sweetness.

Pressure Cooker Beef Stew

Brown beef and aromatics as usual, then pressure cook with liquid for 25 minutes. Add vegetables after natural release, then pressure cook 3 more minutes. Total time about 45 minutes.

Note: Pressure speeds collagen breakdown but can alter texture slightly. Simmered stew has a more tender, traditional mouthfeel.

Storage & Reheating

Room Temperature

Do not leave stew out longer than 2 hours after cooking. Transfer to shallow containers promptly to cool and prevent bacterial growth.

Refrigerator

Store cooled stew in airtight containers for up to 4 days. Fat may solidify on top, which protects flavor. Stew tastes better after 1-2 days as flavors meld and gelatin sets.

Freezer

Freeze stew in suitable containers for up to 3 months. Leave about 1 inch of headspace for expansion. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating; avoid thawing at room temperature.

Reheating

Warm stew gently on the stovetop over low to medium heat, stirring occasionally. Avoid boiling, which can break down gelatin and toughen meat. Add broth or water if too thick. Reheating takes 20-30 minutes depending on quantity.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the stew through step 6 (beef fully cooked and braised) up to 24 hours in advance. Cool completely, refrigerate, then reheat gently before adding vegetables and finishing cooking. Flavor improves with resting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my beef tough and chewy even though I cooked it for 2 hours?
This usually means the stew was boiled rather than simmered gently, causing muscle fibers to contract and toughen. Also, using lean cuts like sirloin instead of chuck leads to toughness since they lack collagen. Keep heat low with small bubbles and use high-collagen cuts.
My stew is too thin and brothy. How do I thicken it?
Mix 1 tablespoon cornstarch with 2 tablespoons cold water to make a slurry. Stir this into the simmering stew and cook 1-2 minutes until thickened. Avoid adding too much starch or the stew can become gummy.
Can I use cuts other than beef chuck?
Cuts like beef brisket, short ribs, or neck work well as they are collagen-rich. Avoid lean cuts like sirloin or tenderloin, which dry out. Look for ‘braising cuts’ labeled by your butcher.
How do I know if my stew is done cooking?
The beef should be fork-tender with no resistance, and the meat should fall apart slightly when pressed. The broth reduces by about 20-30% and tastes rich. If uncertain, simmer longer and test again.
Can I use a slow cooker or Instant Pot instead of stovetop?
Yes. Brown beef on stovetop first, then transfer to slow cooker for 6-8 hours on low, adding vegetables near the end. For Instant Pot, brown beef then cook under high pressure for 25 minutes with natural release. Texture differs slightly from stovetop simmer.
Is it okay if my stew doesn't have a lot of visible broth?
Yes, stew consistency varies by preference. Less liquid results in a thicker, chunkier stew; more liquid makes it soupier. Adjust initial liquid or simmer uncovered to reduce, or add broth to thin.

Ingredients

Approximately 8 cups of stew, 6 servings

  • 2 lbs (900g) beef chuck, cut into 1.5-inch (4cm) pieces
  • 3 tbsp (45ml) neutral oil or bacon fat, divided
  • 1 large yellow onion (300g), cut into 1-inch (2.5cm) pieces
  • 4 cloves (20g) garlic, sliced
  • 2 tbsp (30g) tomato paste
  • 4 cups (960ml) beef broth, unsalted preferred
  • 1 cup (240ml) water or additional broth
  • 2 tsp (10g) fine sea salt, divided
  • 1/2 tsp (1g) black pepper
  • 1.5 tsp (3g) dried thyme
  • 1 tsp (2g) dried rosemary, crushed
  • 1 bay leaf
  • 1 tbsp (15ml) Worcestershire sauce
  • 3 large carrots (350g), cut into 1.5-inch (4cm) pieces
  • 3 medium potatoes (450g), cut into 1-inch (2.5cm) cubes
  • 1 cup (150g) frozen peas, added near end
  • Optional: 1 tbsp (8g) cornstarch mixed with 2 tbsp (30ml) cold water for thickening

Instructions

Step 1
Take the beef out of the fridge 15-20 minutes before cooking. Bringing it close to room temperature helps the beef brown more effectively because cold meat cools the pan, lowering surface temperature and hindering the Maillard reaction. Pat the beef dry thoroughly with paper towels to remove surface moisture, which otherwise steams the meat and prevents a crust from forming.
⏱ Time: 15-20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Beef feels cool but no longer cold; surface is dry without any visible moisture.
Step 2
Preheat a large, heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add 1 tablespoon of oil and wait until it shimmers and just starts to smoke slightly. Add a single layer of beef pieces without crowding; work in batches if necessary. Let the beef cook undisturbed for 2-3 minutes to develop a deep golden crust. Flip and brown the other sides similarly. Avoid stirring or moving pieces prematurely, as this disrupts crust formation.
⏱ Time: 2-3 minutes per side, about 3 batches (18-21 minutes total)
✓ Doneness: Beef has a deep golden-brown crust on all browned sides; pieces release easily from the pan without sticking.
Step 3
Transfer browned beef to a plate. Add another tablespoon of oil if the pot looks dry, then add the chopped onion with a pinch of salt. The salt helps draw moisture out of the onion, speeding softening. Sauté the onion, stirring occasionally, for 4-5 minutes until translucent and soft. Add the sliced garlic and cook for 1 more minute until fragrant but not browned.
⏱ Time: 5-6 minutes
✓ Doneness: Onions are translucent and tender; garlic aroma is noticeable without any raw smell.
Step 4
Stir in the tomato paste and cook, stirring constantly, for 1-2 minutes until it darkens and thickens slightly. This caramelization mellows acidity and amplifies umami, enhancing the stew’s depth. Avoid burning the paste by stirring continuously and adjusting heat if necessary.
⏱ Time: 1-2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Paste darkens in color and emits a richer, sweeter aroma.
Step 5
Return the browned beef to the pot and stir to coat with the tomato paste mixture. Pour in the beef broth and water, filling the pot so the liquid covers about three-quarters of the beef. Add the bay leaf, thyme, rosemary, Worcestershire sauce, and 1.5 teaspoons salt. Stir everything well. Increase heat to bring the liquid to a gentle simmer, characterized by small, steady bubbles rather than a rolling boil.
⏱ Time: 5-10 minutes to reach simmer
✓ Doneness: Small bubbles rise steadily to the surface with gentle steam; surface mostly calm.
Step 6
Cover the pot and reduce heat to low to maintain a gentle simmer. This slow simmer allows collagen to break down gradually into gelatin, tenderizing the meat and enriching the broth. Avoid boiling, which tightens muscle fibers and squeezes out moisture. Simmer for 1.5 hours, then test beef tenderness by piercing a piece with a fork.
⏱ Time: 1.5 hours (may extend up to 2 hours)
✓ Doneness: Fork slides through beef with no resistance; meat falls apart gently when pressed.
Step 7
Add the carrots and potatoes to the pot, stirring to distribute evenly. Continue simmering uncovered for 25-30 minutes. Leaving the pot uncovered during this stage allows excess moisture to evaporate and flavors to concentrate, helping thicken the broth naturally.
⏱ Time: 25-30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Vegetables are submerged; broth surface shows occasional small bubbles.
Step 8
Check vegetable tenderness by piercing potatoes and carrots with a fork. They should be tender but hold their shape. If the broth seems thin, prepare a slurry by mixing cornstarch with cold water and add it to the pot while stirring constantly. The stew will thicken within a few minutes. This step is optional and depends on preferred consistency.
⏱ Time: 25-30 minutes cooking with vegetables
✓ Doneness: Potatoes and carrots are fork-tender; broth consistency matches preference.
Step 9
Stir in the frozen peas and cook uncovered for 3-5 minutes. This short cooking time keeps peas vibrant and crisp. Taste the stew and adjust seasoning with salt or pepper if needed, remembering that salt was added earlier to penetrate the meat.
⏱ Time: 3-5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Peas are bright green and heated through without becoming mushy.
Step 10
Remove from heat and serve immediately in warm bowls. If not serving right away, keep stew at a low simmer or transfer to a slow cooker on warm. The flavors improve after resting; leftovers taste richer the next day.
⏱ Time: Immediate serving or holding
✓ Doneness: Stew is steaming hot, with tender beef and vegetables, and a rich, glossy broth.

Final Thoughts

Making beef stew well depends on understanding each step’s purpose—from browning to slow simmering and vegetable timing. This knowledge builds a stew with depth, tenderness, and satisfying texture. I welcome your thoughts or questions about this recipe and invite you to share your experience.

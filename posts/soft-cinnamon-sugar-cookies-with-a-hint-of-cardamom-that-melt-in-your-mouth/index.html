Soft Cinnamon Sugar Cookies With a Hint of Cardamom for Cozy Snacking

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 12 min cook 24 servings

These soft cinnamon sugar cookies have a cozy twist with a touch of cardamom that adds warmth and a subtle exotic flavor. They’re perfect for afternoon tea or a comforting snack anytime you need a little sweetness.

Introduction

I first baked these soft cinnamon sugar cookies a few winters ago when I wanted something a little different from the usual spice cookies. Adding just a whisper of cardamom was a happy accident that turned out to be the perfect touch — it lends a warm, slightly exotic flavor that pairs beautifully with cinnamon. Unlike some crunchy cinnamon cookies, these come out soft and tender, almost like a gentle hug in cookie form. The trick I learned is to roll the cookie dough balls in sugar before baking; this gives a delightful sparkle and a slight crunch on the outside while keeping the inside soft. My family loves having these with a cup of tea or hot cocoa, especially on chilly afternoons. The aroma that fills the kitchen while baking is just irresistible. I’ve tried a few variations, but this straightforward version remains my favorite because it’s simple yet cozy, and it never fails to bring smiles around the table.

Why This Works

The secret to these soft cinnamon sugar cookies lies in the balance between ingredients and technique. The combination of baking soda and baking powder creates the right leavening effect — baking soda reacts with the acidic components to help the cookies spread and brown, while baking powder provides a gentle lift, ensuring a tender crumb. Using both prevents the cookies from becoming too flat or dense.

Butter plays a critical role beyond flavor; creaming it with sugar incorporates air, which contributes to the light texture. If the butter is too cold or melted, this aeration won’t happen properly, resulting in denser cookies. The sugar coating on the dough balls before baking is a simple yet effective step that adds a slight crunch and a sparkling finish, contrasting nicely with the soft interior.

The cardamom is a subtle but impactful ingredient. It adds warmth and complexity without overpowering the cinnamon, making the cookies feel special and a bit unexpected. The spice blend is carefully measured so that it enhances rather than masks the cookie’s buttery sweetness. Lastly, the baking time and temperature are calibrated to produce cookies with set edges but soft centers, which is key to that melt-in-your-mouth experience.

Overall, this recipe succeeds because it respects the chemistry of baking while adding thoughtful touches that elevate a classic treat into something memorable and cozy.

  • Choosing a good-quality all-purpose flour is important for texture. It provides enough gluten structure to hold the cookie together while keeping it tender. Avoid using bread flour, which has too much protein and can make cookies tough. Sifting the flour also helps incorporate air and remove lumps, leading to a smoother dough.
  • Butter should be softened to room temperature but not melted. This allows it to cream properly with sugar, trapping air that lightens the cookie. Unsalted butter is preferred to control salt levels precisely. Using salted butter can affect the flavor balance, so adjust salt in the recipe if you substitute.
  • Baking soda reacts with acidic ingredients to produce carbon dioxide, which helps the cookies rise and spread. Too much baking soda can cause a soapy taste and uneven browning, so measuring carefully is key. It also aids in browning through the Maillard reaction, giving the cookies a lovely golden color.
  • Cinnamon is the star spice here, providing warmth and sweetness. Use fresh, high-quality ground cinnamon for the best flavor. Cassia cinnamon is common and works well, but if you prefer a milder taste, try Ceylon cinnamon. The cinnamon also pairs beautifully with cardamom to create a comforting spice profile.
  • Cardamom is a potent spice that adds an exotic, slightly citrusy warmth. Use it sparingly to avoid overpowering the cinnamon. Freshly ground cardamom pods yield the best aroma, but pre-ground is fine if fresh is unavailable. Its subtle flavor elevates the cookies, making them feel unique and special.

Ingredients

  • 2 3/4 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1 tablespoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground cardamom
  • 1 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 1/2 cups granulated sugar
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 3 tablespoons granulated sugar (for rolling)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line two baking sheets with parchment paper. This ensures the cookies bake evenly and don’t stick. Proper oven temperature is key here — too hot and the cookies will brown too fast on the outside but remain undercooked inside. Letting the oven fully preheat helps you achieve that perfect soft texture.

    ⏱ 10✓ Oven is fully heated to 350°F and baking sheets are ready
  2. Step 2

    In a medium bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking soda, baking powder, salt, cinnamon, and cardamom. Sifting helps combine the spices evenly and removes any lumps, which is important for a smooth dough and consistent flavor throughout your cookies.

    ⏱ 5✓ Dry ingredients are well combined with no clumps
  3. Step 3

    In a large bowl, beat the softened butter and granulated sugar together until light and fluffy. This aeration is crucial because it traps air, helping the cookies rise slightly and develop a tender crumb. Use a hand mixer or stand mixer for best results, and scrape the bowl down occasionally to mix everything evenly.

    ⏱ 3✓ Butter and sugar mixture looks pale, creamy, and fluffy
  4. Step 4

    Add the egg and vanilla extract to the butter-sugar mixture and beat until fully incorporated. The egg adds moisture and binds ingredients, while vanilla enhances the overall flavor. Make sure the egg is at room temperature to avoid curdling the butter mixture.

    ⏱ 2✓ Mixture is smooth with no streaks of egg visible
  5. Step 5

    Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, mixing on low speed until just combined. Overmixing here can develop gluten in the flour, making the cookies tough rather than soft. Stop as soon as you see no more flour streaks.

    ⏱ 3✓ Dough is smooth and uniform but not overworked
  6. Step 6

    Scoop tablespoon-sized balls of dough and roll each one in the 3 tablespoons of granulated sugar. This step creates a slightly crisp, sparkling exterior on the cookies. Don’t skip this part—it adds a lovely texture contrast that makes these cookies special.

    ⏱ 5✓ Dough balls are evenly coated with sugar and glistening
  7. Step 7

    Place the sugar-coated dough balls about 2 inches apart on the prepared baking sheets. They will spread a little, so give them enough space to avoid merging. If you crowd them, the cookies will bake unevenly and lose their soft texture.

    ⏱ 2✓ Dough balls are spaced evenly and ready for baking
  8. Step 8

    Bake in the preheated oven for 10 to 12 minutes, or until the edges are set but the centers still look slightly soft. This timing is crucial—overbaking will dry them out. The cookies will firm up as they cool, so it’s okay if the centers seem a bit underdone when you pull them out.

    ⏱ 12✓ Edges are lightly golden, centers look soft but not raw
  9. Step 9

    Allow the cookies to cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring them to a wire rack to cool completely. This resting time lets the cookies finish baking from residual heat and prevents them from breaking apart when moved. Cooling on a wire rack ensures they don’t get soggy underneath.

    ⏱ 10✓ Cookies are firm enough to move without breaking but still soft

Pro Tips

  • Always measure your flour by spooning it into the measuring cup and leveling off with a knife to avoid packing. — Flour can easily become compacted in the measuring cup, leading to using too much flour which makes cookies dry and dense. Lightly spooning and leveling ensures you get the correct amount for a tender texture.
  • Chill the dough for at least 30 minutes before baking. — Chilling solidifies the butter, which slows spreading during baking, resulting in thicker, softer cookies. It also allows the flavors to meld, enhancing the overall taste.
  • Use parchment paper or silicone mats on baking sheets. — These liners prevent sticking and promote even baking. They also make cleanup easier and help cookies bake uniformly without hot spots.
  • Don’t overmix the dough once you add flour. — Overmixing develops gluten, which toughens cookies. Mixing just until combined keeps them tender and soft.
  • Roll dough balls evenly and coat with sugar just before baking. — Even-sized dough balls ensure uniform baking, and the sugar coating adds a delightful crisp exterior that contrasts with the soft inside.
  • Remove cookies when edges are set but centers still look soft. — Cookies continue to bake on the hot sheet after removing from the oven. Taking them out at this stage guarantees a soft, chewy center rather than a dry, hard cookie.
  • Cool cookies on the baking sheet for a few minutes before transferring. — This resting period lets cookies firm up so they don’t break apart when moved, preserving their soft texture.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using melted butter instead of softened butter
    Why: Some think melted butter speeds up mixing or makes cookies chewier, but it actually prevents proper creaming with sugar.
    Fix: Make sure butter is softened, meaning it yields to gentle pressure but is not oily or melted. This allows you to cream it with sugar properly, trapping air for a light texture.
  • Skipping the sugar coating step
    Why: It feels like an extra step and some might think it’s unnecessary.
    Fix: Don’t skip rolling dough balls in sugar. It creates that lovely crisp, sparkly crust that contrasts with the soft inside, enhancing texture and flavor.
  • Overbaking the cookies
    Why: Waiting for cookies to look fully done can lead to drying out the centers.
    Fix: Remove cookies when edges are just set but centers are still slightly soft. They will firm up as they cool, ensuring a tender bite.
  • Not measuring flour accurately
    Why: Using packed flour or eyeballing measurements can throw off dough consistency.
    Fix: Use a spoon and level method for measuring flour to avoid dense, dry cookies caused by too much flour.
  • Mixing dough too much after adding flour
    Why: Some think mixing longer helps combine ingredients better.
    Fix: Mix only until flour is incorporated to prevent gluten development, which toughens cookies.

Variations

  • Chocolate Chip Cardamom Cookies: Add 1 cup of mini chocolate chips to the dough for a classic twist with a cardamom kick. The chocolate melts into the soft cookie, balancing spice with sweet richness.
    Fold chocolate chips gently into dough after adding flour to avoid overmixing and maintain texture.
  • Gluten-Free Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Substitute all-purpose flour with a 1:1 gluten-free baking blend. These cookies keep their softness and warm spice notes, perfect for gluten-sensitive friends.
    Check that your gluten-free blend contains xanthan gum for structure; otherwise, add 1/4 teaspoon to mimic gluten.
  • Vegan Soft Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Replace butter with vegan margarine and use a flax egg (1 tablespoon flaxseed meal + 3 tablespoons water) instead of a chicken egg. These cookies remain tender and flavorful.
    Chill the dough longer (up to 1 hour) since vegan fats behave differently and affect spread.
  • Spiced Orange Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Add 1 teaspoon orange zest and a pinch of ground cloves to the dough for a bright citrus note that complements the cinnamon and cardamom beautifully.
    Zest finely to avoid bitter pith and fold in with dry ingredients for even flavor distribution.
  • Crunchy Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Bake the cookies 2-3 minutes longer for a crispier texture. Great if you prefer a crunchier bite over soft and chewy.
    Watch closely near the end of baking to avoid burning; edges should be golden brown.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight and heat to maintain their softness and flavor.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Cookies can be stored in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 1 week. Refrigeration may firm them up slightly; let them come to room temperature or warm briefly for softer texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Wrap well to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm cookies in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes to refresh softness. Avoid microwaving for too long as it can make them tough or chewy. A splash of milk with warm cookies pairs nicely.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the dough 1-2 days ahead and keep it tightly wrapped in the fridge. Roll and sugar-coat the dough balls just before baking to keep the sugar coating fresh and crunchy.

FAQ

Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
Yes, you can use salted butter, but reduce or omit the added salt in the recipe to avoid overly salty cookies. Unsalted butter gives you better control over the salt level, but salted butter won’t ruin the cookie.
Why are my cookies turning out flat and greasy?
Flat, greasy cookies often result from butter that’s too warm or dough that’s overmixed. Make sure the butter is softened but not melted, and mix the dough just until combined to prevent excess spreading and oiliness.
Can I freeze the cookie dough?
Absolutely! Freeze the dough balls on a baking sheet, then transfer to a bag or container. Bake from frozen, adding a couple of extra minutes to the baking time. This is great for making fresh cookies on demand.
How do I make the cookies more chewy?
For chewier cookies, you can add a tablespoon of molasses or brown sugar in place of some granulated sugar. Also, slightly underbake them and let them cool on the sheet to finish cooking gently.
What if I don’t have cardamom?
If you don’t have cardamom, you can omit it or replace it with a pinch of nutmeg or ginger to keep a warm spice note. The cookies will still be delicious, just less exotic in flavor.
Why do I need both baking soda and baking powder?
Baking soda reacts with acidic ingredients to help spread and brown, while baking powder provides lift and tenderness. Using both balances rise and texture, giving soft but well-shaped cookies.
Can I make these cookies gluten-free?
Yes, substitute all-purpose flour with a gluten-free baking blend that includes xanthan gum. The cookies will remain soft but might be a bit more delicate, so handle gently when transferring.
How do I keep the cookies soft longer?
Store cookies in an airtight container with a slice of bread or a damp paper towel to retain moisture. Avoid refrigeration if you want to keep them soft, as it can dry them out.

Fresh Pea and Mint Soup with a Refreshing Twist for Spring Evenings

Lummy Hayes Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 4 servings

This fresh pea and mint soup is a light yet satisfying dish that brightens chilly spring nights. The mint adds a lively freshness that makes it feel special. Perfect for a cozy starter or a light supper!

Introduction

I first made this fresh pea and mint soup on a chilly spring evening when I wanted something light but still comforting. It’s been a favorite ever since because it’s so simple yet feels special thanks to the bright mint. What makes this version stand out is using fresh peas when possible and adding a touch of lemon juice at the end to really lift the flavors. Also, blending the soup until silky smooth gives it a creamy texture without needing heavy cream. I remember the first time I tried making it, I rushed the sauté step and the onion didn’t soften properly — lesson learned! Taking the time to gently cook the onions and garlic really builds the base flavor. The finished soup is vibrant green, velvety, and refreshing, perfect as a starter or a light dinner. It’s the kind of dish that feels fancy but is super easy to pull together, even on a weeknight. Plus, it’s great for spring because it’s light and fresh but still warming enough when the evenings are cool. If you love simple, fresh flavors with a little twist, you’ll enjoy this pea and mint soup as much as I do.

Why This Works

This pea and mint soup succeeds because of how the ingredients and techniques work together to create a balance of fresh, sweet, and bright flavors. Starting with gently sautéed onions and garlic builds a savory base without overpowering the delicate peas. Slow cooking the onions releases their natural sugars, which complements the sweetness of the peas and softens their texture.

The peas themselves provide the star flavor and vibrant green color. Using fresh peas when possible enhances sweetness and freshness, while frozen peas are a great convenient alternative that still maintain good flavor and texture. Cooking the peas just until tender preserves their bright color and prevents them from becoming mushy or dull.

Adding fresh mint right before blending infuses the soup with a bright herbal note that lifts the entire dish. Mint’s essential oils are volatile, so adding it late ensures those fresh flavors aren’t lost to prolonged cooking. The pureeing process creates a smooth, velvety texture that feels indulgent without heavy cream.

Finally, finishing with a squeeze of lemon juice adds acidity that balances sweetness and enhances the overall flavor. This acid brightens the soup, making it refreshing and lively rather than flat. Optional yogurt adds creaminess and a subtle tang, rounding out the mouthfeel without masking the fresh pea and mint flavors.

  • When buying fresh peas, look for pods that are bright green and plump without any blemishes or shriveling. Fresh peas are sweeter and more tender than frozen, providing the best flavor and texture. If fresh peas aren’t available, high-quality frozen peas work well and retain much of the sweetness and vibrant color. Avoid canned peas, which tend to be mushy and lack flavor. Fresh or frozen peas are essential for that bright green color and natural sweetness that define this soup.
  • Choose fresh mint with vibrant green leaves that aren’t wilted or brown. Mint adds a refreshing herbal brightness that complements the peas perfectly. It’s important to add mint towards the end of cooking to preserve its bright flavor and essential oils. Using dried mint won’t give the same fresh burst and can taste muted or earthy. Fresh mint also works beautifully as a garnish to add color and scent to the finished soup.
  • Using a good-quality low-sodium vegetable broth adds depth and umami without overpowering the delicate pea flavor. Broth enriches the soup base, while water can make it taste flat. If you use water, be sure to season the soup well. Homemade broth or a trusted brand with clean ingredients is best to keep the soup light and fresh. The broth also provides liquid to cook the peas evenly and helps create a smooth texture when blended.
  • Freshly squeezed lemon juice is crucial for adding brightness and balance to the soup. The acidity cuts through the sweetness of the peas and richness of any added yogurt, lifting the overall flavor. Bottled lemon juice lacks the fresh brightness and can taste dull or artificial. Adding lemon juice at the end preserves its lively zing and prevents it from cooking away during simmering.
  • Greek yogurt adds creaminess and a subtle tang without heaviness, making the soup feel richer and more indulgent. Use plain, unsweetened Greek yogurt to avoid altering the flavor. Adding it off the heat prevents curdling and keeps the texture smooth. For a dairy-free version, coconut cream or cashew cream can be substituted but will change the flavor profile slightly.

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons olive oil, extra virgin
  • 1 medium onion, finely chopped
  • 2 garlic cloves, minced
  • 4 cups fresh or frozen peas
  • 4 cups vegetable broth, low sodium
  • 1/4 cup fresh mint leaves, chopped
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
  • 1/2 cup plain Greek yogurt or crème fraîche (optional, for creaminess)
  • Juice of half a lemon

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Heat the olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the chopped onion and sauté gently for about 7-8 minutes until softened and translucent, stirring occasionally. This step is key to build a flavorful base for the soup—cooking the onion slowly releases its natural sweetness without browning. Avoid rushing or turning the heat too high, which can cause the onions to brown and add bitterness. Watch for a soft texture and a sweet aroma as your cue that the onions are ready for the next step.

    ⏱ 8✓ Onions are soft, translucent, and smell sweet without any browning
  2. Step 2

    Add the minced garlic to the pot and cook for 1-2 minutes, stirring constantly to prevent burning. Garlic cooks quickly and can become bitter if overdone, so keep the heat moderate and watch carefully. This step infuses the soup with a subtle garlic fragrance that complements the peas and mint perfectly. Once fragrant, proceed immediately to the next step to keep garlic from overcooking.

    ⏱ 2✓ Garlic is fragrant and lightly softened but not browned
  3. Step 3

    Add the fresh or frozen peas and stir to combine with the onion and garlic mixture. Pour in the vegetable broth and bring everything to a gentle boil over medium-high heat. This step starts softening the peas and blending the flavors. Using vegetable broth instead of water adds depth, but if you use water, consider adding a pinch of salt to compensate. Avoid boiling too hard as it can dull the bright pea flavor—just a gentle boil will do.

    ⏱ 10✓ Soup is bubbling gently and peas look tender and bright green
  4. Step 4

    Reduce the heat to low and simmer the soup uncovered for 10-12 minutes. This allows the peas to become tender enough to blend smoothly while letting some liquid evaporate to concentrate flavors. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking. Simmering too long can dull the fresh pea taste, so keep an eye on timing. The peas should be soft but still vibrant.

    ⏱ 12✓ Peas are tender when pierced with a fork; broth slightly thickened
  5. Step 5

    Remove the pot from heat and add the chopped mint leaves. Using an immersion blender, puree the soup until silky smooth. If you don’t have an immersion blender, carefully transfer the soup in batches to a regular blender. Adding mint before blending helps infuse the soup with fresh herbal notes evenly. Be cautious when blending hot liquids to avoid splatters—vent the blender lid slightly and blend in short bursts.

    ⏱ 5✓ Soup is smooth and evenly blended with a bright green color
  6. Step 6

    Stir in the lemon juice and season with salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste. The lemon juice brightens and balances the sweetness of the peas and freshness of the mint. Taste as you go to get the seasoning just right. Adding acid at the end preserves its lively zip.

    ⏱ 2✓ Soup tastes balanced with a fresh, bright finish
  7. Step 7

    Optional: For a creamier texture, stir in the Greek yogurt or crème fraîche just before serving. Do this off the heat to prevent curdling. This adds richness without overpowering the fresh flavors and gives the soup a lovely velvety mouthfeel. If you prefer vegan, you can skip this or use a plant-based alternative.

    ⏱ 2✓ Soup is creamy and smooth without any lumps or separation
  8. Step 8

    Serve the soup warm, garnished with a few fresh mint leaves or a drizzle of olive oil if you like. This final touch adds visual appeal and a hint of extra flavor. Pair with crusty bread or a light salad for a complete meal. The soup should be vibrant, fresh, and comforting at this point.

    ⏱ 3✓ Soup is hot and garnished attractively
  9. Step 9

    If storing leftovers, let the soup cool to room temperature before transferring to airtight containers. This prevents condensation and keeps the soup fresh longer. Reheat gently on the stove or in the microwave with a splash of broth or water to loosen if it thickens. Proper storage and reheating preserve the soup’s fresh flavor and texture.

    ⏱ 1✓ Soup is cooled and stored properly without condensation inside container

Pro Tips

  • Slowly sauté onions on medium-low heat until translucent. — Sautéing onions slowly releases their natural sweetness and creates a flavorful base without browning, which could add bitterness. Rushing this step or using high heat can cause uneven cooking and harsh flavors, detracting from the soup’s delicate pea and mint notes.
  • Add fresh mint just before blending the soup. — Mint’s essential oils are volatile and can dissipate with prolonged cooking. Adding mint at the end preserves its bright, fresh flavor and vibrant color, ensuring the soup tastes lively and aromatic rather than dull or cooked down.
  • Use an immersion blender for smooth texture and easy cleanup. — Immersion blenders allow you to puree the soup directly in the pot, which saves time and reduces mess. Blending until silky smooth creates a creamy mouthfeel without needing cream, enhancing the soup’s lightness and freshness.
  • Season gradually and taste often, especially after adding lemon juice. — Lemon juice brightens flavors but also impacts salt perception. Seasoning in stages helps avoid over-salting and achieves balanced flavor. Tasting as you go ensures the soup is perfectly seasoned and harmonious.
  • Use fresh or frozen peas, avoid canned peas. — Fresh or frozen peas retain natural sweetness and texture essential for this soup. Canned peas are often overcooked, mushy, and bland, which would negatively affect the soup’s flavor and consistency.
  • Add Greek yogurt off the heat to prevent curdling. — Yogurt can curdle if heated too quickly or at high temperatures. Stirring it in after removing the soup from heat keeps the texture smooth and creamy without separation.
  • Serve with a drizzle of good olive oil or fresh mint garnish. — A finishing drizzle of olive oil adds richness and a fruity aroma, enhancing mouthfeel. Fresh mint leaves add color and an extra burst of herbal freshness, making the soup more visually appealing and flavorful.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Cooking onions too quickly or at high heat.
    Why: Many cooks rush to speed up prep and crank up the heat, assuming it saves time without impact. This can cause onions to brown or burn, adding bitterness.
    Fix: Cook onions gently over medium-low heat until translucent and soft, stirring occasionally. This develops natural sweetness and builds a better flavor base for the soup.
  • Adding mint too early in cooking.
    Why: People often add herbs early to infuse flavor, but mint’s delicate oils degrade with heat.
    Fix: Add fresh mint just before blending or at the end of cooking to preserve its fresh, bright flavor.
  • Overcooking peas until mushy and dull in color.
    Why: Leaving peas to simmer too long can lead to loss of vibrant color and fresh flavor.
    Fix: Simmer peas just until tender (about 10-12 minutes) to keep their bright green color and sweet taste intact.
  • Using canned peas instead of fresh or frozen.
    Why: Canned peas are convenient but often mushy and flavorless, tempting some to use them.
    Fix: Opt for fresh or frozen peas for better texture and sweetness that define the soup’s character.
  • Adding yogurt while soup is still boiling.
    Why: High heat can cause yogurt to curdle and separate, ruining smooth texture.
    Fix: Remove soup from heat and let it cool slightly before stirring in yogurt for creamy consistency.

Variations

  • Vegan Coconut Mint Pea Soup: Replace Greek yogurt with coconut cream for a vegan twist. The coconut adds a subtle sweetness and creamy texture that pairs beautifully with mint and peas. Use vegetable broth as usual and finish with a squeeze of lemon to balance the flavors.
    Coconut cream thickens the soup slightly more than yogurt, so adjust broth quantity if needed for desired consistency.
  • Spicy Pea and Mint Soup: Add a pinch of red pepper flakes or a diced jalapeño during the onion sauté for a subtle kick. This warms up the fresh flavors and gives the soup a lively edge without overpowering the mint and peas.
    Be cautious with spice levels; add gradually and taste to avoid overwhelming the delicate flavors.
  • Pea and Mint Soup with Crispy Bacon Topping: For a smoky contrast, top the soup with crispy bacon bits or pancetta. The salty crunch complements the fresh, creamy soup and adds a hearty touch that’s great for cooler nights.
    Add bacon just before serving to retain crispness and avoid sogginess.
  • Pea and Mint Soup with Fresh Basil: Swap half the mint for fresh basil leaves for a slightly different herbal flavor. Basil adds a sweet, peppery note that pairs nicely with peas and lemon, giving the soup a new aromatic dimension.
    Add basil along with mint just before blending to preserve brightness.
  • Chunky Pea and Mint Soup: Reserve some cooked peas before blending and stir them back in for texture. This variation offers a rustic feel with bursts of pea flavor and a bit of chewiness instead of fully smooth soup.
    Blend only part of the soup and fold in reserved peas gently to keep their shape.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: The soup can be safely kept at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered. Beyond that, refrigerate to avoid spoilage. Cover with a lid or plastic wrap to prevent contamination and skin forming on top.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftovers in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The soup may thicken when cold; reheat gently with a splash of broth or water to restore consistency. Flavor may mellow slightly but remains delicious.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze the soup in airtight, freezer-safe containers for up to 2 months. Leave some headspace as the soup will expand. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat gently. Texture remains good if properly thawed and reheated.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat on the stovetop over low to medium heat, stirring occasionally. Add a splash of broth or water if the soup is too thick. Avoid boiling to preserve fresh flavors. Microwave reheating is also fine in short intervals, stirring in between.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the soup base up to 2 days ahead, including sautéing onions and cooking peas. Store in the fridge without blending. Blend, add mint, lemon juice, and yogurt just before serving to keep flavors bright and fresh.

FAQ

Can I use frozen peas instead of fresh?
Absolutely! Frozen peas are a great substitute and often picked at peak freshness, which keeps their sweetness intact. Just add them directly to the pot without thawing and cook as directed. The soup will still have that lovely vibrant color and flavor.
How do I prevent the soup from turning dull green or brownish?
To keep the soup bright green, avoid overcooking the peas and mint. Simmer peas just until tender, add mint at the end, and blend quickly. Overcooking or prolonged exposure to heat causes chlorophyll to break down, dulling the color.
Can I make this soup ahead of time?
Yes, you can prepare most of the soup a day or two ahead. Cook the base and peas, then refrigerate without blending. Blend and add fresh mint, lemon juice, and yogurt when ready to serve for best flavor and texture.
What if I don’t have an immersion blender?
No worries! You can carefully transfer the soup in batches to a regular blender. Make sure to vent the lid slightly to avoid pressure buildup from hot liquid. Blend in short pulses until smooth and return to the pot.
Is this soup vegan-friendly?
The base recipe is vegan if you omit the Greek yogurt or substitute it with a plant-based cream alternative like coconut cream. The flavors remain fresh and delicious with this swap.
Why do you add lemon juice at the end?
Lemon juice adds brightness and balances the sweetness of the peas. Adding it at the end preserves its fresh, vibrant acidity, which would fade if cooked too long.
How can I make the soup creamier without dairy?
Try blending in some cooked potato or cauliflower for natural creaminess, or use coconut cream. These options add body without dairy, keeping the soup smooth and luscious.
Can I freeze leftover soup?
Yes, the soup freezes well for up to 2 months. Cool it completely, store in airtight containers leaving space for expansion, and thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating gently.

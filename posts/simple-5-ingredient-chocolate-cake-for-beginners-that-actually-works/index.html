Simple 5-Ingredient Chocolate Cake That’s Moist and Delicious Every Time

Lummy Hayes Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 8 servings

This chocolate cake is a lifesaver when you want something rich but fuss-free. Just five ingredients come together for a super moist, chocolatey treat that’s perfect for birthdays or any day you need a little sweetness.

Introduction

I first made this chocolate cake on a whim when I realized I only had five ingredients in the pantry but needed something sweet for an impromptu get-together. Honestly, I wasn’t expecting much, but the result completely surprised me. It’s moist, rich, and has a deep chocolate flavor despite its simplicity. What sets this recipe apart is how it balances the cocoa with just enough sugar and milk to create a tender crumb without any fancy add-ins. I love that it’s so forgiving, perfect for beginners or anyone who wants a quick bake without a long ingredient list. I’ve tried more complicated recipes before, but this one always wins for ease and taste. The trick I learned was to sift the dry ingredients well and not overmix the batter, which keeps the cake light and fluffy. When it’s done, the cake has a beautiful crackly top and a soft, almost fudgy center that’s irresistible. It’s great for birthdays, casual dinners, or just whenever you’re craving chocolate but don’t want to fuss.

Why This Works

This recipe succeeds because of its minimal yet balanced ingredient list, which focuses on quality and technique rather than complexity. The combination of all-purpose flour, cocoa powder, sugar, baking powder, and milk creates a tender crumb with a rich chocolate flavor without needing butter or eggs.

Sifting the dry ingredients is key to aerating the flour and cocoa powder, which helps the cake rise and achieve a light texture. Baking powder acts as the leavening agent, releasing carbon dioxide when heated to create lift. The milk hydrates the flour and dissolves the sugar, contributing moisture and tenderness.

The temperature and timing of baking are critical. Baking at 350°F ensures even heat distribution, promoting Maillard reactions on the cake’s surface that develop flavor and a slight crust. Avoiding overmixing preserves the delicate crumb structure by limiting gluten development. Finally, cooling the cake properly prevents sogginess and allows flavors to mature.

Altogether, these factors produce a chocolate cake that’s moist, tender, and rich despite its simplicity. This recipe proves that sometimes less really is more when you understand the science behind the ingredients and baking process.

  • Look for fresh, high-quality all-purpose flour with a protein content around 10-12%. This balance provides enough gluten for structure without making the cake tough. Sifting flour before use removes lumps and adds air, which helps with a lighter crumb. Avoid using self-rising flour here as it contains salt and leavening agents that can throw off the recipe’s balance.
  • Choose natural unsweetened cocoa powder with a deep chocolate aroma. Avoid Dutch-processed cocoa unless you adjust the baking powder because Dutch processing neutralizes acidity, affecting leavening. Cocoa powder contributes both flavor and color but can dry out baked goods if overused, so sticking to the recommended amount is important.
  • Regular granulated sugar dissolves into the batter to provide sweetness and tenderness. Sugar also helps retain moisture by interfering with gluten formation, which keeps the cake soft. Using too much sugar could make the cake overly sweet or cause it to collapse, so precise measurement is key.
  • Baking powder is a chemical leavening agent that releases carbon dioxide when heated, causing the cake to rise. Use fresh baking powder for best results — old or expired powder won’t produce enough lift, leading to a dense cake. The recipe’s amount is calibrated to balance rise without causing the cake to overflow or collapse.
  • Room temperature milk hydrates the dry ingredients and helps dissolve sugar, leading to a smooth batter. It also adds fat and proteins that contribute to the cake’s moistness and tenderness. Using cold milk can cause the batter to seize or bake unevenly, so always bring it to room temperature before mixing.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1 cup milk, room temperature

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease a 9-inch round cake pan and lightly dust it with flour to prevent sticking. This step is crucial because it ensures your cake comes out cleanly without breaking apart. If you skip this, your cake might stick or crumble when you try to remove it. Also, preheating the oven fully before baking helps the cake rise evenly and develop a nice crust on top.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Oven fully preheated to 350°F; pan is evenly greased
  2. Step 2

    In a large mixing bowl, sift together the flour, cocoa powder, baking powder, and sugar. Sifting helps to remove lumps and aerate the dry ingredients, which is key for a light texture. If you skip sifting, your cake could have dense spots or uneven baking. Mix the dry ingredients gently to combine, but don’t overdo it to avoid developing gluten.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are evenly mixed, no lumps visible
  3. Step 3

    Slowly add the milk to the dry mixture, stirring gently with a spatula or wooden spoon until just combined. The batter should be smooth but slightly thick. Avoid overmixing here, as that can make the cake tough. The milk’s temperature matters too — room temperature helps the ingredients blend better and promotes even baking.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Batter is smooth with no visible flour patches
  4. Step 4

    Pour the batter into the prepared cake pan, smoothing the top with a spatula. Tap the pan lightly on the counter to release any large air bubbles trapped in the batter. This helps prevent holes or tunnels inside the cake and ensures an even crumb.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter evenly spread and surface smooth
  5. Step 5

    Place the pan in the center of the preheated oven and bake for 28-30 minutes. Resist opening the oven door during the first 20 minutes to avoid temperature drops that can cause the cake to sink. Start checking for doneness around 28 minutes by inserting a toothpick into the center; it should come out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter.

    ⏱ 28-30 minutes✓ Toothpick inserted in center comes out mostly clean with few crumbs
  6. Step 6

    Once baked, remove the cake from the oven and let it cool in the pan for about 10 minutes. This resting time helps the cake firm up slightly, making it easier to remove from the pan without breaking. Avoid cooling it completely in the pan as it can trap steam and make the bottom soggy.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cake edges start to pull away from the pan slightly
  7. Step 7

    Run a knife around the edges of the cake to loosen it, then carefully invert it onto a cooling rack. Let it cool completely before slicing. Cooling fully is important because the cake firms up and the flavors deepen, giving you that rich, moist texture we’re aiming for.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Cake is completely cool to the touch and holds shape when sliced
  8. Step 8

    Serve the cake plain or dusted with powdered sugar. If you want to dress it up, a simple dollop of whipped cream or fresh berries pairs beautifully without overpowering the chocolate flavor. This cake also freezes well if you want to save leftovers — wrap tightly in plastic wrap and foil.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Cake slices cleanly and presents beautifully
  9. Step 9

    Store any leftovers in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days or refrigerate to extend freshness. When reheating, warm gently in the microwave for about 15 seconds to bring back softness without drying it out.

    ⏱ N/A✓ Cake remains moist and tender after storage

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your dry ingredients together before mixing. — Sifting combines and aerates the flour, sugar, cocoa powder, and baking powder, which prevents lumps and helps the cake rise evenly. Skipping this can lead to dense spots or uneven texture.
  • Use room temperature milk for mixing the batter. — Cold milk can cause the batter to seize or mix unevenly, resulting in a denser cake. Room temperature milk blends better with the dry ingredients, promoting a smooth batter and consistent crumb.
  • Don’t overmix the batter once you add the milk. — Overmixing develops gluten, which makes the cake tough and chewy instead of tender. Stir gently until just combined to keep the crumb soft and light.
  • Preheat your oven fully before baking. — Baking in a cold or unevenly heated oven prevents proper rise and can cause the cake to be dense or collapse. A fully preheated oven ensures even cooking and that the baking powder activates correctly.
  • Avoid opening the oven door during the first 20 minutes of baking. — Opening the door lets heat escape and can cause the cake to sink or bake unevenly. Wait until the cake has set before checking doneness.
  • Let the cake cool in the pan for 10 minutes before removing. — This resting period allows the cake to firm up slightly, preventing it from breaking apart when you turn it out of the pan.
  • Use a toothpick to test doneness near the end of baking. — A toothpick inserted in the center should come out mostly clean with a few moist crumbs. If it’s wet, the cake needs more time. This prevents under or over-baking.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping sifting the dry ingredients
    Why: People often want to save time or don’t realize sifting affects texture and rise.
    Fix: Take the extra few minutes to sift flour, cocoa powder, sugar, and baking powder together. This ensures even mixing and aeration, leading to a lighter, more uniform cake.
  • Using cold milk straight from the fridge
    Why: It’s easy to forget to bring ingredients to room temperature.
    Fix: Plan ahead and leave milk out for 30 minutes before mixing. This helps the batter combine smoothly and prevents dense spots.
  • Overmixing the batter after adding milk
    Why: Some think mixing longer makes the batter smoother, but it actually develops gluten.
    Fix: Mix gently until just combined. A few lumps are okay. This keeps the cake tender and soft.
  • Opening the oven door too early
    Why: Curiosity or impatience can lead to opening the door before set time.
    Fix: Resist the urge to peek until at least 20 minutes have passed to keep consistent oven temperature and avoid sinking.
  • Not greasing and flouring the pan properly
    Why: Rushing or assuming nonstick pans will do the job alone.
    Fix: Grease the pan well and dust lightly with flour to prevent sticking. This helps the cake release cleanly and keeps the crust intact.

Variations

  • Add Espresso Powder for a Mocha Twist: Mix in 1 teaspoon of instant espresso powder with the dry ingredients to deepen the chocolate flavor with subtle coffee notes. This adds complexity without overpowering the cake. Great for coffee lovers who want a sophisticated twist.
    Espresso powder dissolves easily and intensifies cocoa’s richness. No adjustment to liquids needed.
  • Use Buttermilk Instead of Milk: Swap regular milk for buttermilk to add tanginess and extra moisture. The acidity also reacts with baking powder to create a slightly fluffier texture. Expect a subtle tang that balances the sweetness nicely.
    Buttermilk is thicker, so the batter might be a bit denser but rises well. No changes to baking time.
  • Top with Chocolate Ganache: For a decadent finish, pour a simple chocolate ganache over the cooled cake. It’s just chocolate and cream melted together, adding a glossy, rich layer that elevates this simple cake to a celebration-worthy dessert.
    Ganache sets at room temperature but can be chilled for a firmer coating.
  • Add Fresh Berries and Whipped Cream: Serve slices with fresh raspberries or strawberries and a dollop of lightly sweetened whipped cream. This adds freshness and lightness, contrasting the rich chocolate perfectly for a balanced dessert.
    Add berries just before serving to keep them fresh and vibrant.
  • Make It Vegan by Using Plant-Based Milk and Oil: Replace dairy milk with almond or oat milk and add 2 tablespoons of neutral oil to keep moisture. This version is great for vegan or dairy-free diets without sacrificing texture or flavor.
    Oil adds fat that milk usually provides; no eggs needed in this recipe.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: This cake keeps well at room temperature for up to 2 days if stored in an airtight container to prevent drying out. Keep it away from direct sunlight or heat sources to maintain moisture and flavor.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 5 days. The cake may firm up and lose some softness when cold, so allowing it to come to room temperature before serving or warming slightly helps restore texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cake tightly in plastic wrap and aluminum foil, then freeze for up to 3 months. To thaw, transfer to the fridge overnight or leave at room temperature for a few hours. Avoid thawing in warm places to prevent sogginess.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm slices gently in the microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes. Adding a small splash of milk or covering loosely with foil helps retain moisture during reheating.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can mix the dry ingredients up to 2 days ahead and store them in an airtight container. Add milk and bake fresh when ready. Alternatively, bake the cake and freeze it, then thaw and serve when needed for quick dessert prep.

FAQ

Can I substitute the all-purpose flour with gluten-free flour?
Yes, you can use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend, but results may vary. Look for a blend that includes xanthan gum or add 1/4 teaspoon yourself to help with structure. The cake might be slightly more crumbly but still delicious.
What if I don’t have baking powder?
You can substitute baking powder with 1/4 teaspoon baking soda plus 1/2 teaspoon cream of tartar per teaspoon of baking powder needed. This combo mimics baking powder’s leavening but be precise to avoid off flavors or sinking.
Can I use melted butter instead of milk?
Milk hydrates the dry ingredients and keeps the batter smooth, while butter adds fat and richness. Using melted butter only changes texture and might make the cake denser and greasier. For best results, stick to milk or add butter along with milk.
Why did my cake turn out dense and heavy?
Common reasons include overmixing the batter, using expired baking powder, or opening the oven door too early. Mix until just combined, check your leavening agent’s freshness, and avoid temperature drops during baking.
Can I double the recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, doubling works fine; just use a larger pan and adjust baking time accordingly. Start checking doneness 5-10 minutes before the original time to avoid overbaking.
Is it okay to add eggs to this recipe?
This recipe is designed egg-free for simplicity. Adding eggs can make the cake richer and more structured, but you’ll need to adjust the amounts of other ingredients and possibly reduce baking powder. Try a tested recipe if adding eggs.
How can I make the cake more moist?
Use buttermilk instead of milk or add a tablespoon of vegetable oil to the batter. Also, avoid overbaking and ensure the cake is stored properly to retain moisture.
What is the best way to store leftover cake?
Store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days or refrigerate up to 5 days. For longer storage, freeze wrapped tightly for up to 3 months. This keeps the cake fresh and prevents drying.

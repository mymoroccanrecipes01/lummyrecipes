Fresh and Vibrant Vegan Pea and Mint Soup for Spring Days

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 4 servings

This bright pea and mint soup is such a refreshing way to welcome spring! Using frozen peas keeps it quick, while fresh mint adds a cool twist. I love serving it as a light lunch or starter when the sun’s out.

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this pea and mint soup during a spring weekend at a friend's house, and it instantly became one of my favorite light meals. What I love about this version is how easy it is to make using frozen peas, which keeps the prep super quick without sacrificing that fresh bright flavor. Plus, adding fresh mint at the end really lifts the whole soup, giving it a clean, refreshing note that feels like spring in every spoonful. Over time, I’ve learned that gently sautéing the onions and garlic before adding the broth adds a subtle sweetness that balances the peas perfectly. Also, blending the soup until silky smooth but still vibrant green makes it look as good as it tastes. It’s a perfect starter or light lunch, especially on warmer days when you want something nourishing but not heavy. I’ve tried versions with cream or potatoes, but this one stays light and vegan, which I appreciate. The lemon juice at the end is my secret weapon—it brightens everything up and makes the flavors pop. Honestly, it’s a simple recipe that feels fancy enough to serve guests but easy enough for a weeknight. I hope you enjoy making and eating it as much as I do!

Why This Works

This soup works so well because of the balance between the sweet peas and the bright, fresh mint. Frozen peas retain their sweetness and color because they're flash-frozen at peak ripeness, locking in nutrients and flavor. Cooking them gently in broth softens them just enough, releasing their natural sugars without losing that fresh taste.

The sautéed onions and garlic create a flavorful base through the Maillard reaction, which develops subtle sweetness and complexity without overpowering the peas. Cooking the aromatics slowly and avoiding browning is key to keeping the soup light and fresh.

Blending the soup until smooth creates a luxurious texture that feels indulgent but remains light. The fresh mint added after cooking provides a cooling counterpoint to the sweetness, with volatile oils that would otherwise dissipate if cooked too long. Finally, a splash of lemon juice brightens and balances the flavors, helping to lift the dish and add a fresh acidity that keeps it from feeling flat.

Every step is designed to preserve the delicate flavors and vibrant color, making this soup a perfect example of how simple ingredients combined thoughtfully can create something truly special.

  • When choosing frozen peas, look for ones that are bright green and free of freezer burn. These peas are picked and frozen quickly, preserving their sweetness and vibrant color. Avoid thawing before cooking to prevent mushiness; adding them frozen to hot broth keeps their texture intact and flavor fresh.
  • Fresh mint is essential for that cool, bright flavor. Choose leaves that are vibrant and free from browning or wilting. Adding mint after cooking preserves its essential oils, which give the soup its signature freshness. Dried mint won't provide the same aromatic lift and can taste bitter if overcooked.
  • A good quality vegetable broth adds depth and body without overpowering the soup. Homemade or low-sodium store-bought broths work best to allow the pea and mint flavors to shine. Avoid broths with strong herbs or spices, which can clash with the delicate balance here.
  • Using a mild, good-quality olive oil for sautéing the onions and garlic adds richness and a subtle fruity note. It also helps soften the aromatics gently without burning. Extra virgin olive oil can be used, but avoid overheating to prevent bitterness.
  • Freshly squeezed lemon juice adds the perfect acidic brightness that balances the sweetness of the peas and the coolness of the mint. Bottled lemon juice lacks the fresh aroma and can sometimes impart a metallic taste, so fresh is best. Add it at the end to preserve its vibrant flavor and avoid dulling the soup's color.

Ingredients

  • 4 cups frozen peas
  • 1 small onion, chopped
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 4 cups vegetable broth
  • 1/2 cup fresh mint leaves, loosely packed
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Juice of 1 lemon

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Heat the olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the chopped onion and cook gently until it becomes soft and translucent, about 5-7 minutes. This step is crucial because slowly cooking the onion releases its natural sweetness, which balances the fresh peas later. Avoid browning the onions as that changes the soup’s delicate flavor. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking and keep the heat moderate to soften without color.

    ⏱ 7✓ Onions are soft, translucent, and fragrant but not browned
  2. Step 2

    Add the minced garlic to the pot and sauté for about 1 minute until fragrant. Garlic cooks quickly and can burn easily, so keep stirring and watch closely. This step infuses the base with a gentle aromatic flavor that complements the peas without overpowering them.

    ⏱ 1✓ Garlic becomes fragrant but remains pale, not browned
  3. Step 3

    Pour in the vegetable broth and bring the mixture to a gentle boil. Then add the frozen peas. Using frozen peas helps retain that sweet, fresh flavor and vibrant color since they were picked at peak ripeness. Let the soup simmer for about 10-15 minutes so the peas become tender and the flavors meld together.

    ⏱ 15✓ Peas are tender when pierced with a fork and broth simmers gently
  4. Step 4

    Remove the pot from heat and add the fresh mint leaves. Using fresh mint instead of dried makes a huge difference—it adds a bright, cooling flavor that really lifts the soup. Let the mint steep for a couple of minutes to infuse before blending.

    ⏱ 3✓ Mint leaves wilt slightly and aroma becomes noticeable
  5. Step 5

    Carefully transfer the soup in batches to a blender, or use an immersion blender right in the pot. Blend until silky smooth and bright green. This smooth texture makes the soup feel luxurious and highlights the freshness of the peas and mint. Avoid over-blending or the soup can become too thin.

    ⏱ 5✓ Soup is creamy, smooth, and uniformly bright green
  6. Step 6

    Return the blended soup to the pot and warm gently over low heat. Season with salt and pepper to taste. Adjusting seasoning at this stage is important because blending can mute flavors. Warming slowly keeps the soup silky without breaking it down further.

    ⏱ 5✓ Soup is hot but not boiling, flavors well balanced
  7. Step 7

    Stir in the lemon juice just before serving. The acidity wakes up the flavors and adds a fresh zing that makes the whole soup pop. Don’t add lemon too early or it can dull the color and soften the texture.

    ⏱ 2✓ Soup has a bright, fresh aroma and balanced tang
  8. Step 8

    Serve the soup warm in bowls, optionally garnishing with a few fresh mint leaves or a drizzle of olive oil for extra richness. This final touch makes it look inviting and adds a subtle flavor layer. I like to serve it with crusty bread on the side.

    ⏱ 3✓ Soup looks vibrant with fresh garnish and is ready to enjoy
  9. Step 9

    If you want to store leftovers, allow the soup to cool completely before transferring to an airtight container. This prevents condensation that can dilute flavor. Reheat gently on the stove adding a splash of water or broth if needed to loosen the texture.

    ⏱ 2✓ Soup is cooled down and stored properly without condensation

Pro Tips

  • Don’t overcook the peas; simmer just until tender. — Overcooking peas can cause them to lose their vibrant color and fresh sweetness, resulting in a dull, mushy soup. Cooking them just until tender ensures the soup stays bright and flavorful.
  • Add fresh mint after cooking, not during. — Mint’s essential oils are delicate and evaporate with heat. Adding it after cooking preserves its fresh aroma and flavor, giving the soup a refreshing lift.
  • Use an immersion blender for easier cleanup. — Blending directly in the pot saves time and reduces mess, allowing you to control the soup’s texture without transferring hot liquid multiple times.
  • Season at the end after blending. — Blending can mute flavors, so adjusting salt and pepper last ensures the seasoning is just right and balanced.
  • Use fresh lemon juice to brighten the soup. — Fresh lemon juice offers vibrant acidity and aroma that bottled versions lack, enhancing the overall flavor profile.
  • Sauté onions slowly over medium heat. — Slow cooking releases natural sweetness without browning, creating a balanced base that complements the peas.
  • Serve with crusty bread for a complete meal. — The bread adds texture contrast and makes the light soup more filling and satisfying.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overcooking the peas until they’re mushy.
    Why: Many cooks assume peas need long cooking to soften, but frozen peas cook quickly and become mushy if left too long.
    Fix: Simmer peas just 10-15 minutes until tender but still hold shape, then blend immediately to preserve fresh flavor and texture.
  • Adding mint during cooking instead of after.
    Why: People often add herbs early to infuse flavor, but mint’s delicate oils evaporate with heat.
    Fix: Add fresh mint leaves after removing soup from heat to keep its bright, refreshing flavor intact.
  • Skipping the sauté step for onions and garlic.
    Why: In a rush, cooks might add raw aromatics directly to broth, missing flavor development.
    Fix: Gently sauté onions and garlic first to build a sweet, flavorful base that enhances the soup’s overall taste.
  • Using bottled lemon juice instead of fresh.
    Why: Convenience often leads to bottled lemon juice, which lacks fresh aroma.
    Fix: Always use freshly squeezed lemon juice added at the end for brightness and vibrant flavor.
  • Blending the soup too long until watery.
    Why: Over-blending breaks down fibers excessively, thinning texture.
    Fix: Blend just until smooth and creamy, stopping before the soup becomes too thin.

Variations

  • Add Coconut Milk for Creaminess: Stir in 1/2 cup of coconut milk after blending for a subtle tropical creaminess that complements the peas and mint beautifully. This adds richness without dairy and creates a silky texture.
    Adding coconut milk after blending preserves its delicate flavor and prevents curdling.
  • Spicy Kick with Jalapeño: For a spicy twist, sauté a small diced jalapeño with the onions and garlic. It adds a gentle heat that pairs surprisingly well with the cool mint and sweet peas.
    Remove seeds for milder heat and add jalapeño early to infuse the base.
  • Add Fresh Spinach for Extra Greens: Toss in a handful of fresh spinach leaves just before blending for an extra boost of nutrients and a deeper green color. Spinach blends smoothly and enhances the soup’s freshness.
    Add spinach after peas are cooked so it wilts gently without losing color.
  • Serve Chilled as a Summer Soup: Cool the soup completely and refrigerate for 2 hours to serve cold. It’s incredibly refreshing on hot days and makes a great appetizer or light lunch.
    Adjust seasoning after chilling, as cold temperatures can mute flavors.
  • Use Fresh Peas Instead of Frozen: If you have fresh peas in season, use them instead of frozen for a sweeter, slightly firmer soup. You may need to cook them a bit longer to soften fully.
    Fresh peas require about 20 minutes simmering; watch texture carefully.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: This soup can safely sit at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered. Beyond that, bacteria can grow, so refrigerate promptly to keep it fresh and safe.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store soup in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavor may deepen but the vibrant green color can dull slightly. Reheat gently on the stove or microwave, adding a splash of broth if it thickens too much.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze soup in airtight containers or heavy-duty freezer bags for up to 3 months. To prevent freezer burn, remove as much air as possible before sealing. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat gently, stirring well to restore texture.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm soup over low heat on the stove, stirring occasionally to prevent sticking. Heat until just steaming, about 5-7 minutes. Add a splash of broth or water if too thick, and avoid boiling to keep flavors fresh.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the soup base (sautéed onions and garlic with broth and peas) up to 2 days ahead and refrigerate. Blend fresh mint and lemon juice just before serving for maximum flavor and freshness.

FAQ

Can I use dried mint instead of fresh mint?
Dried mint has a much stronger, sometimes bitter flavor and lacks the fresh, bright aroma of fresh mint. If you must use dried, add it sparingly during cooking, but know it won’t give the same refreshing lift. Fresh mint added at the end is always best.
What if I don’t have a blender?
Without a blender, you can mash the peas with a potato masher for a chunkier texture, but the soup won’t be as smooth or creamy. Alternatively, using a food processor or immersion blender works best for that silky finish.
Can I make this soup ahead of time?
Yes! You can make the base soup a day or two ahead and refrigerate. Add the fresh mint and lemon juice just before serving to keep the flavors vibrant and fresh.
Why does my soup turn dull green after blending?
This happens if peas are overcooked or blended too long, breaking down chlorophyll. To keep it bright, cook peas just until tender and blend briefly until smooth.
How can I make this soup creamier without dairy?
Stir in coconut milk or a splash of plant-based cream after blending. This adds a rich, creamy texture without overpowering the fresh flavors.
Is it okay to add lemon juice before cooking?
It’s better to add lemon juice at the end because cooking it can dull the soup’s color and reduce its fresh brightness. Adding it last keeps the flavors lively.
Can I substitute vegetable broth with water?
You can use water in a pinch, but broth adds depth and richness. If using water, consider adding extra seasoning or herbs to boost flavor.
Why is the soup bitter sometimes?
Bitterness can come from overcooked garlic, burnt onions, or old mint. Cook aromatics gently and add fresh mint at the end to avoid this issue.

Fresh Asparagus and Pea Salad with Lemon Vinaigrette

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 5 min cook 4 servings

This asparagus and pea salad captures the bright freshness of spring vegetables with a crisp texture and a lively lemon vinaigrette. The combination of slightly tender asparagus, sweet peas, and zesty citrus creates a well-balanced dish that works well as a light lunch or a side for warmer weather meals.

Introduction

I often turn to this asparagus and pea salad in early spring when fresh vegetables start to appear at farmers' markets. The crisp snap of the asparagus combined with the sweetness of peas reminds me of those first warm days when light, refreshing dishes feel especially welcome. What sets this salad apart is its simple lemon vinaigrette that brightens the natural flavors without overpowering them. Whether served as a light lunch or alongside a grilled main, this salad brings a clean, fresh contrast to richer dishes. Its straightforward preparation and vibrant colors make it a staple in my seasonal cooking rotation.

Why This Works

The success of this salad lies in its balance of textures and flavors. Asparagus, when briefly cooked, retains a slight crunch and a subtle grassy note that complements the sweet, tender peas. The lemon vinaigrette introduces acidity and brightness, which helps to cut through the natural richness of the olive oil and enhances the freshness of the vegetables.

Cooking the asparagus just until tender-crisp preserves its vibrant green color and fresh flavor, while the quick blanching of peas prevents them from becoming mushy. The Dijon mustard in the dressing emulsifies the oil and lemon juice, creating a smooth coating that clings well to the vegetables. The addition of a little honey or maple syrup balances the acidity, rounding out the flavor profile without making it sweet.

  • Choosing firm, bright green asparagus with closed tips is key to achieving the crisp texture this salad depends on. Overcooking will result in limp stalks and dull color. Cutting into uniform pieces ensures even cooking and a pleasant mouthfeel.
  • Fresh peas are ideal for their natural sweetness and snap. If using frozen peas, thaw them gently and avoid cooking further to preserve texture. They add a burst of color and a soft contrast to the asparagus.
  • Freshly squeezed lemon juice and zest provide the essential acidity and citrus aroma. The zest adds oils that enhance the fragrance, while the juice brightens the overall flavor. Bottled lemon juice lacks this depth and freshness.
  • Extra virgin olive oil with a fruity and grassy profile complements the vegetables and forms the base of the vinaigrette. Using a high-quality oil impacts the salad’s flavor significantly.
  • This ingredient acts as an emulsifier, allowing the oil and lemon juice to combine smoothly. It also contributes a mild pungency that deepens the dressing’s complexity.

Ingredients

  • 300g (10.5 oz) fresh asparagus, trimmed and cut into 2-inch (5 cm) pieces
  • 150g (5 oz) fresh or frozen peas, thawed if frozen
  • 1 small red onion (about 70g), thinly sliced
  • 30g (1/4 cup) fresh parsley, roughly chopped
  • Zest and juice of 1 lemon (about 2 tablespoons juice)
  • 3 tablespoons (45 ml) extra virgin olive oil
  • 1 teaspoon (5 ml) Dijon mustard
  • 1 teaspoon (5 ml) honey or maple syrup (optional)
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Bring a medium pot of salted water to a boil. Add the asparagus pieces and blanch for 2-3 minutes until they turn bright green and are just tender but still crisp.

    ⏱ 2-3 minutes✓ Asparagus is vivid green and yields slightly when pierced with a fork but remains firm
  2. Step 2

    Add the peas to the boiling water during the last 30 seconds of the asparagus blanching. Remove both vegetables with a slotted spoon and immediately transfer them to a bowl of ice water to stop cooking and preserve color.

    ⏱ 30 seconds plus cooling time✓ Peas are bright green and tender without being mushy; asparagus stays crisp after cooling
  3. Step 3

    Drain the vegetables thoroughly after cooling, then place them in a large mixing bowl. Add the thinly sliced red onion and chopped parsley.

    ⏱ 2-3 minutes✓ Vegetables are dry to the touch to avoid diluting the dressing
  4. Step 4

    In a small bowl, whisk together lemon juice, lemon zest, Dijon mustard, and honey or maple syrup if using. Gradually whisk in the olive oil until the dressing is smooth and emulsified.

    ⏱ 2-3 minutes✓ Dressing appears glossy and homogenous without separation
  5. Step 5

    Pour the dressing over the vegetables and toss gently to coat everything evenly. Season with salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste.

    ⏱ 1-2 minutes✓ All ingredients are evenly coated with dressing; salad glistens with a light sheen
  6. Step 6

    Let the salad rest at room temperature for 10 minutes before serving to allow flavors to meld and the acidity to soften slightly.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Salad aroma brightens; vegetables retain texture and fresh appearance

Pro Tips

  • Do not overcook the asparagus. — Overcooked asparagus becomes mushy and loses its bright color, diminishing the salad's fresh texture and visual appeal.
  • Shock the vegetables in ice water immediately after blanching. — This stops the cooking process instantly, preserving the vibrant color and crispness essential to the salad's character.
  • Use freshly grated lemon zest instead of pre-packaged. — Fresh zest releases essential oils that brighten the dressing’s aroma and flavor, which bottled zest cannot replicate.
  • Whisk the vinaigrette thoroughly to emulsify. — A well-emulsified dressing clings better to the vegetables, ensuring every bite is flavorful and evenly dressed.
  • Adjust the sweetness in the dressing based on lemon acidity. — Lemons vary in tartness; balancing with a touch of honey or maple syrup prevents the vinaigrette from tasting overly sharp or sour.
  • Slice the red onion very thinly. — Thinner slices integrate more smoothly with the salad, providing a mild onion flavor without overpowering the other ingredients.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the ice water bath after blanching.
    Why: People often rush to finish and neglect this step, leading to overcooked, dull-colored vegetables.
    Fix: Prepare a bowl of ice water before blanching and transfer vegetables immediately to preserve color and texture.
  • Using bottled lemon juice instead of fresh.
    Why: Bottled juice is convenient but lacks fresh citrus oils and brightness.
    Fix: Always use freshly squeezed lemon juice and zest for maximum flavor impact.
  • Overdressing the salad.
    Why: Adding too much vinaigrette can make the salad soggy and mask the fresh vegetable flavors.
    Fix: Add dressing gradually and toss gently; the salad should have a light coating, not be swimming in dressing.
  • Not drying vegetables well after blanching.
    Why: Excess water dilutes the dressing and makes the salad watery.
    Fix: Drain thoroughly and gently pat dry with a kitchen towel before tossing with dressing.

Variations

  • Vegan Variation: Use maple syrup instead of honey for the dressing to keep it fully plant-based. Add toasted sunflower seeds for extra texture and protein.
    Maple syrup provides natural sweetness without altering the vinaigrette's acidity. Seeds add crunch and nutritional value without affecting dressing stability.
  • Mediterranean Twist: Add crumbled feta cheese, chopped kalamata olives, and a sprinkle of fresh oregano to the salad for a richer, savory flavor profile.
    Feta adds saltiness and creaminess, complementing the citrus dressing. Oregano enhances herbal notes while olives contribute a briny contrast.
  • Spring Herb Salad: Incorporate fresh mint, dill, and chives along with parsley to deepen the herbaceous character of the salad.
    These herbs introduce layers of fresh, bright flavors that harmonize with lemon and vegetables, enhancing the springtime appeal.
  • Add Toasted Nuts: Include toasted almonds or pine nuts for a nutty flavor and crunchy texture that contrasts with the tender vegetables.
    Toasting nuts enhances their aroma and adds complexity. Add nuts just before serving to maintain their crunch.
  • Grain Bowl Addition: Serve the salad over cooked quinoa or farro for a more substantial meal. Drizzle extra vinaigrette over the grains to tie flavors together.
    Grains provide a chewy base and additional nutrients, turning this salad into a balanced lunch option without altering the core vegetable preparation.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the salad at room temperature only for up to 1 hour to maintain freshness.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Keep the salad covered in an airtight container for up to 2 days. Note that vegetables may soften slightly over time.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Not recommended to freeze this salad as the texture and freshness will degrade.
  • ♨️ Reheating: This salad is best served cold or at room temperature and is not intended for reheating.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare and blanch the vegetables a few hours ahead, store separately from the dressing, then toss together just before serving to preserve texture.

FAQ

Can I make this asparagus and pea salad ahead of time?
Yes, you can blanch the vegetables and prepare the dressing in advance, but toss them together just before serving to maintain crisp texture.
Why is my asparagus soft and dull after making the salad?
This usually happens if asparagus is overcooked or not shocked in ice water after blanching. Blanch briefly and cool immediately in ice water to keep it bright and firm.
Can I use frozen peas instead of fresh?
Frozen peas work well if thawed gently and not cooked further. They retain sweetness and texture but avoid boiling them again to prevent mushiness.
How long does the salad last in the fridge?
Stored in an airtight container, the salad keeps well for up to 2 days, though vegetables may soften slightly over time.
Can I substitute lemon with another citrus fruit?
Lime juice can be used as a substitute, but it will slightly change the flavor profile. Avoid using orange juice as it is sweeter and less acidic.

Final Thoughts

This asparagus and pea salad celebrates the simplicity and freshness of spring produce. Its straightforward preparation and balanced flavors make it a versatile addition to many meals. I welcome your thoughts and any variations you've tried in the comments below.

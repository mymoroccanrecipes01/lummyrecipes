One Bowl Classic British Sponge Cake That’s Moist and Easy

Lummy Hayes
Published on May 1, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
8 Servings
Easy Difficulty

This one bowl British sponge cake is a total lifesaver—super moist with a tender crumb and zero fuss. Just mix it all together in one bowl, no mess! Great for afternoon tea or a quick sweet treat.

Pro Tips

Always use room temperature ingredients for better mixing.
Room temperature butter and eggs blend more smoothly, creating a stable batter that traps air well. Cold ingredients can cause the mixture to curdle or result in uneven texture, while melted butter changes the crumb structure, leading to a denser cake.
Sift your flour and baking powder together before adding.
Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking powder, preventing clumps. This ensures the leavening agent works uniformly, giving your sponge an even rise and a light, tender crumb.
Fold the flour gently into the batter to avoid overmixing.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which toughens the cake. Folding carefully keeps the batter light and airy, preserving the delicate texture typical of a classic sponge.
Don’t open the oven door during the first 20 minutes of baking.
Opening the door too early causes temperature fluctuations that can make the cake sink. Keeping the oven closed allows the leavening agents to work properly and the cake to rise evenly.
Use a timer and test doneness with a skewer after 30 minutes.
Baking times can vary slightly, so testing ensures your cake is perfectly cooked without drying out. A clean skewer means the cake is done, while wet batter indicates it needs more time.
Cool the cake in the tin briefly before transferring to a wire rack.
Cooling in the tin prevents the cake from breaking apart when hot, while transferring it to a wire rack prevents condensation buildup that can make the bottom soggy.
Use unsalted butter to control salt levels in your cake.
Salt enhances flavor but too much can overpower the sponge. Using unsalted butter lets you adjust salt precisely, keeping the flavor balanced and the crumb tender.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overmixing the batter after adding flour

Why it happens Many people think mixing thoroughly is good, but overmixing develops gluten, resulting in a tough, dense cake instead of a soft sponge.

Fix Fold the flour in gently with a spatula just until no flour streaks remain. Stop mixing as soon as the flour is incorporated to keep the batter light and airy.

Using cold eggs or butter

Why it happens It’s easy to forget to bring ingredients to room temperature, but cold butter and eggs don’t blend well and can cause curdling or lumps.

Fix Plan ahead to let eggs and butter sit out for about 30 minutes before baking. If you’re in a rush, gently warm eggs in warm water and soften butter in short bursts in the microwave, but avoid melting.

Opening the oven door early

Why it happens Impatience or curiosity makes bakers peek too soon, causing temperature drops that can make the cake collapse.

Fix Resist the urge to open the door until at least 20 minutes have passed. Use the oven light to check progress instead.

Not greasing the cake tin properly

Why it happens Skipping greasing or lining can cause the cake to stick and break when removed.

Fix Grease the tin thoroughly with butter or baking spray and dust with flour, or line with parchment paper for easy release.

Using expired baking powder

Why it happens Old baking powder loses its potency, resulting in poor rise and dense texture.

Fix Check the expiration date and test by mixing a little in warm water to see if it bubbles. Replace if inactive.

Variations

Lemon Zest Sponge

Add 1 tbsp finely grated lemon zest to the batter for a fresh citrus twist. It brightens the flavor and pairs perfectly with a dusting of powdered sugar or a lemon glaze. The zest adds subtle aroma without changing the texture.

Note: Fold the zest in with the flour to distribute evenly. Bake as usual.

Chocolate One Bowl Sponge

Replace 30g of flour with unsweetened cocoa powder for a chocolate version. This adds a rich, deep flavor while keeping the moist texture. Serve with whipped cream or berries for a decadent treat.

Note: Sift cocoa powder with flour and baking powder to avoid lumps.

Berry Swirl Sponge

Fold in 100g fresh or frozen berries gently into the batter before baking. This adds bursts of juicy sweetness and a pretty marbled effect. Great for summer teatime or brunch.

Note: Toss berries in a little flour before folding to prevent sinking.

Almond Sponge

Add 50g ground almonds to the dry ingredients for a nutty flavor and slightly denser texture. It makes the cake more moist and adds a lovely aroma. Perfect with a dusting of icing sugar or almond slivers on top.

Note: Reduce flour by 50g to compensate for the almonds.

Gluten-Free Sponge

Use a gluten-free self-raising flour blend instead of regular flour. This keeps the recipe accessible for gluten-sensitive eaters. The texture will be slightly different but still moist and tender.

Note: Ensure your baking powder is gluten-free and sift the flour well.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

The cake keeps well covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Use a cake dome or airtight container to prevent it from drying out. Avoid leaving it uncovered, as sponge cakes tend to harden quickly.

Refrigerator

Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The cake may firm up and lose some moisture when chilled, so it’s best served at room temperature. Bring it out 30 minutes before serving or warm gently if desired.

Freezer

Freeze the cooled cake wrapped tightly in plastic wrap and aluminum foil for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature. Avoid freezing cream or fresh fruit toppings, add those fresh after thawing.

Reheating

Warm slices gently in the microwave for 10-15 seconds or in a low oven (150°C) for 5-7 minutes to refresh softness. Adding a small splash of milk before reheating can help restore moisture. Avoid overheating as it dries out the cake.

Make Ahead

You can bake the cake 1-2 days ahead and store it wrapped at room temperature or in the fridge. Prepare any frosting or toppings separately and add just before serving to keep everything fresh and vibrant.

FAQ

Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
Yes, but reduce or omit any added salt in the recipe if using salted butter. Unsalted butter gives you better control over the flavor balance, but salted butter won’t ruin the cake—just adjust accordingly.
Why did my cake sink in the middle?
Sinking often happens if the oven temperature is too low, or if you opened the oven door too early. Also, underbaking or too much leavening can cause this. Make sure to bake at the correct temperature and avoid opening the door during the first 20 minutes.
Can I make this cake dairy-free?
You can substitute dairy butter with plant-based margarine and use a dairy-free milk alternative like almond or oat milk. The texture might be slightly different but it will still be moist and tasty.
What if I don’t have self-raising flour?
You can make your own by mixing 200g plain flour with 2 tsp baking powder and a pinch of salt. Sift well before using to ensure even distribution.
Is it okay to use frozen eggs?
Frozen eggs can be used if properly thawed in the fridge overnight and brought to room temperature before mixing. This helps prevent curdling and ensures smooth batter.
How do I know when the cake is fully baked?
Insert a skewer or toothpick in the center; it should come out clean or with a few moist crumbs. The cake should also spring back when gently pressed and have a golden color on top.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, you can double the ingredients but use a larger cake tin or bake in two tins. Adjust baking time accordingly and check doneness with a skewer.
Why is my cake dry despite following the recipe?
Dryness can result from overbaking or using too much flour. Make sure to measure flour correctly by spooning and leveling, and avoid baking longer than needed.

What You'll Need

One 8-inch round cake, serves 8

  • 200g caster sugar, granulated
  • 200g unsalted butter, softened
  • 4 large eggs, room temperature
  • 200g self-raising flour, sifted
  • 1 tsp baking powder
  • 2 tbsp milk, room temperature
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by preheating your oven to 180°C (350°F) and greasing an 8-inch round cake tin. This step ensures your cake won’t stick and will bake evenly. Using softened butter is key here because it blends smoothly with sugar, creating a light, fluffy base. If the butter is too cold, it won’t mix well and you might get lumps.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Greased tin should feel lightly coated but not greasy to the touch
Step 2
In a large mixing bowl, cream the softened butter and caster sugar together using a hand mixer or wooden spoon. This process traps air and helps the cake rise. Cream until the mixture is pale and fluffy, which usually takes about 3-5 minutes. Rushing this step can make your cake dense.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture looks lighter in color and has a soft, airy texture
Step 3
Crack in the eggs one at a time, beating well between each addition. This gradual incorporation prevents the batter from curdling. If the eggs are cold, the mixture might split, so room temperature eggs are important. If curdling happens, add a spoonful of flour to bring it back together.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter looks smooth and glossy with no separation
Step 4
Gently fold in the sifted self-raising flour and baking powder using a spatula. Folding rather than stirring vigorously keeps the batter light and airy, which is essential for a soft sponge. Overmixing here can develop gluten and make the cake tough.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flour is just incorporated with no visible streaks
Step 5
Add the vanilla extract and milk, folding gently to combine. The milk adds moisture and helps loosen the batter slightly, contributing to the tender crumb. Don’t overwork the batter at this point.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter has a smooth, slightly glossy appearance
Step 6
Pour the batter evenly into the prepared cake tin and smooth the top with a spatula. This helps the cake bake evenly and gives a nice flat surface if you plan to decorate. Avoid tapping the tin too hard to prevent air bubbles collapsing.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter sits level in the tin with no large air pockets
Step 7
Place the tin in the center of the preheated oven and bake for about 30 minutes. Avoid opening the oven door in the first 20 minutes to prevent the cake from sinking. The heat activates the baking powder, causing the cake to rise beautifully.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake is golden brown and springs back when lightly pressed
Step 8
Test if the cake is done by inserting a skewer in the center; it should come out clean or with just a few moist crumbs. If it’s wet, bake for a few more minutes but watch closely to avoid drying out.
⏱ Time: 1-2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Clean skewer or only a few crumbs attached
Step 9
Let the cake cool in the tin for 10 minutes, then turn it out onto a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling on a rack prevents the bottom from becoming soggy and helps the cake set perfectly before slicing or decorating.
⏱ Time: 15-20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake feels firm and cool to the touch

One Bowl Classic British Sponge Cake That’s Moist and Easy

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 8 servings

This one bowl British sponge cake is a total lifesaver—super moist with a tender crumb and zero fuss. Just mix it all together in one bowl, no mess! Great for afternoon tea or a quick sweet treat.

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this one bowl British sponge cake recipe during a hectic week when I wanted something sweet but didn’t have time for complicated baking. The idea of mixing everything in one bowl without dirtying multiple dishes was a game changer. Plus, it always turns out beautifully moist with a soft, tender crumb—something I struggled with in past sponge attempts. What makes this version so reliable is the balance of ingredients and the gentle folding technique I learned after a few failed tries. I used to overmix and ended up with a dense cake, but now I just combine everything carefully to keep the batter airy. It’s perfect for afternoon tea or when you need a quick dessert that feels homemade without the fuss. The vanilla adds a warm aroma, and the texture is light but satisfying. Honestly, it’s become a favorite in my kitchen because it’s foolproof and delicious every time.

Why This Works

This one bowl sponge cake works because it balances simplicity with technique, ensuring a moist and airy texture every time. The creaming of butter and sugar is crucial as it incorporates air bubbles that expand during baking, giving the cake its lightness. Using softened butter rather than melted or cold butter allows for this aeration without compromising texture.

The gradual addition of eggs helps emulsify the batter, stabilizing the air pockets and preventing the mixture from curdling. Room temperature eggs blend better, maintaining a smooth batter. Folding in the flour gently avoids developing gluten, which would make the cake tough instead of tender.

Using self-raising flour with baking powder means the leavening is built-in, creating a consistent rise. The milk adds moisture and loosens the batter slightly, contributing to a soft crumb. Baking at a steady 180°C (350°F) ensures the cake cooks evenly without drying out.

Finally, letting the cake cool properly on a wire rack keeps the crumb from becoming soggy and helps it set with the perfect texture. Each step works together to produce a classic British sponge that’s moist, tender, and effortlessly delicious.

  • Caster sugar is finer than regular granulated sugar, which helps it dissolve quickly during creaming. This creates a smoother batter and contributes to the light texture of the sponge. When buying, look for pure white sugar with no clumps. If you don’t have caster sugar, you can pulse regular sugar in a blender to achieve a similar texture.
  • Unsalted butter is preferred so you can control the salt level in your cake. It should be softened to room temperature for easy creaming; cold butter won’t mix well and melted butter changes the texture entirely. Using high-quality butter adds richness and moisture to the cake, improving flavor and crumb.
  • Self-raising flour contains a balanced amount of baking powder, which is essential for the cake’s rise. Using plain flour with added baking powder can work, but the ratio must be precise. Sifting the flour before folding in prevents lumps and helps incorporate air for a lighter sponge.
  • Baking powder acts as a chemical leavening agent, releasing gas bubbles when heated to help the cake rise. It’s important to measure accurately; too much can cause a bitter taste and uneven texture, while too little leads to a dense cake. Fresh baking powder is best for reliable results.
  • Vanilla extract adds warmth and depth to the flavor without overpowering the simplicity of the sponge. Use pure vanilla extract rather than imitation for the best aroma. Adding it after the flour helps preserve its delicate flavor, enhancing the overall taste of the cake.

Ingredients

  • 200g caster sugar, granulated
  • 200g unsalted butter, softened
  • 4 large eggs, room temperature
  • 200g self-raising flour, sifted
  • 1 tsp baking powder
  • 2 tbsp milk, room temperature
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preheating your oven to 180°C (350°F) and greasing an 8-inch round cake tin. This step ensures your cake won’t stick and will bake evenly. Using softened butter is key here because it blends smoothly with sugar, creating a light, fluffy base. If the butter is too cold, it won’t mix well and you might get lumps.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Greased tin should feel lightly coated but not greasy to the touch
  2. Step 2

    In a large mixing bowl, cream the softened butter and caster sugar together using a hand mixer or wooden spoon. This process traps air and helps the cake rise. Cream until the mixture is pale and fluffy, which usually takes about 3-5 minutes. Rushing this step can make your cake dense.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Mixture looks lighter in color and has a soft, airy texture
  3. Step 3

    Crack in the eggs one at a time, beating well between each addition. This gradual incorporation prevents the batter from curdling. If the eggs are cold, the mixture might split, so room temperature eggs are important. If curdling happens, add a spoonful of flour to bring it back together.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Batter looks smooth and glossy with no separation
  4. Step 4

    Gently fold in the sifted self-raising flour and baking powder using a spatula. Folding rather than stirring vigorously keeps the batter light and airy, which is essential for a soft sponge. Overmixing here can develop gluten and make the cake tough.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Flour is just incorporated with no visible streaks
  5. Step 5

    Add the vanilla extract and milk, folding gently to combine. The milk adds moisture and helps loosen the batter slightly, contributing to the tender crumb. Don’t overwork the batter at this point.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Batter has a smooth, slightly glossy appearance
  6. Step 6

    Pour the batter evenly into the prepared cake tin and smooth the top with a spatula. This helps the cake bake evenly and gives a nice flat surface if you plan to decorate. Avoid tapping the tin too hard to prevent air bubbles collapsing.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Batter sits level in the tin with no large air pockets
  7. Step 7

    Place the tin in the center of the preheated oven and bake for about 30 minutes. Avoid opening the oven door in the first 20 minutes to prevent the cake from sinking. The heat activates the baking powder, causing the cake to rise beautifully.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Cake is golden brown and springs back when lightly pressed
  8. Step 8

    Test if the cake is done by inserting a skewer in the center; it should come out clean or with just a few moist crumbs. If it’s wet, bake for a few more minutes but watch closely to avoid drying out.

    ⏱ 1-2 minutes✓ Clean skewer or only a few crumbs attached
  9. Step 9

    Let the cake cool in the tin for 10 minutes, then turn it out onto a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling on a rack prevents the bottom from becoming soggy and helps the cake set perfectly before slicing or decorating.

    ⏱ 15-20 minutes✓ Cake feels firm and cool to the touch

Pro Tips

  • Always use room temperature ingredients for better mixing. — Room temperature butter and eggs blend more smoothly, creating a stable batter that traps air well. Cold ingredients can cause the mixture to curdle or result in uneven texture, while melted butter changes the crumb structure, leading to a denser cake.
  • Sift your flour and baking powder together before adding. — Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking powder, preventing clumps. This ensures the leavening agent works uniformly, giving your sponge an even rise and a light, tender crumb.
  • Fold the flour gently into the batter to avoid overmixing. — Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which toughens the cake. Folding carefully keeps the batter light and airy, preserving the delicate texture typical of a classic sponge.
  • Don’t open the oven door during the first 20 minutes of baking. — Opening the door too early causes temperature fluctuations that can make the cake sink. Keeping the oven closed allows the leavening agents to work properly and the cake to rise evenly.
  • Use a timer and test doneness with a skewer after 30 minutes. — Baking times can vary slightly, so testing ensures your cake is perfectly cooked without drying out. A clean skewer means the cake is done, while wet batter indicates it needs more time.
  • Cool the cake in the tin briefly before transferring to a wire rack. — Cooling in the tin prevents the cake from breaking apart when hot, while transferring it to a wire rack prevents condensation buildup that can make the bottom soggy.
  • Use unsalted butter to control salt levels in your cake. — Salt enhances flavor but too much can overpower the sponge. Using unsalted butter lets you adjust salt precisely, keeping the flavor balanced and the crumb tender.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overmixing the batter after adding flour
    Why: Many people think mixing thoroughly is good, but overmixing develops gluten, resulting in a tough, dense cake instead of a soft sponge.
    Fix: Fold the flour in gently with a spatula just until no flour streaks remain. Stop mixing as soon as the flour is incorporated to keep the batter light and airy.
  • Using cold eggs or butter
    Why: It’s easy to forget to bring ingredients to room temperature, but cold butter and eggs don’t blend well and can cause curdling or lumps.
    Fix: Plan ahead to let eggs and butter sit out for about 30 minutes before baking. If you’re in a rush, gently warm eggs in warm water and soften butter in short bursts in the microwave, but avoid melting.
  • Opening the oven door early
    Why: Impatience or curiosity makes bakers peek too soon, causing temperature drops that can make the cake collapse.
    Fix: Resist the urge to open the door until at least 20 minutes have passed. Use the oven light to check progress instead.
  • Not greasing the cake tin properly
    Why: Skipping greasing or lining can cause the cake to stick and break when removed.
    Fix: Grease the tin thoroughly with butter or baking spray and dust with flour, or line with parchment paper for easy release.
  • Using expired baking powder
    Why: Old baking powder loses its potency, resulting in poor rise and dense texture.
    Fix: Check the expiration date and test by mixing a little in warm water to see if it bubbles. Replace if inactive.

Variations

  • Lemon Zest Sponge: Add 1 tbsp finely grated lemon zest to the batter for a fresh citrus twist. It brightens the flavor and pairs perfectly with a dusting of powdered sugar or a lemon glaze. The zest adds subtle aroma without changing the texture.
    Fold the zest in with the flour to distribute evenly. Bake as usual.
  • Chocolate One Bowl Sponge: Replace 30g of flour with unsweetened cocoa powder for a chocolate version. This adds a rich, deep flavor while keeping the moist texture. Serve with whipped cream or berries for a decadent treat.
    Sift cocoa powder with flour and baking powder to avoid lumps.
  • Berry Swirl Sponge: Fold in 100g fresh or frozen berries gently into the batter before baking. This adds bursts of juicy sweetness and a pretty marbled effect. Great for summer teatime or brunch.
    Toss berries in a little flour before folding to prevent sinking.
  • Almond Sponge: Add 50g ground almonds to the dry ingredients for a nutty flavor and slightly denser texture. It makes the cake more moist and adds a lovely aroma. Perfect with a dusting of icing sugar or almond slivers on top.
    Reduce flour by 50g to compensate for the almonds.
  • Gluten-Free Sponge: Use a gluten-free self-raising flour blend instead of regular flour. This keeps the recipe accessible for gluten-sensitive eaters. The texture will be slightly different but still moist and tender.
    Ensure your baking powder is gluten-free and sift the flour well.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: The cake keeps well covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Use a cake dome or airtight container to prevent it from drying out. Avoid leaving it uncovered, as sponge cakes tend to harden quickly.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The cake may firm up and lose some moisture when chilled, so it’s best served at room temperature. Bring it out 30 minutes before serving or warm gently if desired.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze the cooled cake wrapped tightly in plastic wrap and aluminum foil for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature. Avoid freezing cream or fresh fruit toppings, add those fresh after thawing.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm slices gently in the microwave for 10-15 seconds or in a low oven (150°C) for 5-7 minutes to refresh softness. Adding a small splash of milk before reheating can help restore moisture. Avoid overheating as it dries out the cake.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can bake the cake 1-2 days ahead and store it wrapped at room temperature or in the fridge. Prepare any frosting or toppings separately and add just before serving to keep everything fresh and vibrant.

FAQ

Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
Yes, but reduce or omit any added salt in the recipe if using salted butter. Unsalted butter gives you better control over the flavor balance, but salted butter won’t ruin the cake—just adjust accordingly.
Why did my cake sink in the middle?
Sinking often happens if the oven temperature is too low, or if you opened the oven door too early. Also, underbaking or too much leavening can cause this. Make sure to bake at the correct temperature and avoid opening the door during the first 20 minutes.
Can I make this cake dairy-free?
You can substitute dairy butter with plant-based margarine and use a dairy-free milk alternative like almond or oat milk. The texture might be slightly different but it will still be moist and tasty.
What if I don’t have self-raising flour?
You can make your own by mixing 200g plain flour with 2 tsp baking powder and a pinch of salt. Sift well before using to ensure even distribution.
Is it okay to use frozen eggs?
Frozen eggs can be used if properly thawed in the fridge overnight and brought to room temperature before mixing. This helps prevent curdling and ensures smooth batter.
How do I know when the cake is fully baked?
Insert a skewer or toothpick in the center; it should come out clean or with a few moist crumbs. The cake should also spring back when gently pressed and have a golden color on top.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, you can double the ingredients but use a larger cake tin or bake in two tins. Adjust baking time accordingly and check doneness with a skewer.
Why is my cake dry despite following the recipe?
Dryness can result from overbaking or using too much flour. Make sure to measure flour correctly by spooning and leveling, and avoid baking longer than needed.

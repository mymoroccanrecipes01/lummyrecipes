Fluffy British Cheddar Biscuits That Melt in Your Mouth Every Time

Lummy Hayes
Published on April 22, 2026
15 min Prep Time
20 min Time
8 Servings
Easy Difficulty

These British cheddar biscuits are buttery, fluffy, and loaded with sharp cheese that melts perfectly. They’re a cozy snack or a delicious side for soups. I always make extra because they disappear fast!

Pro Tips

Keep your butter and utensils cold to maintain flakiness.
Cold butter creates steam pockets as it melts in the oven, which helps the biscuits rise and develop flaky layers. If the butter warms up too much during mixing, it blends too thoroughly with the flour, resulting in a dense, heavy biscuit.
Don’t twist the biscuit cutter when cutting the dough.
Twisting seals the edges of the biscuit dough, preventing it from rising properly. Press straight down and lift the cutter to keep the edges sharp and allow the biscuits to expand evenly while baking.
Use freshly grated cheddar cheese instead of pre-shredded.
Pre-shredded cheese contains anti-caking agents that can affect melting and texture. Freshly grated cheese melts smoothly, ensuring gooey pockets of flavor throughout the biscuit.
Fold the dough gently a couple of times before cutting.
Folding creates layers in the dough that trap steam during baking, which helps produce flaky, tender biscuits. Overworking the dough can develop gluten and make biscuits tough.
Bake biscuits on parchment paper for easy removal and even baking.
Parchment prevents sticking and promotes even heat distribution, leading to uniformly golden biscuits with a nice crust. It also makes cleanup a breeze.
Serve biscuits warm for best flavor and texture.
Biscuits fresh from the oven have a crisp crust and soft, melty interior. They tend to dry out or become crumbly as they cool, so warming them slightly before serving revives their delicious texture.
Use whole milk for richer flavor and moist texture.
Whole milk adds fat and moisture, contributing to a tender crumb and richer taste. Lower-fat milks can make the dough drier and the biscuits less flavorful.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overworking the dough

Why it happens Many cooks think kneading more will make the dough better, but too much handling develops gluten, making the biscuits tough instead of tender.

Fix Mix the ingredients just until they come together, then gently fold the dough a couple of times before cutting. Avoid rolling or kneading excessively.

Using warm butter

Why it happens Butter softens quickly at room temperature, and some cooks don’t realize it needs to be cold for flaky texture.

Fix Keep butter refrigerated until just before use and work quickly. Chill utensils if needed to keep everything cold.

Twisting the biscuit cutter

Why it happens Twisting feels natural to cut through dough, but it seals the edges and prevents proper rising.

Fix Press straight down and lift the cutter without twisting to keep edges sharp and allow biscuits to rise evenly.

Adding too much milk

Why it happens Pouring milk all at once can make dough too wet and sticky, hard to handle and resulting in dense biscuits.

Fix Add milk gradually, stirring gently until the dough just comes together and is slightly sticky.

Using old baking powder

Why it happens Expired baking powder loses its leavening power, causing biscuits not to rise properly.

Fix Check the expiration date on your baking powder and replace it regularly to ensure fluffy biscuits.

Variations

Herb and Garlic Cheddar Biscuits

Add 1 teaspoon of dried mixed herbs and 1 minced garlic clove to the dry ingredients for a fragrant twist. These biscuits pair beautifully with soups or stews, offering an herby, savory flavor boost.

Note: Be sure to mix the herbs and garlic evenly with the flour to avoid clumping. The garlic flavor intensifies slightly during baking.

Spicy Jalapeño Cheddar Biscuits

Fold in 1/4 cup finely chopped jalapeños and reduce the mustard powder slightly. These spicy biscuits add a kick to your snack or meal, perfect for those who like a little heat.

Note: Use fresh jalapeños and remove seeds for milder heat. The moisture from peppers may require slightly less milk.

Whole Wheat Cheddar Biscuits

Substitute 1 cup of all-purpose flour with whole wheat flour for a nuttier flavor and a bit more fiber. Texture will be slightly denser but still tender and flavorful.

Note: Whole wheat flour absorbs more liquid, so you might need to add a tablespoon more milk to achieve the right dough consistency.

Bacon and Cheddar Biscuits

Stir in 1/2 cup cooked, crumbled bacon for a smoky, savory biscuit. This makes a fantastic breakfast or brunch side that’s hearty and flavorful.

Note: Ensure bacon is crispy and well drained to avoid soggy dough. Mix in gently to distribute evenly.

Vegan Cheddar-Style Biscuits

Use vegan butter and a plant-based milk like oat milk, and substitute cheddar with a vegan cheese alternative. These biscuits still deliver buttery, cheesy vibes for dairy-free diets.

Note: Vegan cheese melts differently, so expect a slightly different texture but still tasty and satisfying.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the biscuits covered with a clean kitchen towel or in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 12 hours. They’re best eaten fresh but can be enjoyed the same day without losing much texture.

Refrigerator

Keep biscuits in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. The texture firms up when cold, so warming them slightly in the oven or microwave before serving brings back softness and melty cheese.

Freezer

Wrap biscuits tightly in plastic wrap and place in a freezer bag for up to 2 months. To thaw, leave at room temperature for an hour or warm gently in the oven. Avoid microwaving frozen biscuits directly to prevent toughness.

Reheating

Reheat biscuits in a preheated oven at 350°F (175°C) for about 5-7 minutes until warmed through and crisp on the outside. Adding a small dab of butter before reheating restores moisture and flavor. Avoid microwaving too long as it can make them rubbery.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the dough and cut the biscuits ahead of time, then refrigerate the unbaked biscuits on a baking sheet covered with plastic wrap for up to 24 hours. When ready, bake straight from the fridge, adding a minute or two to baking time.

FAQ

Can I use pre-shredded cheese instead of freshly grated cheddar?
While you can use pre-shredded cheese, I recommend freshly grating your cheddar for the best melt and flavor. Pre-shredded cheese often contains anti-caking agents that can affect texture and how well it melts inside the biscuits.
What happens if I overmix the biscuit dough?
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which makes the biscuits tough and dense instead of light and fluffy. Mix just until the dough comes together and then gently fold a couple of times to create layers.
Can I substitute the mustard powder?
Mustard powder adds a subtle tang that enhances the cheese flavor, but if you don’t have any, you can omit it without ruining the recipe. The biscuits might be a bit less complex in flavor but still delicious.
Is it okay to use low-fat milk instead of whole milk?
Whole milk gives the biscuits richness and moisture that low-fat milk lacks. Using skim or low-fat milk may result in drier, less tender biscuits. If you must, consider adding a tablespoon of melted butter to compensate.
How do I know when the biscuits are done baking?
Look for golden brown tops and bottoms, and biscuits that have puffed up nicely. They should feel firm on top but still soft inside. Avoid underbaking, as this can leave a doughy center.
Can I freeze the dough before baking?
Yes! You can freeze the cut biscuits on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer bag once solid. Bake from frozen, adding a few extra minutes to the baking time.
What’s the best way to store leftover biscuits?
Store leftovers in an airtight container at room temperature for same-day use or in the fridge for up to 3 days. Reheat in the oven for best texture and flavor.
Can I make these biscuits gluten-free?
You’d need to use a gluten-free flour blend suited for baking. Texture and rise may differ, so it might take some experimentation. Adding xanthan gum can help mimic gluten’s elasticity.

What You'll Need

8 biscuits

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon mustard powder
  • 6 tablespoons cold unsalted butter, diced
  • 1 1/2 cups sharp cheddar cheese, grated
  • 3/4 cup whole milk

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 425°F (220°C). In a large bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, salt, and mustard powder. This step ensures your dry ingredients are evenly distributed, which helps the biscuits rise uniformly. Don’t skip sifting if you want a light texture—it aerates the flour and removes any lumps.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are evenly mixed with no clumps or streaks.
Step 2
Add the cold diced butter to the flour mixture. Use a pastry cutter or your fingertips to rub the butter into the flour until it resembles coarse crumbs with pea-sized bits. The cold butter creates pockets of steam during baking, which is key for the biscuits’ flaky layers. Be careful not to overwork the dough here, or the biscuits will turn out tough.
⏱ Time: 7
✓ Doneness: Mixture looks crumbly with visible small butter pieces.
Step 3
Stir in the grated sharp cheddar cheese until evenly combined. The cheese adds a wonderful sharpness and richness that infuses throughout the biscuits. Mixing it in now ensures every bite has cheesy goodness.
⏱ Time: 2
✓ Doneness: Cheese is evenly distributed without clumping.
Step 4
Pour in the whole milk gradually while stirring gently with a fork or spatula. Add just enough milk to bring the dough together into a slightly sticky ball. If you add too much milk, the dough will be too wet, and the biscuits won’t rise properly. Take your time and stop once the dough holds together.
⏱ Time: 3
✓ Doneness: Dough holds together but remains soft and a bit sticky.
Step 5
Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Gently pat it into a rectangle about 3/4 inch thick. Fold the dough over onto itself a couple of times to create layers, then pat it back down. This folding technique helps create flaky layers inside the biscuits. Avoid kneading or overworking the dough here.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Dough is smooth, layered, and roughly 3/4 inch thick.
Step 6
Using a biscuit cutter or a glass, cut out rounds from the dough, pressing straight down without twisting. Twisting can seal the edges and prevent proper rising. Place the biscuits on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper, close but not touching to allow expansion.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Biscuits are neatly cut with clean edges and spaced on the tray.
Step 7
Gather the scraps, gently reshape, and cut more biscuits until all dough is used. This ensures minimal waste and consistent batch size. Remember to handle the dough gently to keep it light and fluffy.
⏱ Time: 3
✓ Doneness: All dough used with minimal handling and smooth edges.
Step 8
Bake the biscuits in the preheated oven for 15-20 minutes until they’re puffed up and golden on top. The high heat helps the biscuits rise quickly and develop a beautiful crust. Avoid opening the oven door too early to prevent collapse.
⏱ Time: 20
✓ Doneness: Biscuits are golden brown and puffed with a light crust.
Step 9
Remove from the oven and let the biscuits cool for 5 minutes on the baking sheet before transferring to a wire rack. This resting time allows the crumb to set so they don’t fall apart when you bite in. Serve warm with butter or alongside your favorite soup.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Biscuits hold their shape and are warm to the touch.

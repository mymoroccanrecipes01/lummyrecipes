Easy and Delicious Honey-Glazed Ham for a Perfect Easter Dinner

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 90 min cook 8 servings

This honey-glazed ham is my go-to for Easter—sweet, juicy, and so simple to make. The glaze caramelizes beautifully, giving it that perfect golden crust. Great for family gatherings or any special occasion!

Introduction

I remember the first time I made a honey-glazed ham for Easter—it was a total game changer. Before that, I’d always been intimidated by ham, thinking it was complicated or required hours of work. But this recipe is so straightforward and rewarding that it quickly became a family favorite. What sets my version apart is the balance of sweet and tangy in the glaze, thanks to the honey mixed with a touch of apple cider vinegar and Dijon mustard. Plus, studding the ham with whole cloves adds a subtle warmth that fills the kitchen with the most comforting aroma. The trick I learned is to baste the ham regularly during baking to get that beautiful, sticky glaze without drying out the meat. When it’s done, the ham is juicy inside with a caramelized, golden crust that’s irresistible. It looks stunning on the table, too, making it a perfect centerpiece for any holiday dinner or special occasion. Honestly, it’s become my go-to for Easter and beyond, and I think you’ll love how easy and delicious it is!

Why This Works

One key to this recipe's success lies in the glaze composition and application. The blend of honey, brown sugar, and Dijon mustard creates a perfect balance of sweetness, acidity, and slight tang that enhances the natural flavors of the ham without overpowering it. The brown sugar caramelizes during baking, contributing to the appealing golden crust through Maillard reactions, which deepen flavor and add complexity.

The slow roasting at a moderate temperature ensures the ham heats evenly and stays juicy. Because it’s a fully cooked ham, the goal is to warm it through gently and let the glaze build layers of flavor without drying the meat. Frequent basting is essential here—it keeps the surface moist and allows the sugars in the glaze to develop a sticky, flavorful coating instead of burning or drying out.

Scoring the ham is more than decorative—it increases the surface area for the glaze to penetrate, allowing the spices and honey to infuse subtle flavor deeper into the meat. Studding with cloves adds aromatic warmth, releasing essential oils as the ham bakes, which enhance the overall sensory experience.

Finally, the resting period after baking is crucial. Allowing the ham to rest redistributes the internal juices, ensuring each slice is tender and moist rather than dry. Altogether, these techniques work harmoniously to produce a visually stunning and deliciously tender honey-glazed ham that’s perfect for holiday celebrations.

  • Choose raw or pure honey with a mild floral flavor to avoid overpowering the ham. Honey not only sweetens but also helps the glaze caramelize into a shiny crust during baking. Avoid overly processed honey, which may lack depth of flavor. If substituting, use maple syrup or agave nectar, though the flavor profile will shift slightly.
  • Packed brown sugar provides moisture and a rich molasses note that deepens the glaze. Light brown sugar is preferred for a milder taste, but dark brown sugar can be used for a more intense flavor. Make sure it’s fresh and clump-free to blend smoothly into the glaze.
  • Dijon adds subtle acidity and spice, balancing the sweetness of honey and sugar. It also acts as an emulsifier, helping to bind the glaze ingredients together. Avoid using yellow mustard, as it’s milder and less complex, which can flatten the glaze’s flavor.
  • Cloves offer a warm, aromatic spice that complements the sweetness of the glaze. When studding the ham, insert them sparingly to avoid bitterness. They release essential oils during baking, enhancing aroma and flavor. Ground cloves in the glaze add subtle depth but use sparingly to prevent overpowering.
  • This vinegar adds brightness and acidity, cutting through the glaze’s sweetness to prevent it from becoming cloying. It helps balance flavors and tenderizes the ham’s surface slightly. Avoid using white vinegar, which is harsher and less nuanced.

Ingredients

  • 1 fully cooked bone-in ham (about 8 pounds)
  • 1 cup honey
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar, packed
  • 1/4 cup Dijon mustard
  • 1/4 cup apple cider vinegar
  • 1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground cloves
  • Whole cloves for studding the ham (optional)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 325°F (165°C). While the oven warms up, remove the ham from its packaging and pat it dry with paper towels. This step is important because excess moisture on the surface can prevent the glaze from sticking properly, and you want a nice caramelized crust. Using a sharp knife, score the ham in a diamond pattern about 1/8 inch deep—this helps the glaze penetrate and creates a lovely decorative effect. If you like, stud the intersections of the cuts with whole cloves for added flavor and aroma. Place the ham on a rack in a roasting pan to allow even heat circulation during cooking.

    ⏱ 15 minutes✓ Ham is scored with diamond pattern and cloves inserted; surface is dry to touch
  2. Step 2

    In a small saucepan, combine honey, brown sugar, Dijon mustard, apple cider vinegar, cinnamon, and ground cloves. Heat the mixture over medium heat, stirring occasionally until the sugar dissolves and the glaze becomes smooth and slightly thickened—this usually takes about 5 minutes. This glaze is the star of the dish, so taking the time to meld the flavors really pays off. Avoid boiling aggressively; you want gentle simmering to prevent burning. Once ready, set aside to cool slightly before glazing the ham.

    ⏱ 7 minutes✓ Glaze is smooth, shiny, and slightly thickened
  3. Step 3

    Place the ham in the preheated oven and bake uncovered for about 1 hour and 15 minutes, basting every 15 minutes with the prepared honey glaze. Use a basting brush or spoon to coat the surface evenly. Basting is crucial—it not only builds up layers of sticky sweetness but also keeps the ham moist and prevents it from drying out. Watch for the glaze to turn a deep golden brown and become glossy. If the glaze starts to darken too quickly or burn, reduce the oven temperature slightly.

    ⏱ 75 minutes✓ Glaze is caramelized and ham surface is golden brown
  4. Step 4

    About 10 minutes before the ham is done, increase the oven temperature to 400°F (205°C) to give the glaze a final caramelized finish. Keep a close eye during this step to avoid burning. This quick blast of high heat intensifies the glaze’s flavor and creates a slightly crisp crust that contrasts beautifully with the tender meat inside. Once done, remove the ham from the oven and tent it loosely with foil. Let it rest for 15 minutes before slicing. Resting allows the juices to redistribute, making each slice juicy and tender.

    ⏱ 10 minutes plus 15 minutes resting✓ Glaze is deeply caramelized and ham is resting off heat
  5. Step 5

    Carefully transfer the ham to a serving platter and slice against the grain for maximum tenderness. Use a sharp carving knife to get clean slices. Arrange the slices attractively and drizzle any pan juices or extra glaze over the top for extra flavor. Serving warm is best, but leftovers reheat nicely. Pair it with your favorite sides like roasted vegetables or a fresh spring salad to complete your Easter feast.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Slices are moist, tender, and evenly cut
  6. Step 6

    If you want to prepare ahead, you can bake the ham fully, then cool it completely before wrapping tightly in foil and refrigerating. When ready to serve, reheat gently at 275°F (135°C) for about 20-30 minutes until warmed through, basting occasionally with leftover glaze to refresh the flavor and moisture. This makes entertaining much easier and keeps your ham tasting fresh.

    ⏱ 30 minutes reheating✓ Ham warmed through and glaze glossy again
  7. Step 7

    For a smoky twist, add a few drops of liquid smoke to the glaze mixture before heating. This gives the ham a subtle barbecue flavor without needing a smoker. It’s a fun way to vary the recipe and impress guests who love bold tastes. Just be cautious—not too much, as liquid smoke can overpower easily.

    ⏱ N/A✓ Glaze with smoky aroma balanced with honey sweetness
  8. Step 8

    If you prefer a thicker glaze, after baking brush on an extra layer of glaze and broil the ham for 2-3 minutes. Watch carefully to avoid burning. This step adds a sticky, lacquered finish that’s visually stunning and deliciously rich in flavor.

    ⏱ 3-5 minutes✓ Glaze is bubbling and slightly charred in spots
  9. Step 9

    To serve leftovers, slice the ham thin and use it in sandwiches or salads. The sweet glaze pairs wonderfully with mustard or creamy dressings. It’s a great way to enjoy the flavors of your Easter meal in new ways throughout the week.

    ⏱ N/A✓ Ham slices moist and flavorful in cold or warmed dishes

Pro Tips

  • Score the ham in a diamond pattern before glazing. — Scoring increases the surface area for the glaze to cling to and allows flavors to penetrate deeper. It also helps the heat distribute evenly, ensuring the glaze caramelizes beautifully without burning.
  • Baste the ham every 15 minutes during baking. — Regular basting keeps the ham moist and helps build layers of sticky glaze. Skipping this step can result in a dry ham or a glaze that burns instead of caramelizing evenly.
  • Let the ham rest for at least 15 minutes before slicing. — Resting allows the juices to redistribute throughout the meat, making the slices juicier and more tender. Cutting immediately after baking causes the juices to spill out, drying the meat.
  • Use a roasting rack in the pan. — Raising the ham off the pan bottom lets heat circulate evenly around the meat. This prevents the ham from steaming in its juices and helps the glaze crisp up all over.
  • Don’t rush the glaze preparation. — Gently heating the glaze ingredients dissolves the sugar and melds flavors without burning. Boiling aggressively can cause bitterness or crystallization, ruining the texture and taste.
  • Increase oven temperature at the end for a caramelized finish. — A short blast of higher heat crisps and deepens the glaze’s color and flavor. Avoid leaving it too long to prevent burning.
  • Use leftover glaze to refresh the ham when reheating. — Reapplying glaze adds moisture and flavor to the reheated ham, preventing it from drying out and keeping it tasting fresh.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not scoring the ham before glazing
    Why: Many cooks skip scoring to save time or because they think it’s purely decorative.
    Fix: Take a few extra minutes to score the ham in a diamond pattern. This allows the glaze to penetrate better and creates a more attractive presentation, enhancing both flavor and appearance.
  • Skipping basting during baking
    Why: Some assume the glaze will stick on its own or forget to baste regularly.
    Fix: Set a timer to baste every 15 minutes. This keeps the ham moist and builds up a thick, sticky glaze instead of a dry or burnt surface.
  • Using too high oven temperature from the start
    Why: Rushing the cooking process to save time is common, but high heat can dry out the ham.
    Fix: Bake at a moderate 325°F (165°C) to warm the ham evenly without drying. Finish with a higher temperature only briefly to caramelize the glaze.
  • Cutting the ham immediately after baking
    Why: Impatience or serving urgency often leads to slicing straight from the oven.
    Fix: Allow the ham to rest loosely covered for at least 15 minutes. This step ensures the juices redistribute, resulting in moist, tender slices.
  • Burning the glaze by leaving it unattended
    Why: Glaze sugars can burn quickly if not monitored, especially near the end of baking.
    Fix: Keep an eye on the ham during the final caramelization step and reduce oven heat if the glaze darkens too fast. Frequent basting also helps prevent burning.

Variations

  • Maple-Glazed Ham: Swap the honey for pure maple syrup for a deeper, woodsy sweetness. Add a splash of orange juice to brighten the glaze. This variation offers a richer, more complex flavor profile with a hint of citrus.
    Maple syrup is thinner than honey, so reduce apple cider vinegar slightly to keep glaze consistency. Watch glaze closely to avoid burning due to higher sugar content.
  • Spicy Mustard Glaze: Add a teaspoon of hot mustard or a pinch of cayenne pepper to the glaze for a subtle kick. This gives the ham a spicy edge that balances the sweetness nicely, perfect for those who like a bit of heat.
    Adjust spice levels gradually to avoid overpowering the ham. Baste carefully as spicy glazes can darken faster.
  • Pineapple and Brown Sugar Glaze: Incorporate pineapple juice and crushed pineapple into the glaze for a tropical twist. The acidity tenderizes the meat while adding fruity brightness, making this perfect for summer gatherings.
    Reduce sugar slightly to compensate for pineapple’s natural sweetness. Strain crushed pineapple if preferred to avoid texture in glaze.
  • Herb-Infused Glaze: Stir fresh chopped rosemary and thyme into the glaze for an aromatic herbal note. This variation pairs wonderfully with roasted vegetables and adds fresh complexity to the dish.
    Add herbs near the end of glaze preparation to preserve their flavor. Remove before serving if desired for a cleaner presentation.
  • Brown Sugar and Mustard Dry Rub: Instead of a glaze, rub the ham with a mixture of brown sugar, mustard powder, and spices before roasting. This creates a crusty, flavorful exterior with less sticky sweetness.
    Apply the rub evenly and let the ham rest before baking to let the flavors penetrate. Baste with a simple honey syrup for moisture if desired.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: The ham can be safely kept at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered loosely with foil or a clean cloth. Beyond that, bacteria growth increases, so it’s best to refrigerate leftovers promptly.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftover ham in an airtight container or tightly wrapped with plastic wrap for up to 4 days. The flavor and texture may firm up when cold, but reheating gently restores juiciness. Avoid drying by reheating with a splash of water or glaze.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze sliced ham in airtight freezer bags or containers for up to 3 months. Wrap tightly to prevent freezer burn. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat gently to retain moisture and flavor.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat ham slices in a covered baking dish at 275°F (135°C) for 20-30 minutes, basting occasionally with leftover glaze or water to keep moist. Avoid microwave reheating for large portions as it can dry out the meat.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can bake the ham fully a day ahead, then cool and refrigerate. On serving day, reheat gently with glaze basting. Prepare the glaze ahead to save time. This strategy reduces last-minute stress and keeps flavors fresh.

FAQ

Can I use a pre-cooked spiral ham for this recipe?
Yes! Pre-cooked spiral hams work wonderfully with this glaze. Since they’re already cooked, you’ll mainly be reheating and glazing. Reduce baking time to about 10-15 minutes per pound and baste frequently to avoid drying out the thinner slices.
What if I don’t have Dijon mustard?
You can substitute with yellow mustard or whole grain mustard, but Dijon offers a smoother, more balanced flavor. If using yellow mustard, reduce the quantity slightly to avoid overpowering the glaze’s sweetness.
How do I prevent the glaze from burning?
Keep the oven temperature moderate at 325°F and baste regularly to keep the surface moist. If the glaze starts to darken too quickly, tent the ham with foil or lower the oven temperature slightly.
Can I make the glaze ahead of time?
Absolutely! Prepare the glaze a day ahead and store it in the refrigerator. Warm gently before using to restore its pourable consistency. This saves time on the day you bake the ham.
Is it necessary to stud the ham with cloves?
No, it’s optional. Cloves add aromatic warmth and a festive look, but if you don’t like strong spice notes, you can skip this step without affecting the overall success of the recipe.
How long should I let the ham rest after baking?
Rest the ham for at least 15 minutes loosely tented with foil. This step helps the juices redistribute, making the meat juicier and easier to slice.
Can I use this glaze on other meats?
Yes, the honey-mustard glaze works well with pork loin, roasted chicken, or even turkey. Adjust cooking times accordingly based on the meat’s size and type.
What sides go best with honey-glazed ham?
Classic sides like roasted vegetables, scalloped potatoes, green beans, or a fresh spring salad complement the sweet and savory flavors perfectly. The glaze’s sweetness pairs nicely with both earthy and tangy accompaniments.

Classic Italian Easter Cookies with a Hint of Anise Flavor

Lummy Hayes Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 20 min cook 24 servings

These traditional Italian Easter cookies are simple yet full of flavor, featuring a subtle anise twist. Perfect for spring celebrations, they’re easy to make and share with loved ones.

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this Italian Easter cookie recipe during a family trip to Sicily a few years ago. It was a chilly spring afternoon, and my Nonna handed me a plate filled with these golden, anise-scented treats. I was hooked immediately. What makes these cookies stand out is the beautiful balance of simple ingredients with that subtle anise flavor, which gives them a unique, aromatic twist. Unlike some overly sweet or heavy cookies, these are light, crisp around the edges, and tender inside. The trick I learned from my Nonna is to lightly crush the anise seeds before mixing them in; it releases their flavor without overpowering the dough. Also, resting the dough for a bit before baking helps the cookies hold their shape and develop a perfect texture. Whether you’re celebrating Easter or just craving something sweet and traditional, this recipe is a wonderful way to bring a little Italian charm into your kitchen. Plus, they’re easy enough for a beginner but impressive enough to share with friends and family. You'll love how these cookies look and taste fresh out of the oven, dusted with a little powdered sugar. Give them a try and bring a little springtime joy to your table!

Why This Works

These Italian Easter cookies owe their success to a few key techniques and ingredient interactions. First, the creaming of butter and sugar is essential. This process traps air, which helps create a light texture and tender crumb. Without properly creaming, the cookies risk becoming dense and heavy.

The addition of eggs one at a time also plays a critical role. Eggs act as both a binding agent and a source of moisture, but if added too quickly, they can cause the mixture to curdle, negatively impacting texture. Mixing them slowly ensures a smooth, emulsified dough.

The crushed anise seeds are more than just a flavoring; they release aromatic oils that infuse the dough with a subtle licorice note characteristic of traditional Italian pastries. Crushing the seeds just before mixing maximizes this effect. Also, chilling the dough before baking is crucial to prevent excessive spreading, which preserves the cookies’ shape and texture.

Finally, baking just until the edges are golden while keeping the centers soft ensures a perfect balance of crispness and tenderness. This recipe balances these elements beautifully, resulting in cookies that are both flavorful and texturally pleasing.

  • Choose a good quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for this recipe. It provides the right balance of protein to develop enough gluten for structure without making the cookies tough. Avoid bread flour as it has too much protein, resulting in chewier cookies, and cake flour which is too soft.
  • Using unsalted butter allows you to control the salt level precisely. The butter should be softened to room temperature for proper creaming. Cold butter won’t incorporate air well, and melted butter will result in a greasy dough that spreads too much.
  • Look for whole anise seeds in the spice aisle or an Italian market. Crushing them just before use releases their essential oils, giving the cookies a fresh, aromatic flavor. Ground anise powder can be used but may be stronger and less fresh-tasting.
  • A fresh, active baking powder is important to give these cookies a slight lift and lightness. Old or expired baking powder can lead to flat, dense cookies. Measure carefully as too much can cause an off taste.
  • Use large eggs at room temperature to ensure they combine smoothly into the dough. Cold eggs can cause the butter mixture to seize or curdle, affecting dough texture.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 3 large eggs
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 tablespoon anise seeds, lightly crushed
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • Powdered sugar for dusting

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C) and lining a baking sheet with parchment paper. This ensures your cookies bake evenly and don’t stick. While the oven warms up, lightly crush the anise seeds using a mortar and pestle or the back of a spoon—this step is crucial because it releases their aromatic oils without making the flavor too intense. If you skip this, the seeds might stay whole and not infuse the dough properly.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Anise seeds should look cracked but not ground to powder
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, cream together the softened butter and granulated sugar until the mixture is pale and fluffy. This usually takes about 3-5 minutes using a hand or stand mixer on medium speed. Creaming incorporates air, which helps the cookies rise slightly and gives them a tender crumb. Rushing this step or mixing too little will result in denser cookies.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Butter and sugar mixture looks light, creamy, and smooth
  3. Step 3

    Beat in the eggs one at a time, mixing well after each addition. Add the vanilla extract here as well. Eggs bind the dough and provide moisture; adding them slowly ensures they incorporate evenly without curdling the butter mixture. If you add eggs too quickly, the batter may separate, affecting texture.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Mixture is smooth, glossy, and well combined
  4. Step 4

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. This step ensures the baking powder is evenly distributed, which is important for uniform rising. Sifting also removes lumps that could create uneven texture in the cookies.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are well mixed and airy
  5. Step 5

    Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, mixing on low speed or folding gently with a spatula. Be careful not to overmix; once the flour is just incorporated, stop. Overmixing develops gluten, which can make cookies tough instead of tender.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dough comes together without streaks of flour but is not overly sticky
  6. Step 6

    Fold in the crushed anise seeds evenly throughout the dough. This ensures every bite has that lovely anise aroma. If you add them too late or unevenly, some cookies may lack flavor.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Anise seeds are evenly distributed in the dough
  7. Step 7

    Cover the dough with plastic wrap and chill it in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes. Chilling firms up the dough, which helps the cookies keep their shape while baking and prevents spreading. Skipping this step may cause flat cookies.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Dough feels firm and cool to the touch
  8. Step 8

    Using a teaspoon or small cookie scoop, drop rounded balls of dough onto the prepared baking sheet, spacing them about 2 inches apart. Slightly flatten each ball with the palm of your hand or the bottom of a glass. This helps them bake evenly and develop a tender crumb with crispy edges.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cookies are evenly spaced and flattened to about 1/2 inch thick
  9. Step 9

    Bake the cookies in the preheated oven for 18-20 minutes, or until the edges turn golden brown and the centers are set but still soft. Keep an eye on them starting at 18 minutes to avoid overbaking, which can dry them out. Once done, let cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. Dust with powdered sugar once completely cool for a pretty, sweet finish.

    ⏱ 20 minutes✓ Edges are golden and centers feel firm but not hard

Pro Tips

  • Lightly crush anise seeds just before mixing. — Crushing releases the essential oils that carry the anise flavor. If you add whole seeds, the flavor won’t infuse the dough as well, resulting in bland cookies.
  • Chill the dough for at least 30 minutes before baking. — Cold dough spreads less in the oven, helping the cookies keep their shape and develop a nice texture. Skipping chilling often leads to flat, greasy cookies.
  • Use room temperature eggs for smoother mixing. — Cold eggs can cause the butter to seize or curdle, which affects the dough’s consistency and final cookie texture.
  • Don’t overmix once the flour is added. — Overmixing develops gluten, making cookies tough. Mix just until combined for tender results.
  • Line your baking sheet with parchment paper. — This prevents sticking and promotes even baking, making cleanup easier and protecting the cookie bottoms.
  • Flatten dough balls slightly before baking. — This encourages even baking and helps create the perfect crisp edges with a tender center.
  • Watch baking time closely starting at 18 minutes. — Overbaking dries out cookies. Look for golden edges and set centers to know they’re done.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not crushing the anise seeds
    Why: Many assume whole seeds will flavor the dough sufficiently, but the essential oils remain locked inside the seeds.
    Fix: Use a mortar and pestle or the back of a spoon to gently crush the seeds just before mixing to release their aroma and flavor.
  • Skipping the chilling step
    Why: Impatience or underestimating the importance of chilling can lead to spreading cookies.
    Fix: Always chill dough for at least 30 minutes to firm it up and prevent excessive spreading.
  • Overmixing after adding flour
    Why: It’s easy to overwork the dough while trying to fully incorporate flour.
    Fix: Mix only until no flour streaks remain to keep cookies tender.
  • Using melted butter instead of softened
    Why: Some cooks melt butter for ease, but it changes dough texture.
    Fix: Use butter softened to room temperature for proper creaming and air incorporation.
  • Baking too long and drying out cookies
    Why: Not monitoring closely can lead to overbaking.
    Fix: Remove cookies when edges are golden and centers still look slightly soft; they firm up while cooling.

Variations

  • Lemon Zest Italian Easter Cookies: Add 1 tablespoon of finely grated lemon zest to the dough for a fresh, citrus twist. This brightens the flavor and pairs beautifully with the anise. Expect a slightly more fragrant cookie with a hint of tanginess.
    Zest should be added with the eggs to evenly distribute the citrus oils.
  • Almond Italian Easter Cookies: Replace 1/2 cup of flour with finely ground almonds for a nutty flavor and tender crumb. This variation adds richness and a subtle crunch that complements the anise.
    Almond meal absorbs moisture differently, so the dough may be slightly softer; chill well before baking.
  • Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies: Use a 1:1 gluten-free flour blend instead of all-purpose flour. The cookies will be just as tasty but with a slightly different texture—more crumbly but still delicious.
    Add 1/4 teaspoon xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t include it for better structure.
  • Honey-Sweetened Italian Easter Cookies: Swap half the granulated sugar for honey to add moisture and a subtle floral sweetness. This makes the cookies a bit softer and adds complexity to the flavor.
    Reduce other liquids slightly to compensate for honey’s moisture content.
  • Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies: After baking and cooling, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate. This adds a rich contrast to the anise flavor and makes the cookies extra special for celebrations.
    Allow chocolate to set completely on parchment paper before storing.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain crispness. Covering them well prevents them from drying out.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Cookies can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 7 days. They may soften slightly but will remain tasty. Bring to room temperature before serving for best texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Proper wrapping prevents freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving.
  • ♨️ Reheating: If you want to refresh the crispness, warm cookies in a 300°F (150°C) oven for 5 minutes. Avoid microwaving as it can make them chewy. A quick oven warm-up revives texture without drying them out.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the dough 1-2 days ahead and keep it tightly wrapped in the refrigerator. Shape and bake the cookies on the day you plan to serve them for freshest results.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise seeds with another spice?
Yes! Fennel seeds are a great substitute as they have a similar licorice flavor but milder. You can also try a small amount of star anise powder, but use sparingly since it’s more potent. Avoid overpowering the dough.
Why did my cookies spread too much in the oven?
This usually happens if the dough wasn’t chilled or if the butter was too warm or melted. Chilling firms the dough, preventing excessive spreading. Also, make sure your oven temperature is accurate; too low can cause spreading too.
Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
You can, but reduce or omit any additional salt in the recipe to avoid over-salting. Unsalted butter offers better control over seasoning and flavor balance in baked goods.
How do I store these cookies to keep them fresh?
Store in an airtight container at room temperature away from heat or moisture. For longer storage, freeze them properly wrapped. Avoid refrigeration if you want to maintain crisp edges as they may soften.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
To make them vegan, substitute eggs with flax or chia egg replacements, and use a plant-based butter alternative. The texture will be slightly different, but the flavor can still shine with anise.
Why are my cookies tough instead of tender?
Overmixing the dough after adding flour develops gluten, which results in toughness. Mix just until combined and avoid pressing or kneading the dough too much.
Is it necessary to sift the flour?
Sifting helps remove lumps and aerate the flour for a lighter dough. While not mandatory, it improves texture and ensures even mixing, especially when combined with baking powder.
Can I make the dough ahead and freeze it?
Yes, you can freeze shaped dough balls on a baking sheet, then transfer to a bag once solid. Bake from frozen, adding a couple extra minutes to baking time. This is great for planning ahead.

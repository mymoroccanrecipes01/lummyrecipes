Soft Italian Easter Cookies with a Hint of Anise for Springtime Joy

Lummy Hayes Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 20 min cook 24 servings

These Italian Easter cookies are soft, lightly sweet, and have a lovely anise flavor that makes them special. They bake up golden and pillowy, perfect for spring brunches or family gatherings. I always find they bring a little sunshine to the table!

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this Italian Easter cookie recipe at a family gathering years ago, and it quickly became a tradition I look forward to every spring. What I love about this version is how simple the dough comes together—no complicated steps or fancy ingredients, just a few pantry staples plus that magical hint of anise. It’s that subtle licorice flavor that really sets these cookies apart from your everyday sugar cookie and gives them a nostalgic, almost festive charm. I remember the first time I baked them myself, I was worried they might turn out dry or too dense, but the trick I learned was to use softened butter and not overmix the dough. That keeps them soft and tender, almost like little golden pillows. The baking time is short, so you’ll smell that wonderful anise aroma filling your kitchen in no time. When done, they have a delicate crumb and just the right amount of sweetness, making them perfect for sharing at holiday brunches or family get-togethers. Honestly, these cookies always bring a little sunshine to the table and a smile to everyone’s face.

Why This Works

The success of these Italian Easter cookies lies in the balance between tender texture and subtle flavor, achieved through careful technique and ingredient choices. The creaming of butter and sugar is essential because it traps tiny air bubbles, which expand during baking and create a light, soft crumb. Without this step, the cookies would be dense and heavy.

Another key factor is the gentle folding of dry ingredients into the wet mixture. Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which leads to toughness—something we definitely want to avoid for a delicate cookie. Folding preserves the fluffiness from the creaming stage and ensures a tender bite.

Anise extract plays a starring role here, giving the cookies a distinct, slightly sweet licorice note that’s traditional in Italian baking around Easter. It’s potent, so the measured amount is perfect for flavor without overpowering. The chemistry of baking powder also helps these cookies rise just enough, creating a soft, pillow-like texture, while the pinch of salt enhances all the flavors, rounding out the sweetness.

Finally, the baking temperature and timing are calibrated to gently cook the dough through without drying it out. The cookies emerge golden on the edges and soft in the middle, which is exactly the comforting texture that makes them so beloved. Cooling on the baking sheet helps them set without crumbling, preserving their tender structure.

  • Look for fresh, unbleached all-purpose flour for the best flavor and texture. Unbleached flour has a slightly nuttier taste and better protein quality, which helps form just enough gluten to hold the cookies together without making them tough. Sifting before use aerates the flour and removes lumps, contributing to a lighter dough.
  • Choose good-quality unsalted butter and ensure it’s softened but not melted. Soft butter creams well with sugar, trapping air that lightens the dough. Salted butter can be used but reduce added salt accordingly. Butter also adds richness and moisture, critical for tender cookies.
  • Use regular granulated sugar to achieve the right sweetness and texture. It dissolves well during creaming, creating tiny air pockets. Avoid substitutes like powdered sugar here, as they affect texture and moisture differently.
  • Large eggs at room temperature emulsify the dough, binding fat and liquid ingredients while adding moisture and structure. Adding eggs one at a time prevents curdling and ensures a smooth batter.
  • This concentrated flavoring gives the cookies their signature licorice note. Pure anise extract is preferred over artificial flavorings for a more authentic taste. Use sparingly as it’s quite strong; too much can overpower the cookie’s delicate sweetness.
  • A reliable, fresh baking powder is crucial for proper leavening. It reacts with moisture and heat to produce carbon dioxide, causing the dough to rise and become tender. Old or expired baking powder can result in flat, dense cookies.
  • Salt balances sweetness and enhances overall flavor complexity. Even a small amount is important to prevent the cookie from tasting flat.
  • Used as a finishing touch, powdered sugar adds a delicate sweetness and pretty appearance. It doesn’t affect the cookie texture since it’s added after baking.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 3 large eggs
  • 2 teaspoons baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • Powdered sugar for dusting (optional)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line two baking sheets with parchment paper. This step is crucial because the right temperature ensures the cookies bake evenly and develop that lovely golden color without burning. Parchment paper prevents sticking and helps the bottoms brown nicely. Avoid using a baking spray directly on the sheets, as it can cause uneven baking.

    ⏱ 10✓ Oven preheated and baking sheets prepared without wrinkles or tears
  2. Step 2

    In a large mixing bowl, cream together the softened butter and granulated sugar until light and fluffy. This usually takes about 3-5 minutes with a hand mixer or stand mixer. Creaming incorporates air into the mixture, which helps the cookies rise and gives them a tender texture. If you skip this step or rush it, your cookies might turn out dense or greasy.

    ⏱ 5✓ Mixture looks pale, fluffy, and smooth
  3. Step 3

    Beat in the eggs one at a time, mixing well after each addition. This helps emulsify the batter, ensuring the fat and water components blend smoothly. Adding eggs slowly prevents curdling and keeps the dough uniform. If you add them all at once, your dough might separate or become lumpy.

    ⏱ 3✓ Smooth, glossy batter with no visible egg streaks
  4. Step 4

    Stir in the anise extract and salt, mixing just until combined. The anise extract is the star flavor here, so don’t skip or reduce it too much. Salt balances the sweetness and enhances the flavor complexity. Be careful not to overmix at this stage to keep the dough tender.

    ⏱ 2✓ Evenly distributed flavor with no visible patches of extract or salt
  5. Step 5

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour and baking powder. Sifting aerates the flour and ensures the baking powder is evenly dispersed, which is key for light, fluffy cookies. If you skip sifting, the cookies might have uneven texture or dense spots.

    ⏱ 3✓ Fluffy, lump-free flour mixture
  6. Step 6

    Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, folding gently with a spatula or wooden spoon. Folding preserves the air bubbles created earlier and prevents overworking the dough, which can make cookies tough. Mix just until no flour streaks remain.

    ⏱ 5✓ Soft dough that holds together without being sticky or dry
  7. Step 7

    Use a teaspoon or small cookie scoop to drop dough onto the prepared baking sheets, spacing them about 2 inches apart. This spacing allows the cookies to spread slightly without merging. If you crowd the pan, they’ll bake unevenly and stick together.

    ⏱ 5✓ Evenly spaced dough mounds about 1 inch in diameter
  8. Step 8

    Bake the cookies for 15 to 20 minutes, rotating the pans halfway through for even browning. Keep an eye on them starting at 15 minutes; you want them lightly golden on the edges but still soft in the center. Overbaking will dry them out, so remove as soon as edges turn golden.

    ⏱ 20✓ Edges are golden, tops look set but soft
  9. Step 9

    Let the cookies cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring them to a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling on the sheet lets them firm up enough to move without breaking. If you transfer immediately, they might crumble. Once cooled, dust with powdered sugar if you like for a pretty finish.

    ⏱ 10✓ Cookies firm enough to lift without breaking, cooled to room temperature

Pro Tips

  • Always soften butter to room temperature before creaming. — Soft butter creams better with sugar, trapping air that helps cookies rise and become tender. Using cold butter will result in a lumpy mixture and dense cookies, while melted butter can make the dough too loose.
  • Don’t overmix the dough after adding flour. — Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which toughens cookies. Folding just until combined keeps the texture soft and delicate, which is essential for these Italian Easter cookies.
  • Use fresh baking powder for best rise. — Baking powder loses potency over time. Using fresh powder ensures the dough rises properly, resulting in light, fluffy cookies instead of flat, dense ones.
  • Sift flour and baking powder together before adding. — Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking powder. This promotes even rising and prevents lumps, leading to a consistent texture throughout the batch.
  • Space cookies well on the baking sheet. — Leaving enough room prevents cookies from spreading into each other, allowing proper heat circulation and even browning on all sides.
  • Rotate baking sheets halfway through baking. — Ovens often have hot spots, so rotating sheets ensures even baking and consistent color and texture across all cookies.
  • Let cookies cool on the sheet before transferring. — Cooling on the hot sheet lets the cookies set and firm up, reducing breakage when moving to a rack. It also prevents them from becoming too dry.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using melted butter instead of softened butter
    Why: Many assume melted butter makes mixing easier, but it changes the dough texture drastically.
    Fix: Use butter softened to room temperature, not melted. Soft butter creams with sugar properly, creating air pockets that lead to a tender cookie. If you only have melted butter, chill it until slightly firm before mixing.
  • Overmixing after adding flour
    Why: In a rush to combine ingredients, it’s easy to mix flour too much.
    Fix: Fold the flour in gently and stop as soon as it’s incorporated. This prevents gluten overdevelopment and keeps cookies soft.
  • Not measuring flour correctly
    Why: Scooping flour directly with a cup can compact it, adding too much.
    Fix: Spoon flour into the measuring cup and level it off with a knife for accurate measurement, ensuring the dough isn’t too dry.
  • Using old baking powder
    Why: Baking powder loses effectiveness over time, often unnoticed.
    Fix: Test baking powder by adding a teaspoon to warm water; if it bubbles vigorously, it’s fresh. Replace if not.
  • Baking at too high temperature
    Why: Some ovens run hot or cooks increase temp to speed baking.
    Fix: Stick to 350°F (175°C) for even baking. Higher temps brown edges too fast while leaving centers undercooked.

Variations

  • Lemon Zest Italian Easter Cookies: Add 1 tablespoon of fresh lemon zest to the dough for a bright citrus twist that pairs beautifully with the anise flavor. These cookies will have a refreshing aroma and a slightly tangy note that feels perfect for spring.
    Add lemon zest at the same time as the anise extract and salt, folding gently to avoid overmixing.
  • Almond Italian Easter Cookies: Mix in 1/2 cup finely ground almonds to the flour mixture for a nutty depth and a slightly chewy texture. You’ll get a richer flavor and a heartier cookie that still stays soft and tender.
    Reduce flour by 1/2 cup to compensate for almonds’ moisture and texture.
  • Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies: Substitute all-purpose flour with a gluten-free blend that includes xanthan gum to maintain structure. These cookies come out just as soft and flavorful, perfect for those avoiding gluten.
    Use a 1:1 gluten-free flour blend and add 1/4 teaspoon xanthan gum if it’s not included.
  • Anise Seed Italian Easter Cookies: Swap anise extract for 1 tablespoon of lightly crushed anise seeds for a more aromatic, textured bite. The seeds add a subtle crunch and a fresher, more herbal anise flavor.
    Toast anise seeds lightly before crushing to enhance flavor before adding to the dough.
  • Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies: Once cooled, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. This adds a rich, bittersweet contrast to the delicate sweetness and anise flavor, making them extra special for celebrations.
    Use high-quality dark chocolate and chill cookies after dipping for a smooth finish.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to maintain their soft texture and fresh flavor.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: You can refrigerate cookies in an airtight container for up to 7 days. They may become slightly firmer when cold but soften quickly at room temperature. Reheat briefly in a warm oven to restore softness if desired.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies for up to 2 months in a sealed container or freezer bag, separated by parchment paper to prevent sticking. Thaw at room temperature for a few hours before serving. Avoid freezing with powdered sugar dusting, add it after thawing.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm cookies in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes to refresh softness. Avoid microwaving as it can make them chewy or dry out quickly. A light sprinkle of water before warming helps retain moisture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the dough up to 24 hours ahead and keep it refrigerated, tightly wrapped. Shape and bake cookies just before serving for the freshest result. Alternatively, bake cookies and freeze them; thaw on the day you want to serve.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract with something else?
Yes, you can use anise seeds or fennel seeds as a substitute. Toast and crush about 1 tablespoon of seeds and add to the dough instead of extract. The flavor will be slightly different—more herbal and textured—but still delicious.
Why are my cookies turning out dry?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or using too much flour. Measure flour carefully by spooning into the cup and leveling off. Also, bake just until edges are golden; overbaking dries them out.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
To make vegan, substitute butter with a plant-based margarine and eggs with flax eggs (1 tablespoon ground flax + 3 tablespoons water per egg). The texture will be slightly different but still tasty.
How should I store these cookies to keep them soft?
Store in an airtight container at room temperature with a slice of bread inside. The bread helps maintain moisture and keeps cookies soft longer.
Can I freeze the cookie dough?
Yes! Wrap the dough tightly in plastic wrap and freeze for up to 1 month. Thaw overnight in the fridge before shaping and baking as usual.
What’s the best way to measure flour for this recipe?
Avoid scooping directly with the measuring cup. Instead, spoon flour into the cup and level with a knife for accuracy. This prevents packing too much flour, which can make cookies dry.
Can I add other flavors to the dough?
Absolutely! Lemon or orange zest, almond extract, or even a touch of cinnamon can complement the anise nicely. Add these flavors alongside the anise extract and adjust amounts to taste.
Why do my cookies spread too much while baking?
Common causes include melted butter or dough that’s too warm. Use softened, not melted butter, and chill dough briefly if your kitchen is hot before baking.

Classic Italian Easter Cookies with a Hint of Anise Flavor

Lummy Hayes Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 24 servings

These tender, lightly sweet Italian Easter cookies are infused with a subtle anise flavor that makes them truly special. Perfect for holiday celebrations or a cozy coffee break, they bring a little tradition to your table.

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this Italian Easter cookie recipe years ago while visiting family in Naples. These cookies instantly became a cherished tradition in my home because they’re so tender and just lightly sweetened, with that unmistakable hint of anise that’s both comforting and a little nostalgic. What sets this recipe apart is the use of fresh lemon zest alongside the anise extract, which brightens the flavor beautifully and keeps the cookies from being too heavy. I’ve tried other versions before — some too dry, others too dense — but this one strikes the perfect balance every time. One trick I learned is to cream the butter and sugar thoroughly; it creates a soft texture that melts in your mouth. When they come out of the oven, they have a delicate golden hue and a subtle aroma that fills the kitchen with warmth. These cookies are ideal for sharing on Easter morning or anytime you want a light, flavorful treat with your coffee. Honestly, they bring back so many happy memories, and I hope they do the same for you!

Why This Works

These Italian Easter cookies succeed because of the careful balance between ingredients and technique. The creaming of butter with sugar is fundamental—it aerates the mixture, creating tiny air pockets that expand during baking and result in a light, tender crumb. Without this step, the cookies would be dense and heavy.

The combination of baking powder with flour provides the necessary leavening, helping the cookies rise just enough to stay soft rather than cakey. Sifting the flour ensures it's light and lump-free, which contributes to an even texture. Overmixing the dough after adding the flour is avoided to prevent gluten development that would make the cookies tough.

The flavor profile hinges on the anise extract and fresh lemon zest. Anise gives a subtle licorice note traditional to Italian Easter treats, but it’s the lemon zest that adds brightness and complexity, balancing sweetness. The exact amount of anise is important—too much can overpower the cookie, while too little loses the signature aroma.

Finally, baking at a moderate temperature allows the edges to lightly brown while keeping the centers soft and tender. Cooling the cookies on the baking sheet before moving them helps them set properly without breaking. These combined factors make this recipe stand out among other cookie recipes.

  • Choose a standard all-purpose flour with about 10-12% protein content. This level is perfect for cookies because it provides enough structure without making them tough. Avoid bread flour, which is too high in protein and will yield chewier cookies. Sifting the flour before mixing helps aerate it and prevents lumps, ensuring a tender final texture.
  • Use unsalted butter softened to room temperature for better control over the salt content and even creaming. Butter contributes to flavor and tenderness, and the fat content helps create that melt-in-your-mouth texture. Avoid melted butter here; creaming solid butter traps air essential for lightness.
  • Large eggs at room temperature mix more evenly into the batter, helping create a smooth, cohesive dough. Eggs provide moisture, structure, and richness. Adding them one at a time ensures proper emulsification, preventing curdling or separation which can affect texture.
  • Anise extract brings the signature licorice flavor traditional in these cookies. Choose a pure extract rather than artificial flavorings for a more authentic taste. Use sparingly, as it’s potent and can easily overpower the delicate balance of flavors.
  • Fresh lemon zest brightens the cookie’s flavor and adds a subtle citrus aroma that complements the anise. Use a microplane to zest only the yellow part of the peel, avoiding the bitter white pith. This ingredient lifts the overall taste, making the cookies taste fresh and lively.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 3 large eggs
  • 2 teaspoons baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • Zest of 1 lemon
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • Powdered sugar for dusting (optional)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line two baking sheets with parchment paper. This step is crucial because a consistent oven temperature ensures even baking, and parchment prevents sticking, which helps the cookies keep their delicate shape. Avoid skipping the lining as it can cause uneven browning or make cleanup tougher.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Oven preheated and baking sheets ready to go
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, cream the softened butter and granulated sugar together using an electric mixer on medium speed. This should take about 3-5 minutes until the mixture is pale and fluffy. Proper creaming incorporates air, which gives the cookies their tender, light texture. If you rush this or don’t cream enough, the cookies might turn out dense or greasy.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Butter and sugar mixture is light in color and fluffy in texture
  3. Step 3

    Beat in the eggs one at a time, making sure each is fully incorporated before adding the next. This gradual addition helps maintain the emulsion of fat and liquid, preventing curdling and ensuring a smooth dough. If you add eggs too quickly, the batter can separate, affecting the final texture.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Smooth, slightly thick batter with no streaks of egg visible
  4. Step 4

    Mix in the anise extract and lemon zest to the wet ingredients. These add the signature flavor to the cookies, with the anise providing a subtle licorice note and the lemon zest brightening the overall taste. Be careful not to overdo the anise; a little goes a long way.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Evenly distributed fragrant mixture
  5. Step 5

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. Sifting aerates the flour and ensures the baking powder is evenly distributed, which helps the cookies rise uniformly. Skipping this step can result in uneven texture or lumpy dough.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Light, lump-free dry mixture
  6. Step 6

    Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, mixing on low speed or folding gently with a spatula. Mixing slowly prevents overworking the gluten in the flour, which keeps the cookies tender rather than tough. Overmixing here is a common mistake that makes cookies chewy instead of soft.

    ⏱ 4 minutes✓ Dough comes together into a soft, slightly sticky ball
  7. Step 7

    Scoop tablespoon-sized portions of dough onto the prepared baking sheets, spacing them about 2 inches apart. This spacing allows the cookies to expand slightly without merging into each other. If they’re too close, you’ll end up with one big cookie instead of delicate individual ones.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Evenly spaced dough mounds on baking sheets
  8. Step 8

    Bake the cookies in the preheated oven for 12-15 minutes, or until the edges are lightly golden but the centers remain soft. Keep an eye on them starting at 12 minutes to avoid overbaking, which can dry them out. The goal is a tender, melt-in-your-mouth texture with just a hint of color.

    ⏱ 15 minutes✓ Light golden edges and a soft center when gently pressed
  9. Step 9

    Let the cookies cool on the baking sheets for 5 minutes before transferring them to a wire rack to cool completely. This resting time lets them firm up slightly without losing their tender crumb. If you transfer them immediately while hot, they might break apart; waiting too long can cause them to become dry.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cookies hold their shape and have a soft, tender texture

Pro Tips

  • Make sure the butter is softened but not melted before creaming. — Softened butter retains its structure, allowing you to trap air as you cream it with sugar. This trapped air is essential for a light, tender cookie crumb. Melted butter would result in a dense, greasy texture and poor rise.
  • Add eggs one at a time and mix well between additions. — This gradual incorporation helps maintain the emulsion of fat and liquid, preventing the batter from curdling. Proper emulsification ensures even texture and prevents separation, which can ruin the dough’s consistency.
  • Avoid overmixing once the dry ingredients are added. — Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which toughens the cookies and ruins their tender texture. Mixing just until combined keeps the dough soft and ensures a delicate crumb.
  • Use fresh lemon zest instead of bottled for better flavor. — Fresh zest contains natural oils that provide vibrant, fresh citrus notes. Bottled zest or dried lemon peel lacks these oils and can taste dull or artificial, affecting the cookie’s flavor profile.
  • Line baking sheets with parchment paper for even baking. — Parchment paper prevents sticking and promotes even heat distribution on the cookie’s bottom, leading to uniform browning and easier cleanup.
  • Let cookies cool on the baking sheet for a few minutes before transferring. — Hot cookies are fragile and can break apart if moved too soon. Allowing them to rest firms them up slightly, making handling easier without sacrificing softness.
  • Use a cookie scoop to portion dough evenly. — Consistent portioning ensures cookies bake evenly and look uniform. Uneven sizes can cause some cookies to burn while others remain underbaked.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using butter that’s too cold or melted
    Why: Many assume butter just needs to be soft, but some use it straight from the fridge or melted, thinking it’ll mix better.
    Fix: Let butter sit at room temperature until soft but still solid. Cream it thoroughly with sugar to trap air. This step is crucial for texture; cold or melted butter won’t create the light crumb you want.
  • Overmixing the dough after adding flour
    Why: In a rush to combine ingredients, it’s tempting to mix vigorously.
    Fix: Mix gently and only until no dry streaks remain. This prevents gluten development that would make cookies tough instead of tender.
  • Adding too much anise extract
    Why: People often think more flavor is better, especially with extracts.
    Fix: Measure precisely and start with less if unsure. Anise is strong and can overpower the cookie. You want a subtle hint, not a licorice bomb.
  • Skipping the parchment paper
    Why: To save time or because of unfamiliarity with its benefits.
    Fix: Always line sheets with parchment or use silicone mats. This prevents sticking and promotes even baking, which is essential for delicate cookies.
  • Not letting cookies cool before moving
    Why: Impatience leads to transferring hot cookies immediately.
    Fix: Let cookies cool on the sheet for 5 minutes to firm up. This reduces breakage and helps maintain their delicate shape.

Variations

  • Anise and Almond Cookies: Add 1/2 cup finely ground almonds to the flour mix for a nutty twist. The almonds add richness and a bit of texture while complementing the anise flavor beautifully.
    Almonds absorb some moisture, so reduce flour slightly by 2 tablespoons to keep dough consistency similar.
  • Glazed Lemon Anise Cookies: Drizzle a simple lemon glaze made from powdered sugar and lemon juice over cooled cookies for extra sweetness and a zesty finish. This adds a glossy look and bright flavor.
    Wait until cookies are completely cool before glazing to prevent melting.
  • Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies: Dip half of each cooled cookie in melted dark chocolate for a decadent contrast to the anise. This adds richness and a bit of elegance, perfect for gifting.
    Use good quality chocolate and chill dipped cookies on parchment to set.
  • Gluten-Free Version: Substitute all-purpose flour with a 1:1 gluten-free baking blend. The texture will be slightly different—less tender but still delicious—and great for gluten-sensitive guests.
    Add an extra egg white to improve structure and moisture retention in gluten-free dough.
  • Spiced Italian Easter Cookies: Add 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon and 1/4 teaspoon ground cloves to the dry ingredients for a warm, spiced variation. This adds complexity and depth, especially nice in cooler months.
    Spices can slightly darken the dough; watch baking time closely to avoid overbaking.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain their texture and flavor. They’re best enjoyed fresh but hold up well for a few days.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: You can refrigerate the cookies in an airtight container for up to 7 days. Refrigeration may slightly firm them up, so bring them to room temperature before serving for optimal softness and flavor.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze the cookies in a sealed container or freezer bag for up to 2 months. Wrap them tightly to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature for a couple of hours before serving to restore their tender texture.
  • ♨️ Reheating: If desired, warm cookies in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes to refresh their softness. Avoid microwaving as it can make them rubbery. Adding a small slice of bread in the container can help maintain moisture during storage.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can mix the dough and shape it into balls 1-2 days ahead. Store them on a baking sheet covered with plastic wrap in the fridge and bake fresh when ready. This keeps the cookies tender and fresh-tasting.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract with anise seeds?
Yes, but you’ll need to grind the seeds finely and infuse them into the dough for a few minutes before baking to release their flavor. The taste will be a bit more robust and less uniform than extract. Use about 1 teaspoon of ground seeds for this recipe.
Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
You can, but reduce or omit any added salt in the recipe to avoid oversalting. Unsalted butter gives you more control over the final saltiness, which is important for delicate cookies like these.
What’s the best way to store these cookies?
Store them in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days or freeze for longer storage. Keep them away from moisture and heat to maintain their texture and flavor.
How do I prevent cookies from spreading too much?
Make sure your butter is properly softened, not melted, and chill the dough briefly if it’s too warm. Also, measuring flour correctly helps maintain dough consistency to prevent excessive spreading.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
You can substitute eggs with flax eggs or applesauce, and use vegan butter alternatives. However, texture and flavor will differ slightly. I recommend experimenting with small batches to find your preferred balance.
Why should I sift the flour?
Sifting aerates the flour and removes lumps, which helps create a lighter, more tender cookie. It also ensures even distribution of baking powder and salt for consistent rise and flavor.
What’s the difference between anise and fennel extract?
Both have licorice-like flavors, but anise is sweeter and more intense, while fennel is milder and more herbal. Anise extract is traditional in this recipe for authentic flavor.
Can I use lemon juice instead of zest?
Lemon juice adds acidity and moisture but lacks the essential oils found in zest that provide bright citrus aroma. It’s better to use zest for flavor, but a small splash of juice can complement if needed.
